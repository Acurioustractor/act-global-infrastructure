# Chapter 3 · Why This Is Not New

> *Every report has already said this. The research is done. What we have run out of is the political will to act on what we already know.*

Chapter 1 told you that the methodology has been on the table for sixty-five thousand years. Chapter 2 walked the four voices that proved it. This chapter answers the question that follows naturally: *if the evidence is so clear, why hasn't anything changed?*

The answer is in the filing cabinets.

## The list

| Year | Inquiry / Report | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **1991** | Royal Commission into Aboriginal Deaths in Custody | 339 recommendations. Most are not implemented. |
| **2017** | Royal Commission into the Protection and Detention of Children in the Northern Territory (Don Dale) | The conditions described still exist. |
| **2018, 2019, 2020, 2021, 2022, 2023, 2024** | Productivity Commission, Report on Government Services, Youth Justice | Same conclusions, every year. |
| **2022** | Banksia Hill Inquiry, Western Australia | Findings: not implemented. |
| **2024** | Cleveland Youth Detention Centre coronial inquest, Queensland | Findings: pending implementation. |
| **Every year since the position has existed** | Every state child commissioner | The same recommendations. |
| **Every year** | The Australian Human Rights Commission | Five separate national reports. |
| **2009 onwards** | Closing the Gap | The Indigenous overrepresentation number is in every annual report. It does not move. |
| **Three separate periodic reviews** | UN Committee on the Rights of the Child | Australia rated unfavourably each time. |

This is not a complete list. I stopped because the point is made.

## The arithmetic of the political will gap

Detaining one Australian child for one year costs **one and a third million dollars** at the national average rate. For three years it is **three point nine million dollars**. The detention reoffending rate is **eighty-five percent**.

Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander young people are **twenty-one times more likely** to be detained than their non-Indigenous peers. They make up over **sixty percent of the detained population** despite being a fraction of the youth population.

These numbers are not contested by anyone in the field. They are in the Productivity Commission's annual report. They are in the police data. They are in the Closing the Gap dashboard. They are public.

What is contested is what to do about them. And the answer to *what to do about them* has also been published, repeatedly, by every inquiry on the list above.

The gap between *we know what to do* and *we are doing it* is the political will gap. This book exists because the methodology in Part II is what closes that gap. Not by re-arguing the evidence. By making the evidence operational.

## The thirty-five-year clock

The Royal Commission into Aboriginal Deaths in Custody was published in 1991.

The kids being detained today were not born when the Royal Commission was written.

Their parents may not have been born when the Royal Commission was written.

The recommendations the Royal Commission made about youth justice have been re-made, in different language, by every inquiry that has come after. The 2017 Don Dale Royal Commission's findings restated many of the 1991 recommendations as if they were new. The 2022 Banksia Hill inquiry restated them again. The 2024 Cleveland coronial inquest will restate them again.

This chapter is about that clock. The clock is the part of the story the rest of the country does not yet feel as a clock. Once you feel it, the rest of the book is operationally urgent.

## What we know the next inquiry will find

If a new inquiry were commissioned tomorrow into Australian youth justice, the findings would be:

1. Detention does not reduce reoffending. The published rate is around eighty-five percent.
2. It is one of the most expensive interventions in the entire Australian public budget per outcome.
3. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander young people are dramatically overrepresented.
4. Community-led alternatives — where they have been implemented and evaluated, including Maranguka in Bourke under KPMG audit — produce dramatically better outcomes at dramatically lower cost.
5. The mechanism is identity, kinship, cultural authority, and sustained relationship — not behavioural management.
6. The recommendations of every previous inquiry have not been implemented.
7. The system needs to be radically reformed in line with what every previous inquiry has already said.

This is not a guess. This is what every previous inquiry has found. The next inquiry will find the same thing. The inquiry after that will find the same thing.

**We are not asking for another inquiry. We are asking for the implementation of every inquiry that has already been done.**

## The bridge to Part II

Part II of this book is the methodology. It is what implementation looks like when somebody finally decides to do it. It is not new. It is the operationalisation of what every voice in Chapter 2 has already said and what every inquiry on the list above has already recommended.

The reason we built the legibility infrastructure (Chapter 1) is that the implementation has been blocked not by lack of evidence, but by lack of operational visibility. The Australian Living Map of Alternatives, CivicGraph, and Empathy Ledger together make the implementation visible to the people who hold the funding. The methodology in Part II is what they are funding.

You are about to read seven method chapters. None of them are inventions. All of them are syntheses of what is already known. The work of this book is to give the funder, the magistrate, the journalist, and the Aunty in the back room with the kettle a single coherent place to find them.

- **A direct quote from at least one of the inquiry reports** — recommend the 1991 Royal Commission's youth justice section and the 2017 Don Dale findings
- **A specific number of how much money has been spent on inquiries vs how much has been spent on implementation** — this number exists somewhere; it would be brutal
- **A named minister currently in office who is responsible for the next implementation cycle** — the chapter should put one specific person on the page, by name, by portfolio
- **The "we will not commission another landscape review" line** — this is the chapter's version of the funder challenge from `claim-the-living-map-loop`
- **The closing line bridging to Chapter 4** — the methodology is the implementation. The implementation is what every report has already recommended. Now we walk it through.
