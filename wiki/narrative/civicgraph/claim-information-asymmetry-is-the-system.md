# Claim: Information asymmetry isn't a side-effect of the grant system. It is the system.

**The argument.** Every grant round in Australia runs on the same shape. The funder sees the whole pool: who else is applying, how much money is in play, what was funded last year, what patterns repeat across their portfolio. The applicant sees a form, a deadline, a word limit, and a theory of change that isn't theirs. This isn't incompetence. It isn't secrecy. It is the operational logic: the party with the map asks the party without one to perform legibility before it will consider funding them. $107 billion a year flows through this arrangement. Nobody joined the registries until we did.

The community-sector workforce that spends 40% of its week writing applications isn't bad at its job. It is very good at performing compliance — because the system rewards performance, not work. The art project stops asking for permission and starts publishing the map.

## What we haven't said yet

- That the 21 government databases involved were **never designed** to be joined, which is not a bug, it is procurement. Each contract for each system was issued in isolation. Information fragmentation is itself an outcome of the information flows we are mapping.
- That the grant writing workforce is a **labour market** — a specific 40-50K Australians whose jobs exist because the asymmetry exists. Automating that labour away is not neutral. The campaign should name this honestly without pitting workers against the critique.
- That **philanthropic funders lose too** — most can't easily see overlap with their peers, so portfolios duplicate, money sprays, and communities have to tell the same story six times. This is an invitational angle we haven't used.
- That the same pattern repeats in **procurement** ($74B of federal contracts) — contractors with maps win; contractors without them bid blind. SME share of federal value is 35% while winning 52% of contract numbers.
