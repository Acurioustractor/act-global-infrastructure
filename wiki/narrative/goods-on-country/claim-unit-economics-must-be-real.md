# Claim: Target is hope. Number is fact. We will not pitch beds without one.

**The argument:** Goods on Country has a target delivered cost of $400 against a $560 institutional sale price. That target has been the same for two years. A target is not a number — it is a hope wearing the costume of a number. The discipline is to publish actual delivered cost monthly, even when it is bad, and to attach a decision band to the margin: ≥25% scale, 15–24% workable, 5–14% red flag, <5% stop. We do not raise capital, ship orders, or pitch this as enterprise without that number on the page.

## Stat watch

- **Target $400 vs sale $560** is a *target*, not a verified figure. The whole point of the cost report is that the actual number is unknown until the report runs.
- **Decision band thresholds** (25% / 15% / 5%) are operating principles, not benchmarks against external data — they are an internal commitment, not an industry standard.

## What we haven't said yet

- **Never said as a public funder challenge.** *"Most social enterprises pitching us funding cannot show you a delivered unit cost from the last 60 days. Ours is on the page."*
- Never said in the **investor language** of "unit economics first" — the pitch deck does not lead with this
- Never paired with the **distinction between unit cost and fully-loaded cost** in public — internally we know it, externally we have not used it to disarm the "you're not really an enterprise" challenge
- Never used to refuse a meeting — *"We will not present at QBE / SEFA without the last 50 beds' delivered cost in the deck. If we don't have the number, the meeting moves."*
- Never published as a **monthly habit publicly** — the discipline is internal
- Never connected to the **Goods bed lifetime** vs imported alternative comparison — "$X delivered, lasts 10 years; imported $Y, fails in 6 months → 10-year TCO comparison" is in the doc, never in a public pitch
