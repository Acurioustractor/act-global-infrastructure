# The Third Reality

*Not your story. Not my story. A shared truth we can only discover together.*

---

## The rooms that don't talk to each other

Australian youth justice has more reports, more frameworks, more evaluations, more peak bodies, more lived-experience panels, and more government strategies than at any point in its history.

It also has more children in detention, more recidivism, and more lateral violence in the sector than at any point in recent memory.

Eighty per cent of young people released from detention reoffend. It costs $1.3 million a year to detain one child and produce that result. More than sixty per cent of those children are Indigenous.

I have spent the last three years trying to understand why. Not why children offend — that question has been answered a thousand times. But why the answers never reach the people making the decisions.

The problem is not absence of evidence. The evidence exists. The stories exist. The community-led alternatives exist and they work.

The problem is that these things are held in different rooms — and the rooms don't talk to each other.

Frameworks live in consultancy decks that funders read once and file. Stories live in unfunded community organisations that never get asked for their data. Funding flows live in spreadsheets that nobody reads. And Indigenous knowledge lives where it has always lived — in Country, in Elders, in Tuesday afternoon check-ins that no government department has ever counted as governance.

A judge making a sentencing decision sees a court file. A community worker sees a young person. A funder sees a budget line. A politician sees a headline. An Elder sees a child who belongs to Country.

They are all looking at the same reality. But they are each seeing only their piece of it.

---

## What happens when you force the pieces together

In April, fifty-five judges came to Mparntwe — Alice Springs — for a judicial education program run on Country by Traditional Owners.

Kristy Bloomfield and Tanya Turner run Oonchiumpa, a one hundred per cent Aboriginal-led youth program in Central Australia. Kristy is a Central Arrernte, Eastern Arrernte, and Alyawarra Traditional Owner. Her grandfather was Stolen Generations — taken to the Bungalow in Alice Springs as a child. Tanya left a career at the Supreme Court of Victoria to come home and serve her community.

Between them, they've built a program that diverts ninety-five per cent of young people from the justice system. At 97.6 per cent less cost than detention. With a seventy-two per cent school re-engagement rate. While the national system produces eighty per cent reoffending.

They didn't present a PowerPoint to the judges. They put a photograph in each judge's hands. A real young person. A name. An age. A quote.

Nigel, fourteen, from Double Camp: *"When I'm talking to the judge, I feel like I'm panicking."*

Then they said: decide.

The judges decided.

Then Kristy and Tanya told them what they didn't know. He hasn't been home in three weeks. There's no transport to the nearest program. This is his third breach — not his third offence. The person he trusts most was never mentioned in the court file.

Would you make the same decision now?

That moment — the realisation that you have been deciding without knowing what matters — is what I call the Third Reality.

## Three layers, one truth

The Third Reality is what emerges when verified systemic data intersects with sovereign human narrative. It is the shared truth that neither data nor stories can produce alone.

A spreadsheet of justice statistics will not change a policymaker's mind. They have seen the numbers. A singular tragic story can be dismissed as an anomaly. But when you connect an immutable, consent-governed human story to verified systemic evidence, you create something that cannot be ignored.

We built three platforms to make this possible.

**CivicGraph** is the systemic data layer. It connects 587,000 entities across 1.5 million relationships — government contracts, political donations, charity registrations, justice funding, program coverage — into a single searchable map of the Australian civic landscape. It answers the question: *what does the system actually look like?*

When a judge scans the QR code on the back of Nigel's postcard and finds no diversion program within two hundred kilometres of their court, that gap is visible because CivicGraph maps what exists and what doesn't.

**JusticeHub** is the evidence layer. The Australian Living Map of Alternatives holds 1,775 community-led models — programs that work, documented and verified not by government auditors but by the communities themselves. One hundred and twenty-eight are verified at the top three tiers: Indigenous-led, effective, proven.

It answers the question: *what actually works, and where?*

**Empathy Ledger** is the narrative sovereignty layer. Two hundred and twenty-six storytellers. Five thousand media assets. Consent governed at the database level — not bolted on as policy, built in as architecture. OCAP principles — Ownership, Control, Access, Possession — as infrastructure.

It answers the question: *what does this mean for a real person?*

Any one layer alone is dismissible. A politician can ignore a dataset. A funder can dismiss a story. A judge can overlook an evidence report.

But when Nigel's voice is in their hands, the evidence for the alternative is one scan away, and the data shows the system has no plan for him — that is a shared truth that cannot be unseen.

## How it operationalises

The Third Reality is not a concept we explain to people. It is a thing we make them experience.

The judges' postcards are the clearest example. The front is Empathy Ledger — a photograph and words, consent-governed. The back is JusticeHub — a QR code that opens a search filtered to that judge's region, showing real programs with real evidence. The data behind those programs — who funds them, what they cost compared to detention, where the gaps are — is CivicGraph.

One postcard. Three layers. The Third Reality in someone's hands.

But the postcards are one instance. The pattern works everywhere:

**A grant application** for Oonchiumpa. The application cites a ninety-five per cent diversion rate. JusticeHub provides the verified evidence. CivicGraph shows there are zero comparable programs within five hundred kilometres — proving the need. Empathy Ledger provides consent-governed stories from Xavier, Laquisha, CB showing what those outcomes look like in human terms. The funder doesn't receive a report. They receive a Third Reality assessment.

**A policy paper** on youth detention. CivicGraph shows $1.3 million per child, eighty per cent reoffending, sixty per cent Indigenous, 873 local government areas with no funded alternatives. JusticeHub shows 128 verified programs that work. Empathy Ledger shows what detention does to a sixteen-year-old sent 1,500 kilometres from home, in her own words: *"Court is scary because you don't know whether you're getting out or not."*

**A photograph** of a young person in the Brave Ones portrait series. The image is the argument. The data is the evidence. The story is the sovereignty. Brian Duffy's Bowie register applied to the people the system refuses to see with dignity. Hung life-size in galleries, parliament foyers, electorate offices. You stand in front of one and something shifts. That shift is the Third Reality arriving through art rather than information.

## The young people who taught me this

I did not invent the Third Reality. The young people at Oonchiumpa showed me what it looks like when all three layers converge in one life.

Xavier was told by formal reports that he had limited ability to understand. Oonchiumpa bet on his version. He built a Stretch Bed from recycled plastic — a piece of furniture now used for ceremonies and bush trips by the whole community. Fred Campbell, his case worker, said: *"He just was so proud showing them that he can build it. He's quite capable of building that on his own."*

The data says: one young person diverted from the justice system. The evidence says: Oonchiumpa's approach works. Xavier's story says: this is what happens when someone stays long enough to see who you actually are.

That convergence — the number, the evidence, and the person — is the Third Reality.

Laquisha was sixteen when they sent her to Darwin. Fifteen hundred kilometres from home. Twelve-minute phone calls. No family. When I asked her why young people end up in the system, she said one word.

*"Oppression."*

The data confirms her diagnosis. CivicGraph shows the funding flows. JusticeHub shows the program gaps. But the word — that one word from a sixteen-year-old — is what makes the data undeniable. Without her voice, the data is a report. With her voice, the data is a verdict.

Jackquann is fourteen. He raps about identity and belonging. He was asked what keeps him out of trouble. He said: *"Looking after my family."* Not compliance. Not programs. Family.

Nigel is fourteen. He wants to play AFL. He knows that going to school consistently leads to his dream. He just needs someone to walk the path with him. When he talks to a judge, he panics. He is not a complex case. He is a kid who needs a trusted adult.

These are not exceptional stories. Every community in Australia has them. The difference is that Oonchiumpa documents them with consent, verifies them with community authority, and connects them to the evidence that proves alternatives work.

The Third Reality is not a new idea. It is the oldest idea — that a person is more than a file, that a community knows more than a report, and that the truth lives in the space between what the system counts and what a person experiences.

We just built the infrastructure to make that space visible.

## What this means for how we work

The Third Reality is not a product we sell. It is a discipline that shapes how we spend our time.

Every hour should produce at least one of the three layers. If it doesn't — if it's a meeting about a meeting, a report about a report, a strategy document that produces no data, no evidence, and no sovereign narrative — it's admin. And admin should be automated wherever possible.

Forty per cent of our time goes to making experiences — photography, storytelling, community meals, CONTAINED tours, postcards, portraits. This is the narrative sovereignty layer. Ben behind the camera. Nic in the conversation.

Thirty per cent goes to building platforms — Empathy Ledger, JusticeHub, CivicGraph, the wiki. This is the data and evidence layer. Every feature is R&D-eligible, and the Australian government returns 43.5 per cent of every dollar spent on it through the R&D tax offset.

Twenty per cent goes to community relationships — Oonchiumpa, PICC, Mounty Yarns, The Harvest, Barkly Backbone. This is where the evidence is verified and the stories emerge. You cannot build a Third Reality from a desk.

Ten per cent is admin. The AI handles the receipts, the tagging, the matching, the wiki maintenance, the syncing. A Monday morning Telegram message tells me the system is working. If the number looks right, the week's admin is done.

## The bet

The reason the youth justice system doesn't change is not that people don't care. It's that the people who care are looking at different rooms and drawing different conclusions.

The Third Reality is what emerges when you force those rooms together. When a judge holds a young person's photo and scans a code that shows what programs exist in their region and what they cost compared to detention. When a funder reads a grant application that contains verified outcomes and the voice of the young person those outcomes describe. When a politician sees a map of funding deserts overlaid with detention rates overlaid with community-led alternatives that work at 97.6 per cent less cost.

The bet is simple: when enough people see it, the system changes. Not because they were told to. Because they can no longer unsee it.

Fifty-five judges stood in Mparntwe and held a photograph of a fourteen-year-old who panics when he talks to them. Traditional Owners told them what the court file never will. The alternative exists. It works. It costs almost nothing compared to what we spend now.

The only question is whether the people making the decisions have ever been in a room where they had to feel what those decisions produce.

That room is the Third Reality.

And we are building the door.
