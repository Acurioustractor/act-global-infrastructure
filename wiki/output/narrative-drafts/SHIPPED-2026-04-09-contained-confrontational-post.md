# They looked like children.

I was in Spain last year, inside a Diagrama youth justice centre. The young women showed me their bedrooms.

Teddy bears. Family photos. Embroidered pillows.

These are children in the youth justice system. They looked like children.

I have not been able to write that sentence without stopping every time, because somewhere along the line in Australia we stopped looking at the kids in our cells and seeing children. We started seeing risk profiles, recidivism statistics, "young offenders," and the sort of language that lets you forget you are talking about a twelve-year-old.

Here is what is true and what every report says.

The Royal Commission into Aboriginal Deaths in Custody made 339 recommendations in 1991. Most are not implemented.

The Don Dale Royal Commission delivered its findings in 2017. The conditions it described still exist.

Banksia Hill, Cleveland, every coronial inquest of the last decade has said the same thing: detention does not work, the failure rate is over 80 percent, and the kids who go in come out worse.

We know this. The evidence is settled.

What we have run out of is not evidence. It is the willingness to look at a child and see a child.

I am asking one question in public, on the record. If you are a Premier, a Minister, a senior bureaucrat, a journalist, a funder, or someone who has ever quoted "we need more research" as a reason to wait — I am asking you, by name, when you are next going to walk through a youth detention centre and look at the kids inside, the way I walked through Diagrama and looked at the young women showing me their teddy bears.

If you cannot answer that question, the question of what we should do about youth justice in Australia is not actually a question you have permission to answer. You are guessing.

CONTAINED launches April 20. It is a shipping container with three rooms. The first room is what an Australian youth detention cell feels like. The second room is what Diagrama in Spain looks like — the place where children are still treated like children. The third room is what is already happening in Mount Isa, Mount Druitt, Alice Springs, Palm Island.

You can walk through all three in thirty minutes.

If you want to keep arguing about what works in youth justice in Australia, I am asking you to walk through it first.

Then we can talk.

— Ben
