# Draft v2: CivicGraph — LinkedIn

---

I spent two years scraping government databases from a farmhouse in the Sunshine Coast hinterland.

AusTender. ACNC. The AEC donations register. Every state grant portal I could find. Hansard transcripts going back years. Foundation annual reports. Charity financials.

Not because I wanted to. Because I was trying to help a community organisation in Alice Springs understand why they kept getting passed over for funding while the same five consulting firms won every contract.

Turns out, nobody had ever connected the datasets. Not the government. Not the universities. Not the big four firms who charge $500 an hour to navigate what should be public knowledge.

So we did it ourselves.

587,000 entities. 1.5 million relationships between them. $74 billion in government contracts. 301,000 political donations. 30,000 grants. All linked by ABN.

Some of what we found was predictable. 87.5% of federal procurement value goes to 10 entities. Community orgs get crumbs.

Some of it was less comfortable. 140 organisations donate to both major parties AND hold government contracts. $80 million donated. $4.7 billion received back. That's a 58x return. I don't know what to call that, but it's not a coincidence.

873 local government areas in Australia have high disadvantage and low per-capita funding. Nobody knew that number before because nobody had joined the data up. The communities in those 873 places know it in their bones. They just didn't have the spreadsheet to prove it.

We also started tracking Hansard. What ministers promise in parliament about youth justice, mental health, housing, Indigenous affairs. Then we check whether the money follows. Sometimes it does. Often it doesn't. The gap between a press release and a purchase order is where a lot of trust goes to die.

Here's what bothers me most though.

Every community organisation I know has someone brilliant who spends 40% of their week writing grant applications. Reshaping their language to fit a funder's framework. Reporting against KPIs that don't measure what actually matters. Performing compliance instead of doing the work they started the organisation to do.

They're not applying for money. They're asking permission. And the information asymmetry is what keeps it that way. The funder sees the whole pool. The applicant sees a keyhole.

We built CivicGraph to blow the door open. Not to expose anyone. Just to make the information available to the people it's supposed to serve.

If you run a community org and you've ever wondered who else got funded in your space, what your state government actually spent on the thing they announced, or whether there's a single funded program within 200km of your community that does what you do — this is for you.

If you're a journalist and you've ever tried to trace where public money actually ends up — this is for you.

If you're a funder and you're tired of guessing whether you're duplicating what someone else already funded — honestly, this is for you too.

civicgraph.app

We also built a justice evidence layer called JusticeHub with 1,775 community-led models that actually work, and a storytelling platform called Empathy Ledger where the people in these communities own their own stories. But that's a longer conversation.

The short version is: we got tired of the information landscape being designed to keep community organisations blind. So we built the map.

## Why this version is different

What I killed:
- "Until now." (dramatic reveal)
- "Who's in?" / "Who else is done negotiating?" (LinkedIn engagement bait)
- The four-line "What if" rhetorical series (AI parallelism)
- The clean three-platform reveal with line breaks between each (formulaic)
- "Shifting the power dynamic entirely" (puffery)
- "A shared truth the system can't ignore" (sounds like a TED talk)
- Any sentence that could apply equally to 500 different organisations
- Bolded platform names
- Em dashes used for dramatic emphasis (kept only where natural)

What I kept:
- The real numbers (587K, 140 entities, 58x, 873 deserts)
- The anger about information asymmetry
- The grant-writing-as-permission framing (but as observation, not manifesto)
- The Hansard tracking (unique and provocative)
- CivicGraph, JusticeHub, EL — but mentioned casually at the end, not as a product reveal

What's different:
- Opens with a specific story (farmhouse, Alice Springs org, a real reason)
- Admits uncertainty ("I don't know what to call that")
- The platforms appear at the end almost as an afterthought ("but that's a longer conversation")
- Ends on a plain sentence, not a rallying cry
- No section headers, no bullet lists, no formatting tricks
- Reads like someone wrote it at midnight because they were still annoyed
