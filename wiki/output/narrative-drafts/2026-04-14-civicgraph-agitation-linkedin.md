# Draft: The System Doesn't Want You To See This — LinkedIn

---

**Option A — "The $107 billion question"**

$107 billion in government money flows to the Australian charitable sector every year.

672,000 federal contracts. $312 million in political donations. $8.86 billion in grants. 360,000 charities. 873 local government areas identified as funding deserts.

And there is no single system that connects any of it.

Not one.

The data exists. AusTender. ACNC. AEC disclosures. State grant portals. Foundation reports. Hansard transcripts where ministers promise funding that never arrives.

But it's scattered across dozens of databases that were never designed to talk to each other. Which means: if you're a community organisation trying to understand where the money goes, you're blind. If you're a journalist trying to trace a contract, you're guessing. If you're a funder trying to avoid duplication, you're hoping.

Meanwhile, 140 entities in Australia donate to political parties AND hold government contracts. They donated $80 million and received $4.7 billion in contracts. A 58x return.

87.5% of federal procurement value goes to just 10 entities.

This is not a conspiracy. It's an information architecture problem. The people with power have researchers to navigate the complexity. Community organisations have a spreadsheet and a prayer.

So we built the system that connects it.

587,000 entities. 1.5 million relationships. 29.5 million records. $74 billion in contracts tracked. 301,000 political donations mapped. 30,000 grants indexed. 21 government data sources resolved into a single searchable graph.

It tracks Hansard — what politicians promise in parliament — and cross-references it against what actually gets funded.

It identifies 873 funding deserts — places where disadvantage is high and per-capita funding is low.

It shows which programs work and which don't, verified not by government auditors but by the communities running them.

It's called CivicGraph. And it's live.

This is not a pitch for funding. This is a pitch for people.

If you work in community services, justice reinvestment, grant-making, journalism, policy, or civic tech — and you're tired of the information asymmetry that keeps the system opaque — I want to talk to you.

We are not building a better grant portal. We are building civic intelligence infrastructure. The kind that means a community organisation in Tennant Creek can see exactly the same data that a consultant in Sydney sees.

The kind that means when a minister promises $50 million for youth justice in parliament, you can check whether it arrived.

The kind that means 873 communities that have been invisible to the funding system become visible.

Who's in?

**Option B — "Your grant application is a hostage negotiation"**

Every community organisation in Australia knows what it feels like.

You write a grant application. You frame your work in someone else's language. You report against someone else's KPIs. You reshape your mission to fit a funding round that was designed for a problem the funder defined, not the one your community is living.

You are negotiating for permission to do the work you already know needs doing.

And the information asymmetry is the weapon.

The funder knows which other organisations applied. You don't.
The funder knows how much is in the pool. You sort of do.
The funder knows what they funded last year. You have to FOIA it.
The government knows which contracts went where. You have to search AusTender one keyword at a time.

Meanwhile: $107 billion a year flows from government to the charitable sector. $8.86 billion in grants. 672,000 contracts. And nobody has connected the dots.

Until now.

We built a system that maps every public dollar we can find. 587,000 entities. 1.5 million relationships. Contracts, donations, grants, charities, foundations, government agencies connected into one searchable graph.

We track what politicians say in Hansard and whether the money follows.

We identify 873 local government areas where disadvantage is high and funding is low, the deserts where community orgs work hardest and get the least.

We map which organisations donate to both major parties AND hold government contracts. 140 of them. $80 million donated. $4.7 billion received. A 58x return.

This is about levelling the information playing field.

The reason community organisations are so good at writing grant applications is not that they've been trained to. It's that the system has trained them to perform compliance instead of doing their actual work.

What if community organisations could see the full picture? 

What if they could identify the right funder before writing the application? 

What if they could track whether promised funding arrived? 

What if they could show a funder: "here's the gap in your portfolio, and here's the evidence that our approach works"?

A proposition. Shifting the power dynamic entirely.

The system is called CivicGraph. 

The Justice evidence layer is called JusticeHub. 

The storytelling layer is called Empathy Ledger. 

Together they form what we call the Third Reality. Where systemic data meets sovereign human narrative and produces a shared truth the system can't ignore.

We're not asking for permission anymore. We're building the infrastructure so nobody has to.

Who else is done negotiating?

**Option C — "We scraped every government database we could find"**

Two people in the Sunshine Coast hinterland scraped every government database we could legally access.

AusTender. ACNC. AEC donations. State grant portals. ABN registries. Hansard transcripts. Foundation annual reports. Charity financials. Procurement records.

Then we connected them.

587,000 entities. 1.5 million relationships. 29.5 million records across 254 tables.

We can tell you:

— Who funds youth justice in every state and how much they spend
— Which 873 communities are funding deserts
— Which 140 entities donate to political parties AND hold government contracts ($80M donated, $4.7B received)
— What a minister promised in parliament and whether the money followed
— Which community-led programs work and which don't, verified by communities not consultants
— Where 87.5% of federal procurement value actually goes (10 entities)

We did this because nobody else was going to.

The government has the data but no incentive to connect it. The consulting firms have the access but charge $500/hour for what should be public knowledge. The universities have the researchers but publish in journals nobody reads.

Community organisations — the people this data is supposed to serve — have been locked out of their own information landscape.

So we built the key.

CivicGraph is live. JusticeHub is live. The Australian Living Map of Alternatives holds 1,775 community-led models, 128 verified by the communities themselves.

We're looking for:
— Community organisations tired of flying blind
— Journalists who want to trace public money
— Funders who want to stop duplicating effort
— Policy people who want to know if their programs actually reached anyone
— Civic tech builders who want to build on top of this data
— Anyone who thinks public money should be publicly legible

This is not a startup looking for investors. This is an infrastructure project looking for citizens.
