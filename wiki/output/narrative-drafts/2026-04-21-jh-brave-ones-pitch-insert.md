# The Brave Ones

*A living response to the cost of late intervention.*

*From the cost of late intervention to the courage of early recognition.*

Minderoo has already helped Australia name the national bill for acting too late. The 2024 *Cost of Late Intervention* update found Australia now spends **$22.3 billion** each year responding after children and young people are already in crisis, up from **$15.2 billion** in 2019. **Child protection accounts for $10.2 billion, or 43% of that spend. Youth crime accounts for $3.4 billion, or 14%.**

The Brave Ones turns that number into a living, community-led response.

It does not ask Minderoo to fund “storytelling” as soft garnish on hard policy. It asks Minderoo to help fund the public form that lets communities show what dignity, culture, evidence, and relationship look like while a young person is still reachable.

---

## 1. The number

The national problem is already visible.

Australia’s late-intervention economy is highly organised. Detention, child protection, policing, residential care, and crisis systems all have budgets, metrics, institutional pathways, and political gravity.

The community-led work that keeps a young person connected to culture, family, school, Country, and possibility is far less visible. It is often held together by relationships that no spreadsheet has been built to value.

The Brave Ones begins from that asymmetry.

---

## 2. The method

The Third Reality names the deeper failure.

The evidence, the story, the funding context, and the community authority are usually separated:

- a judge sees a file
- a funder sees a budget
- a worker sees a young person
- an Elder sees a child who belongs to Country

The Third Reality is what happens when those forms of knowledge are held together.

In practice:

- **JusticeHub** is the evidence spine
- **CivicGraph** is the system map
- **Empathy Ledger** is the consent and sovereignty layer

This is how late-intervention data becomes a truthful basis for action rather than another report that gets filed and forgotten.

---

## 3. The living response

The Brave Ones gives this whole argument a body.

It is:

- **a journal**
- **an exchange**
- **a portrait**
- **a book**
- **an exhibition**
- **a public node in the evidence system**

Each part does the same thing: it keeps the young person’s own authorship visible while refusing to hide the people who made that possibility real.

The heroes here are not only the young people stepping into the light. They are also the Aunties, Elders, ACCOs, and remote workers whose daily labour keeps children reachable in the face of institutional pain.

That is the moral centre of the whole pitch.

We are spending billions to respond to children once they are already in crisis, while the people who know how to reach them are asked to keep doing life-saving work with almost no structural support, almost no visibility, and almost no power over the systems that judge their children.

The Brave Ones changes that. It does not extract stories from young people. It gives them tools to hold their own story, share it on their terms, and connect it to the evidence and cultural authority that prove another future is already being built.

---

## Why it fits Minderoo

Minderoo says it works by partnering, advocating, developing and de-risking solutions, and generating evidence. The Brave Ones answers all four.

| Minderoo lever | How The Brave Ones answers it |
|---|---|
| **Partner** | Backs communities already doing the work, with relationship-first support rather than extractive reporting |
| **Advocate** | Creates portraits, books, exhibitions, and judge/funder-facing artefacts that shift what decision-makers feel and understand |
| **Develop** | Tests and strengthens a practical model through journals, documentation, exchanges, and community-held editorial process |
| **Generate evidence** | Links sovereign stories to JusticeHub, CivicGraph, funding context, and outcomes, creating a richer evidence base than reports alone |

---

## Where it sits in the ask

The Brave Ones sits **inside** the Three Circles ask of **$2.9M over three years**.

It is not a separate program and not an additional funding wedge.

It is:

- the daily practice that makes Circle Two tangible
- the public form that carries Circle Three outward
- the human trace layer that keeps Circle One honest

The clean Year 1 entry point remains **$780K**.

---

## The line to carry into the room

Minderoo has named the cost of acting too late. The Brave Ones asks what it would take to act while a young person is still reachable, still creating, still connected to culture, and still surrounded by community-led possibility.

The answer is already being held by communities like Oonchiumpa. The Brave Ones does not invent that work. It recognises it, resources it, protects it, and makes it visible.

This is how we move from the cost of late intervention to the courage of early recognition.
