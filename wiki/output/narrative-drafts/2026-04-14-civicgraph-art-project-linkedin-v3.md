# The Aesthetics of Asymmetry — Post 1 of 6

---

I've been making drawings of where the money goes.

Not graphs. Drawings. The kind Mark Lombardi used to make — those sprawling pencil maps of power networks that the FBI asked the Whitney Museum for copies of after 9/11. Except mine are made from Australian public records instead of Panama banking cables.

587,000 entities. 1.5 million lines between them. Government contracts, charity finances, political donations, Hansard transcripts, grant allocations. All joined by ABN. All public. None of it ever connected before.

The drawings keep telling me things I wasn't looking for.

140 organisations donate to both major parties and hold government contracts. $80 million in, $4.7 billion out. I don't know what to call that yet, but it's a shape that keeps repeating.

873 postcodes where disadvantage is high and per-capita funding is low. When I plot them, the map has holes in it. Mimi Onuoha made an art installation called The Library of Missing Datasets — a filing cabinet full of empty folders labelled with the things society chose not to measure. My map has the same quality. The gaps are the argument.

I track what ministers say in parliament about youth justice funding. Then I check whether the money followed. There's a gap between a press release and a purchase order, and trust goes to die there. That gap is also a shape. I'm learning to draw it.

This started because I was trying to help a community organisation in Alice Springs understand why they kept losing grants to the same consulting firms. I thought the answer was in the data. It was — but the data was in 21 different databases that had never been introduced to each other.

So I introduced them.

Now I'm making work about what happens when clarity arrives. The comfort of finally having receipts. The dread of what the receipts reveal. The question Hito Steyerl keeps asking: who benefits from being seen, and who is endangered by it?

Some of this will be animations. Resolution as an emotional dial — sharp is threatening, blur is relief, blur is also erasure. Ryoji Ikeda makes data physical. I want to make the grant application physical — what it feels like to reshape your mission into someone else's framework, to perform compliance instead of doing the work.

Some of it will be maps. Counter-maps. The kind the Anti-Eviction Mapping Project makes in San Francisco, where the data bubbles contain the voices of displaced residents. My maps will have the voices of community workers in Tennant Creek and Palm Island and Mt Druitt talking about what the system looks like from the other end of the keyhole.

Some of it will be missing. Deliberate absences. What we don't measure. What an ABN can't capture. The care labour and the kinship systems and the Tuesday afternoon check-ins that no government database counts as governance.

I'm calling the project The Aesthetics of Asymmetry and I'll be posting episodes over the coming months. Each one tests a different question about what happens when systems become visible.

Episode 1: The Keyhole — what a community org sees vs what the funder sees
Episode 2: Resolution — when clarity helps, when it harms
Episode 3: The Missing Folder — what we chose not to count
Episode 4: The Promise and the Purchase Order — Hansard vs reality
Episode 5: The Alibi — what happens when you drown the system in its own transparency
Episode 6: CARE — who owns the story when everything can be joined up

If you work in community services, journalism, civic tech, philanthropy, or justice — and you've ever suspected the information landscape was designed to keep you in the dark — some of this might land.

The data platform underneath is called CivicGraph. The justice layer is JusticeHub. The storytelling layer is Empathy Ledger. But this project isn't about the platforms. It's about what the platforms revealed, and what it means that nobody had looked before.

## Campaign notes

**Why this works differently from v1/v2:**
- It's an artist announcing a body of work, not a founder pitching a product
- The precedents (Lombardi, Steyerl, Onuoha, Ikeda, AEMP) give it lineage and credibility in the art/critical theory world
- The 6-episode structure gives people a reason to follow — it's a series, not a one-off
- The platforms are mentioned at the end, casually — "this isn't about the platforms"
- The emotional register shifts throughout — curiosity, discomfort, anger, beauty, absence
- No rallying cry at the end. No "who's in?" Just: some of this might land

**6-month campaign rhythm:**
- Month 1: Post 1 (this one) + Episode 1 animation/visualisation ("The Keyhole")
- Month 2: Episode 2 ("Resolution") + one CivicGraph insight post
- Month 3: Episode 3 ("The Missing Folder") + physical artefact (fold-out or poster)
- Month 4: Episode 4 ("The Promise and the Purchase Order") + Hansard tracking reveal
- Month 5: Episode 5 ("The Alibi") + community voice integration
- Month 6: Episode 6 ("CARE") + Empathy Ledger sovereignty framing + exhibition pitch

**Each month also produces:**
- 1 short LinkedIn post with a single data insight from CivicGraph (no manifesto, just the shape)
- 1 visual (Lombardi-style network drawing, or gap map, or Hansard comparison)
- 1 community voice (audio or quote from a real organisation in a funding desert)

**Audience layering:**
- Art world: the lineage (Lombardi, FA, Steyerl) makes this legible as contemporary art practice
- Community sector: the grant-writing-as-compliance framing makes this personal
- Civic tech: the data scale (587K entities, 29.5M records) makes this technically impressive
- Journalists: the Hansard tracking and donation-to-contract ratios make this story material
- Funders: the gap map and evidence commons make this useful (and slightly uncomfortable)

**What you're actually selling (quietly):**
- CivicGraph subscriptions ($79-$1999/mo) to orgs that want the full data
- JusticeHub as the evidence layer for anyone working in justice
- Empathy Ledger as the storytelling infrastructure for community-controlled narratives
- Consulting engagements from orgs that see the art and want the analysis

The art is the marketing. The marketing is the art. The product is the substrate.
