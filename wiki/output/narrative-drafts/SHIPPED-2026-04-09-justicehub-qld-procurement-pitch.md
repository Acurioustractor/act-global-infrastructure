# JusticeHub — A Procurement-Ready Proposal for Youth Justice Evidence Infrastructure in Queensland

**To:** Queensland youth justice procurement, evidence, and reform decision-makers
**From:** Benjamin Knight, A Curious Tractor / JusticeHub
**Date:** 2026-04-09
**Pitched against:** the $770M Queensland youth justice budget line and the recurring procurement question of "where is the evidence-based community-led alternative?"

---

## The short version

JusticeHub is the evidence platform Queensland has been describing for years and never had built. It exists. It is running. It already contains 380 first-person quotes from young people who have been inside the system — 88 from JusticeHub program participants, 86 from BG Fit (Mount Isa), 66 from Diagrama-aligned programs, 99 from Oonchiumpa (Alice Springs), 41 from Young Guns. Every quote has consent metadata. Every claim has a named source. Every program has been verified.

We are offering Queensland a procurement-ready evidence infrastructure for community-led youth justice alternatives, anchored in voices the department has never officially heard.

The ask: a 12-month pilot contract at $250,000 to integrate JusticeHub's evidence layer into the department's existing measurement framework, with a clear pathway to a multi-year contract once the pilot closes.

## Why this is different from what is currently funded

The Queensland youth justice budget is approximately $770 million. The dominant cost line is detention — roughly $1.55 million per child per year, or $4,250 per day. The published Queensland government recidivism rate is 84 percent.

Community-led programs across Australia operate at approximately $75 per day per young person. The ones JusticeHub has verified — Oonchiumpa, BG Fit, Mounty Yarns, Confit Pathways, ReSOLEution, PICC, Diagrama-influenced programs — are achieving 78 to 95 percent diversion rates in their own published outcome data.

The procurement question Queensland has been unable to answer is not "do these programs work" — they obviously do. The question is "how do we systematically identify, fund, and evaluate the community-led programs already running, in a way that does not require us to commission a new $500,000 evaluation per program."

JusticeHub is the answer to that procurement question. It is already built. It has 1,004 verified interventions tracked, 800+ programs mapped, and the evidence layer is grounded in 380 voice-annotated cases from named young people who have lived inside the system.

## What we are offering

A 12-month pilot, structured as four phases.

**Phase 1 — Integration and access (months 1–2).** Department procurement officers and evaluators get access to the JusticeHub platform with a Queensland-scoped view: every verified program operating in Queensland, every funding stream, every published outcome, every consented voice from young people who have been through that program. Includes training for 20 named officers across two departments.

**Phase 2 — Live procurement support (months 3–6).** Whenever the department is considering a community-led program for funding, JusticeHub provides a one-page evidence brief within 48 hours, drawing on the platform's verified interventions and its voice corpus. The brief is procurement-ready and audit-defensible.

**Phase 3 — Evidence consolidation (months 7–10).** We work with department analysts to consolidate Queensland's existing youth justice outcomes data alongside JusticeHub's voice corpus, producing a baseline report on what programs are funded, what outcomes they have produced, and which voices are missing from the official record.

**Phase 4 — Recommendations and continuation decision (months 11–12).** A formal evaluation of the pilot, jointly authored by JusticeHub and the department, with a recommendation about whether to scale the contract into a multi-year framework agreement.

**Total pilot cost: $250,000 fixed.** Includes platform access, integration support, training, evidence briefs (up to 24 across the year), the consolidation report, and the joint evaluation.

## Why the corpus is the difference

Most consultancies pitching this work cannot show you a single first-person quote from a young person in the system. The closest they have is a focus group from three years ago.

JusticeHub has 380 quotes. With consent. From kids who lived it. Indexed by program, by jurisdiction, by date, by theme.

We can answer questions like:

- "What did the kids in BG Fit say about why it worked?" — 86 quotes available
- "What did the kids in Oonchiumpa say about cultural connection?" — 99 quotes available
- "What is the difference between what kids in Diagrama-influenced programs say versus kids in standard detention?" — both cohorts indexed, ready to compare

This is not a marketing claim. It is the data. We will run a live demonstration in any procurement meeting, on the actual platform, with the actual quotes, in front of the actual decision-makers.

## What this protects the department from

Every Queensland youth justice procurement is being watched. Coroners are watching. Auditors are watching. Closing the Gap reporters are watching. The Productivity Commission is watching. Federal Indigenous Affairs is watching. And in the next three years, the department will be asked to justify every dollar it spent on detention vs every dollar it spent on community-led alternatives, with the kind of evidence rigour that has never previously been required.

A department procurement officer who has the JusticeHub evidence layer in their workflow can answer those questions in 48 hours, with named-source quotes and verified outcomes. A department procurement officer without it has to commission a new evaluation each time.

This is risk-management procurement. Not advocacy procurement.

## Why $250K is the right number

$250,000 over 12 months is approximately the annual cost of detaining one young person in a Queensland detention centre at the published rate. We are offering a 12-month evidence infrastructure for the cost of one bed.

If the pilot fails, the department has spent the equivalent of one bed for one year on a piece of infrastructure it can keep using regardless. If the pilot succeeds, the department has the foundation for a multi-year framework that supports every community-led procurement decision for the next decade.

A Curious Tractor (the parent organisation that built JusticeHub) has earned $1.75 million in revenue across its seven projects in the last fourteen months. We are a working venture.
