# The Brave Ones
### A journal, journey and exchange program for young people in youth justice
*Program design document — Companion to The Three Circles*

*Developed from conversations with Oonchiumpa (Mparntwe/Alice Springs), April 2026*
*Updated following CEO review, 16 April 2026*

---

## The idea in one sentence

A structured journal and travel exchange program where young people in youth justice capture their story — digitally and physically — while earning the opportunity to travel between communities and eventually internationally, building agency, cultural exchange, and a body of work that speaks to decision-makers.

---

## Three layers

### 1. The Journal (daily practice)

Every young person gets two things: an **iPad with Apple Pencil** (locked via Guided Access) and a **physical book**.

**Digital journal (iPad — Approach C: Guided Access + Existing Apps):**
- Apple's built-in Guided Access locks the iPad to one app at a time
- **Procreate** ($14.99 one-time) for drawing, painting, finger-painting, collage
- **Voice Memos** for audio recording (addresses literacy barriers — most kids prefer talking or painting over writing)
- Workers batch-upload content to Empathy Ledger weekly
- No custom app in Year 1 — evaluate whether to build one in Year 2 based on pilot data

**Why not a custom app?** The program is the product, not the app. Guided Access ships in a week, costs nothing, and uses professional tools kids already respond to. The $80K saved goes to more travel exchanges and worker time. If Procreate + Voice Memos prove insufficient, build the custom app in Year 2 when you know exactly what kids want.

**Physical journal:**
- A5 hardcover, personal, tactile
- Family members, workers, elders can write in it
- Stickers, drawings, cut-outs, photos printed from the iPad
- Stays with the young person — it's theirs

**The goal state:** a young person walks into Oonchiumpa (or any basecamp) and says *"Can I grab my journal?"* — then sits for two hours and works in it because they want to, not because they're told to.

**What Oonchiumpa already knows works:**
- The girls will sit and paint for two hours unprompted
- Hudson sat on a computer for two hours yesterday — they engage with technology
- Anything tactile works: painting, cutting, finger-painting
- Audio recording + transcription is better than asking kids to write
- The physical object matters — it's personal, it's theirs
- A closed system where they can ONLY do the journal — "they would dig that"

**Consent under Guided Access:**
- Workers use Empathy Ledger's existing consent envelope workflow when uploading
- Each piece of content tagged with consent status (share / don't share / share anonymously)
- Guardian consent for minors captured on paper intake form, recorded in EL
- Verbal consent recorded via Voice Memo before each upload session
- Aboriginal data sovereignty protocols (community verification, elder sign-off for cultural content) handled through each community's existing cultural authority structure

---

### 2. The Rewards + Travel Exchange (earned journeys)

The journal feeds a reward system that unlocks travel. Not punitive — designed to build agency and give young people something concrete to work toward.

**Domestic exchange (Year 1–2):**

| From | To | Duration | Group size |
|------|----|----------|-----------|
| Alice Springs | Mount Isa | 3 days | 4 kids + 2 workers |
| Mount Isa | Alice Springs | 3 days | 4 kids + 2 workers |
| Alice Springs | Sydney | 3–5 days | 4 kids + 2 workers |
| Sydney | Alice Springs | 3–5 days | 4 kids + 2 workers |
| Any basecamp | Any basecamp | 3 days | 4 kids + 2 workers |

**International exchange (Year 2–3, contingent on pilot results and funding):**

| Destination            | Organisation                                        | What they learn                                                                                             |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Spain (Valencia)       | Diagrama Foundation                                 | Youth detention centres run on dignity, not punishment. Staff as educators, not guards. 85% non-recidivism. |
| Amsterdam              | YOPE (Youth Organisation for Political Empowerment) | Young people leading policy advocacy. What it looks like when youth justice listens.                        |
| Portugal / Scandinavia | TBC                                                 | Alternative models, restorative justice at scale                                                            |

**Practical realities from the conversation:**
- Group size: 3–4 young people + 2 workers per trip (2:1 kid-to-worker ratio)
- Need to manage the mix — can't take certain combinations of kids together. Workers know which combinations work.
- Court conditions matter: ankle bracelet timelines, visa eligibility (criminal record dependent)
- The reward is tied to real milestones: *"You get your bracelet off on this date — that's when you can go"*
- Most kids are in programs for 2–6 months — long enough to build toward a trip
- Travel was the thing that got Minderoo excited previously — cultural exchange and knowledge exchange between young people
- These kids have never been on an aeroplane — that alone is transformative

**Each community sets its own reward structure** within a common frame:
- Journals (daily practice)
- Rewards (community-defined milestones)
- Trips (earned travel)
- Sharing (presenting your story to others)

### 3. The Output Layer (art, exhibitions, advocacy)

The journals and exchanges produce a body of work. That work becomes:

**Art exhibitions:**
- Curated from journal entries (with consent via Empathy Ledger)
- Shown in galleries, community spaces, parliaments
- Young people present their own work — they lead
- Brings the public into the story — not a report, an experience

**The Brave Ones book series:**
- Physical books compiled from journal entries, audio transcripts, artwork
- One per community, one national collection
- Goes to politicians, decision-makers, funders, magistrates
- Not ghost-written — assembled from the young person's own material

**JusticeHub integration:**
- Journal data (with consent) links to JusticeHub's evidence base
- Each story sits alongside the program data, funding flows, and Hansard records
- The narrative supports the data — ground-up storytelling meets top-down transparency
- Art exhibitions link to real community programs on the platform
- Empathy Ledger manages all consent and data sovereignty

## Pilot: Oonchiumpa, June 2026

Oonchiumpa is the Year 1 pilot site. This proceeds on existing ACT/Oonchiumpa budget, independent of Minderoo confirmation — making it a proof point, not a promise.

**Why Oonchiumpa first:**
- Deepest relationship (founding basecamp)
- Already doing similar work (research project starting June)
- Brisbane inspiration trip (June 8-13) is the first domestic exchange
- Workers already know which kids engage with technology
- Kristy, Tanya, and Fred have the cultural authority and case knowledge

**Pilot timeline:**

```
2026-06-01  iPads + Guided Access + physical journals set up
2026-06-08  Brisbane inspiration trip = first Brave Ones domestic exchange
2026-08-01  First EL data from Oonchiumpa journals
2026-09-01  Pilot review: what's working, what's not
2026-12-01  First inter-community exchange (Alice Springs <> Mount Isa or Townsville)
```

**Pilot validation criteria:**
- At least 3 kids voluntarily journal in the first month
- At least 1 kid asks for their journal unprompted (the "goal state")
- Worker successfully uploads to EL at least once per week
- Physical journals show use (not blank after 4 weeks)
- Brisbane trip produces at least 1 shareable exchange moment
- Kids prefer drawing/audio over typing (validates Approach C)
- Guided Access stays locked (kids don't bypass it)

## How Empathy Ledger supports data sovereignty

Every piece of content flows through Empathy Ledger v2:

- **Consent envelope:** the young person (and guardian where required) controls what is shared, with whom, and for how long
- **Story ownership:** the young person owns their story — they can revoke access at any time
- **Historical linkage:** journal entries connect to the broader historical and data context on JusticeHub — not to surveil, but to show the system that surrounds each young person
- **Audit trail:** full provenance on every piece of content — who captured it, when, under what consent conditions
- **Cultural protocols:** Indigenous data sovereignty principles built into the consent framework

## Alumni pathway

What happens after a young person finishes their 2-6 months in the program:

- **Their journal stays theirs** — physical book goes home, digital content in EL under their control
- **Graduates become peer mentors** for the next cohort (stipended, Year 2-3)
- **Invited back for exchanges** — alumni can join future trips as peer leaders
- **National network** — over 3 years, a network of young people across 7 communities who went through Brave Ones and now advocate from experience
- **Ongoing EL access** — their story remains theirs, accessible and controllable

The alumni pathway is what makes the program self-sustaining beyond the funding cycle. The young people who went through it become the voices that carry it forward.

## Evaluation framework

Brave Ones evaluation is wired into Oonchiumpa's existing ANU True Justice research partnership:

**Baseline data (captured at intake):**
- Current engagement (school, employment, community)
- Justice system status (charges, conditions, history)
- Self-reported wellbeing (culturally appropriate measure, developed with Oonchiumpa)

**Follow-up windows:**
- 3 months (mid-program)
- 6 months (program end or near-end)
- 12 months (post-program)

**Success measures (beyond artifacts):**
- Journal engagement frequency (EL upload data)
- Self-initiated journaling (worker observation)
- School/training re-engagement rates
- Re-offending rates (where available, with appropriate caveats)
- Exchange participation and peer connections maintained
- Alumni return rate (graduates who re-engage as mentors)

**Ethics:** Through Oonchiumpa's existing ANU True Justice partnership. Not a new ethics application — an extension of existing approved research.

**What "success" looks like:**
- Artifacts (books, exhibitions) prove the model is producing output
- Engagement data proves young people are choosing to participate
- Outcome data (12-month follow-up) proves the model affects trajectories
- Alumni data proves the model is self-sustaining

## What makes this different

**It's not a program bolted onto young people.** It's a practice they choose to do because it's tactile, personal, and leads somewhere real.

**It's not extractive research.** The young person owns every piece of their journal. Empathy Ledger enforces that technically, not just in policy.

**It's not a one-off trip.** It's a structured exchange network between communities who already do the work — extended to international models that prove youth justice can be done differently.

**It's not a book written about young people.** It's a book made BY young people — from their drawings, their recordings, their words — assembled with them, not for them.

**The output goes to the people who make decisions.** Not a report that sits on a shelf. A physical object — a book, an exhibition, a poster — that a magistrate, a minister, or a funder has to hold in their hands and look at.

**The cost comparison:** One iPad costs $1,500. One night in youth detention costs $3,644 (ROGS 2024-25). For the price of one week in detention, you equip a community with journals for a year.
