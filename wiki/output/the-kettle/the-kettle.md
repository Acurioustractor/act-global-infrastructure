# The Kettle

*Benjamin Knight — 9 April 2026*

---

The kettle is always on.

I need you to understand that before anything else.

Not as a metaphor.

Not as a symbol of something larger.

The kettle at Oonchiumpa in Mparntwe is an actual kettle, in an actual room, and it is on right now, tonight, while you are reading this.

There are kids in that room. There are Aunties who have been in that room for longer than most government programs have existed, and they know (without a spreadsheet, without a methodology document, without a funded evaluation) which kid needs tea and which kid needs silence and which kid needs someone to just be in the room with them without asking anything at all.

I have spent years trying to explain that room to people who fund things.

I have not yet found the right words. But I am going to try again here, because something has shifted, and the shift feels important enough to say plainly.

Three years ago I flew to Spain and walked into a youth detention centre in Murcia run by an organisation called Fundación Diagrama. I had been told it was different.

I did not believe it would be that different.

There were curtains.

There were teddy bears on beds.

There were family photographs on walls and embroidered pillows and young women who looked, when I walked in, like the children they were…not like people being processed by a system that had already decided what they were worth.

The staff had been there for fifteen years. Twenty years. More.

The reoffending rate is thirteen per cent.

Australia's is above eighty.

I stood in that room in Murcia and I felt something I can only describe as grief, not for Spain, which had found something, but for here, which had not, or had and keeps choosing not to look at it, which is a different and worse kind of not having.

I came home carrying that room.

Ian Curtis wrote about the gap between what we know and what we do with what we know. He wrote about it the only honest way, which is to say he wrote about it as a physical sensation. The body's knowledge that something is wrong before the mind has agreed to admit it. That is what it feels like to work in this system.

You know.

You have always known.

The knowledge sits somewhere in the chest and doesn't move.

Nick Cave writes about the dead. About the people who stay with you after they are gone, who are more present sometimes than the living. I think about that when I think about the children who have passed through youth detention in this country, not the ones who reoffended, not the statistics, but the ones who sat on a couch somewhere with a kettle on and felt, for one afternoon, like a person someone was glad to see. That feeling doesn't leave them. And its absence doesn't either.

What we have built.

Slowly, over three years, with two people, with earned revenue and no blueprint is the infrastructure that makes the kettle legible.

That sounds small. It is not small.

CivicGraph holds a hundred thousand entities and nearly two hundred thousand relationships between them, every community organisation, every funding body, every government program in the Australian youth justice ecosystem, wired together so you can see, for the first time, what connects to what and what connects to nothing.

The Australian Living Map of Alternatives holds 1,775 community-led models, of which 128 are verified.

Indigenous-led, effective, proven, sitting right now in a database that a magistrate could look at before a sentencing decision if anyone built the bridge.

Empathy Ledger is different.

Empathy Ledger is the part that keeps me up at night in the good way. Three hundred and forty-two storytellers. Every quote traceable back to its source. Every storyteller able to revoke. The design principle underneath it is the one that everything else in this project rests on. That the story belongs to the person who lived it, not to the organisation that collected it, not to the funder that paid for the collection, not to the report that cited it and then filed it.

JusticeHub is the door people can walk through to reach all of it.

These four things share a database.

They talk to each other by foreign key.

When a young person in Mparntwe speaks, that speech can move with consent, with traceability, with the storyteller's hand still on it into every layer of the infrastructure.

From the couch to the courtroom.

From the kettle to the capital.

Nobody else in Australia has this and we have been talking about it like a hobby.

STAY is what comes next.

Three years.

Ten communities.

A person, a storyteller that goes where the communities are and stays there.

Doesn't extract and fly home, doesn't parachute in with a program, doesn't ask the community to reshape itself around a visiting methodology.

Stays.

The word is the whole argument.

Stay where you are.

Don't move children off Country to be punished.

Stay connected.

Don't show up and then disappear when the funding cycle ends.

Stay in the work.

Don't produce the artefact and call it done.

At the end of three years: a library.

Seven method books.

Ten community volumes.

Fifty hand-stitched journals, made by young people, owned by young people.

One national edition, sent to every electorate office in this country, so that the person making decisions about children in a place they have never been has, on their desk, the testimony of the people closest to those children.

The library is not a metaphor either.

The library is what you get when you trust the room.

Elinor Ostrom spent forty years proving empirically what Aboriginal peoples have known for sixty-five thousand years. That the people closest to a problem, when they can see each other's solutions, govern better than any centralised system ever has. She won the Nobel Prize for it in 2009. The evidence is complete. It has been complete for a long time.

What was always missing was the layer that makes it visible to the people with the money and the power and the filing cabinets full of recommendations.

That layer is what we built.

The cost of detaining one Australian child for three years is $3.9 million.

The cost of STAY — ten communities, three years, the full library, young people from European youth justice systems flying in to see what we have built here is less than that.

One child. Three years. One cell.

Or ten communities. Three years. A library.

That is the choice.

It is not a complicated choice.

It is only a choice that requires someone to believe that the room with the kettle is real, that the Aunties' knowledge counts as governance, that the story belongs to the person who lived it.

We believe that.

We have built the proof.

The kettle is on. Come and see.
