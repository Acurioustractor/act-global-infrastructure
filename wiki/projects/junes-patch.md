# June's Patch

> A network of therapeutic gardens providing nature prescriptions for Queensland healthcare workers — responding to a mental health crisis in the caring professions through land, community, and evidence-based social prescribing.

## Overview

June's Patch addresses a hidden crisis: the mental health of the people who care for everyone else. Healthcare workers — nurses, doctors, paramedics, allied health staff — face some of the highest rates of burnout, compassion fatigue, and psychological injury of any workforce. June's Patch responds not with clinical programs or EAP services but with something more fundamental: soil, plants, community, and time.

The project creates a network of therapeutic gardens in Queensland, specifically designed as accessible nature prescription sites for healthcare workers. Each garden is a place of refuge, restoration, and reconnection — with nature, with peers, and with a sense of purpose beyond the clinical environment.

The name honours the tradition of kitchen and patch gardens as places of care and nourishment — practical, grounded, and rooted in relationship.

## The Problem

Healthcare worker burnout and mental health crisis is not new, but its scale has intensified. Structural understaffing, pandemic aftermath, moral injury from systemic failures, and the emotional labour of holding space for suffering day after day — these compound into chronic psychological stress that conventional EAP or clinical responses struggle to address.

Isolation is a particular driver. Healthcare workers often feel they cannot show vulnerability to colleagues or employers. June's Patch creates spaces where showing up, getting hands dirty, and being in community is the intervention — no disclosure required.

## Model

### Therapeutic Gardens
Nature-based environments designed according to evidence from therapeutic landscape research — sensory richness, productive growing, safe enclosure, and community scale.

### Nature Prescriptions for Healthcare Workers
Partnerships with healthcare organisations enable formal referral pathways — a GP or manager can write a "nature prescription" directing a worker to June's Patch, normalising help-seeking and embedding the program within existing health infrastructure.

### Evidence-Based Research
The project builds an evidence base for therapeutic horticulture as a mental health intervention for healthcare workers — contributing to the growing literature on social prescribing and informing future program design.

### Phased Community Ownership
In line with ACT's [[designing-for-obsolescence|Designing for Obsolescence]] philosophy, June's Patch is structured for phased community ownership of individual garden sites. Healthcare worker communities become stewards of their own spaces, reducing dependence on ACT and deepening the ownership that makes therapeutic environments work.

## LCAA Phase

**Listen + Action** — The project begins with deep listening to the experience of healthcare workers: what is actually happening, what does care look and feel like, what do people need beyond what the clinical system can offer? That listening shapes action: building the gardens, establishing the prescribing pathways, growing the community of stewards.
