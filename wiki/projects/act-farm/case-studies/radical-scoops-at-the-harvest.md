# Radical Scoops at The Harvest — the RAA Industry Residency

> A 2,000-square-metre former nursery in Witta, a 12-month lease, about a thousand milk crates, an elder with a saw blade and a story about red cedar, an oyster farmer from up the coast, and ice cream as the connective tissue. Radical Scoops is ACT's first large-scale industry residency — the case study for what an arts-and-industry fellowship looks like when you run it the way we'd build it ourselves.

## What happened

Radical Scoops was selected as the second project in Regional Arts Australia's **Industry Residencies Pilot Program**, delivered with ANAT and the agriculture sector. ACT took a 12-month lease on the former Green Harvest nursery in Witta — roughly 2,000 square metres of fenced ground, glasshouses, standing infrastructure and room to make things — and began treating it as a studio, a convening space, and a research site for one question:

*What happens when arts and industry stop being separate conversations at the community level?*

The residency uses **food as connective tissue** — specifically ice cream, because ice cream is cross-generational, cross-class, cross-cultural, and because Witta sits in the middle of a region with deep dairy and agricultural history. Producers, processors, hospitality workers, designers, educators and neighbours converge at Radical Scoops events rather than being scheduled onto a consultation calendar.

## What we did

- **We treated every element as an artwork.** Structures, objects, experiences. The pop-up deli, the milk-crate pavilion, the oyster pop-ups, the paths — these are not sets that hold art, they *are* the art. This dissolves the consultation-vs-artwork boundary that normally keeps communities outside the creative act.
- **We built on circular economy as a design constraint, not a slogan.** The pavilion planned from about 1,000 milk crates is designed to be **shredded and repurposed into beds for remote communities** at end of life. Oyster-pop-up shells become paths on site. Timber comes from the region. Non-permanence is specified up-front, which changes what you're willing to build.
- **We ran Stage One as pop-ups, not an opening.** The first two weekends of March were the Stage One openings — a pop-up deli and general store using regional timber, oyster pop-ups with a First Nations oyster farmer, garden space exploring timber and milk. The residency earns audience before it asks for one.
- **We partnered locally, not through peak bodies.** There was no formal umbrella dairy industry body engaged. Instead: retired dairy farmers, neighbours, Barry. ACT and RAA both arrived at the same conclusion independently — "industry" at the local level means the people holding knowledge, not the corporate structures on top of them.
- **We started recording Barry.** Barry is an elder neighbour and one of the oldest people left in the region from the agricultural industry. We spent hours on his property, tractors and a WWII crane and old saw blades and stories about Baroon Pocket. He was paid by the government to clear red cedar from a First Nations ceremonial site — he saved timber from it in protest. The site was later flooded for a dam. Red cedar is now extinct in the region. We plan to record his stories with his permission, pay him properly for the audio, and publish the work through an online journal and (consent permitting) the [[empathy-ledger|Empathy Ledger]].
- **We designed an adjacent phase.** The residency funds program delivery for 2025–26. What it doesn't fund is the organisational capacity ACT needs to carry the work forward — governance, digital systems, volunteer pathways, a replication toolkit. We applied to **Queensland Gives** for a $40,000 contribution to a $54,000 **Radical Scoops Capacity Lab** (July 2026 – June 2027), designed explicitly as the "what happens after the residency" phase so other Queensland regions can adapt the model.

## What the work produced

**An instance of the Industry Residency model.** ACT is now the practitioner, not just the theorist. That matters because the argument — that creativity is critical infrastructure for regional industry — now has a live site people can visit, not only a deck.

**A lineage of stories nobody had asked for.** Barry had not, before Radical Scoops, been asked to tell his story. The project is revealing elders holding significant local history who have never shared it. This is not content generation. It is documentation of a knowledge layer that was quietly disappearing.

**Strong photography from day one.** The documentation discipline is live: photos are already being produced, will be open-source for community support, and feed the project's communications through newsletters, socials and a planned online journal with audio.

**A pattern that travels.** The Capacity Lab proposal explicitly frames Radical Scoops as a **replicable model** — the Toolkit is the durable output. Other Queensland regions can adapt the residency shape without having to reinvent governance, digital infrastructure, volunteer pathways, or the circular-hospitality design brief.

**An operating link between The Harvest and BCV.** Radical Scoops is the thickest evidence yet that the [[experiences/harvest-pairings|Harvest Pairings]] experience works — not as a scheduled twin-venue offer but as a practice that treats the two sites as one ecosystem. What happens at The Harvest (industry, food, community) feeds what can happen at [[black-cockatoo-valley|BCV]] (convening, residency, retreat). And vice versa.

## What we learned

- **Industry Residency ≠ Artist Residency.** Radical Scoops shows the difference. An industry residency isn't an artist embedded in a company; it's a community treating its own industry, land and elders as the material, with artists and facilitators holding the room. This has implications for how the [[experiences/artist-residency|Artist Residency at BCV]] sits in the catalog — they are siblings, not the same thing.
- **Lease the land, treat it as studio.** Signing a 12-month lease on 2,000 m² changed what was possible. Short-term venue hires can't hold the arc Radical Scoops is running. The lease *is* the artwork, in a sense.
- **Adjacent funding is the real financial design.** The residency funding covers program delivery. The Capacity Lab funding (if secured) covers governance, systems, people, toolkit. Most residencies end when the program money ends. This one is architected to keep working.
- **Local means the person next door.** No peak body contact, no industry association intro. Barry, a retired dairy farmer across the road, an oyster farmer up the coast. The residency kept trying to find "industry" through formal channels and kept meeting it through organic connections. We stopped resisting that halfway through.
- **The model is exportable because it is place-specific.** This sounds paradoxical. It isn't. The pattern is: find the food that matters here, find the industry that's unspoken here, find the elders who were never asked, build art that makes the connection visible and keep it circular. The content changes per place; the pattern holds.

## How this shapes the farm + harvest experience model

Radical Scoops is the most developed **Industry Residency** ACT has run. Three implications for the experience catalog:

1. **A new experience slot** — *Industry Residency at The Harvest* (or the BCV↔Harvest ecosystem) is not currently in [[experiences/README|the experience catalog]]. Radical Scoops is the case study that earns it. Next time we revise the catalog, this is the addition to make.
2. **Harvest Pairings is proven thicker than "monthly dinners."** [[experiences/harvest-pairings|Harvest Pairings]] should be read in light of Radical Scoops: the pairing is not just programming across two sites, it's operational integration.
3. **The Capacity Lab belongs in the wiki, not just on the farm site.** The Toolkit that comes out of the Capacity Lab phase is an ACT studio artefact — an exportable pattern other regions can take. When it lands, it lives in the wiki first.
