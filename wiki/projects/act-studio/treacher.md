# Treacher

> The tree is no longer in its ground. But it still carries the voice of where it came from.

**Status:** Ideation | **Code:** ACT-TR | **Tier:** Studio

## What It Is

Treacher is an art installation concept centred on an old tree pulled from the Black Cockatoo Valley farm in Witta, Queensland. The tree — removed from the land where it grew — is placed at a new site with speakers embedded in or around it, playing sounds recorded from the plot it originally inhabited. The sounds of its origin follow it.

The installation's title — Treacher — is the name given to this specific tree. A treacher is also an archaic English word for one who betrays or deceives. Whether that etymology is deliberate is not confirmed in available source material. But the resonance is there: a tree displaced from Country is in some sense deceived — separated from the relationship that gave it meaning — while the installation attempts a kind of restoration.

Treacher is in ideation stage. No installation has been built as of the wiki's last available source data. What is documented here is the concept, its connections, and the logic that makes it coherent within ACT's art practice.

## The Concept

The installation plays with three relationships:

**Object and place.** The tree grew in a specific location. It had a relationship with that soil, that microclimate, those surrounding plants and animals. Removal broke that relationship physically. Treacher asks: can sound carry what soil cannot, across displacement? Can a tree that has been uprooted still be heard as belonging somewhere?

**Memory and presence.** The sounds played through the speakers are not the tree's "voice" in any straightforward sense. They are field recordings — the ambient sounds of its origin plot. Wind in the grass, insects, birdsong, rainfall. These sounds do not restore the tree to its ground. But they insist that its ground is still real, still present, still audible.

**Displacement and persistence.** This is the weight that makes Treacher more than an aesthetic exercise. Displacement — of people, of trees, of communities, of cultural knowledge — is one of the recurring facts of Australian history. Treacher doesn't argue with that history. It holds it still and asks the visitor to listen.

## Country and BCV

The Black Cockatoo Valley farm sits in the Sunshine Coast hinterland near Witta, Queensland. It is the land Nick Marchesi personally owns, where ACT conducts R&D work and where The Harvest Witta community space is being developed 1km up the road.

The name — Black Cockatoo Valley — carries ecological meaning. Black cockatoos are indicator species: their presence signals a healthy ecosystem. Their absence signals the opposite. ACT has named this land for what it is trying to restore, not for what has been.

Treacher emerges from this specific geography: a tree from this land, carrying the sounds of this land, placed in dialogue with the question of what we owe to the places that formed us.

## Relationship to ACT Art Practice

Treacher sits within ACT's Studio tier alongside three other art installations that use physical objects and space to create encounters that land in the body before they reach the mind:

**CONTAINED** — shipping containers converted into four rooms of evidence about youth detention. The container format is the argument: young people in the justice system are literally contained. Walking through CONTAINED, you feel what the statistics describe.

**The Confessional** — a horse trailer converted into a mobile listening space. The vehicle becomes a sanctuary; the act of entering creates the conditions for honesty.

**Uncle Allan's Palm Island art** — art from Palm Island that carries the cultural knowledge and authority of a specific place and person.

Treacher's logic is consistent with this family: an object from a specific place, carrying its origin through a different medium (sound instead of visual representation), asking visitors to hold the relationship between thing and ground.

## LCAA Phase: Art

In the [[lcaa-method|LCAA Method]], Treacher sits in the **Art** phase — the moment where action becomes something that lasts. Art in ACT's framework is not decoration; it is the phase where evidence becomes experience, where understanding becomes feeling, where change becomes something a person carries with them out of the installation.

LCAA arc for Treacher:
- **Listen** — to a specific tree on a specific 150 acres of Jinibara Country at [[black-cockatoo-valley|Black Cockatoo Valley]]; to the field recordings of its origin plot — wind, insects, birdsong, rainfall — captured as the displacement happens
- **Curiosity** — can sound carry what soil cannot, across displacement? Can a tree that has been uprooted still be heard as belonging somewhere?
- **Action** — the tree removed, placed at a new site, speakers embedded in or around it; the recordings playing as the installation
- **Art** — the visitor encounters a living thing that has been separated from its ground, still trying to communicate with the place that formed it; Treacher does not describe displacement, it enacts it

Treacher's logic is consistent with the family of ACT studio installations ([[contained|CONTAINED]], [[the-confessional|The Confessional]], [[redtape|Redtape]], Uncle Allan's Palm Island art): an object from a specific place, carrying its origin through a different medium, asking visitors to hold the relationship between thing and ground.

## Connection to The Harvest Witta

The Harvest Witta is a community space 1km from Black Cockatoo Valley, being developed by ACT as a site for food, community programming, art, and creative practice. CONTAINED's permanent installation — the "Contained Wall" — is planned for The Harvest as a standing fixture. Treacher, as an installation born from the adjacent farm, could find a natural home in the same space: the land the tree came from visible from the installation site.

This is not confirmed as a plan. It is the geographic logic of the concept.
