# Palm Island Community Company (PICC)

> A pioneering case study in community control — from state-managed settlement to 100% Aboriginal Community Controlled Organisation.

**Status:** Active | **Code:** ACT-PI | **Tier:** Studio

## Overview

**Established:** 2007
**Location:** Palm Island (Bwgcolman), QLD
**Structure:** Public company limited by shares → transitioned to 100% community-controlled (October 2021)
**Chair:** Luella Bligh
**CEO:** Rachel Atkinson (also Chairperson of QATSICPP)
**Workforce:** 176 staff (as of 2026), 85% Aboriginal/Torres Strait Islander, 78% local Palm Islanders
**Tagline:** "We tell our own stories, measure our own impact, and control our own data — community-led since 2009"
**20th Anniversary:** 2027

## Genesis

Born from a tri-partisan initiative involving the QLD Government, Palm Island Aboriginal Shire Council, and local community. For 14 years, operated as a hybrid model with state and council as primary shareholders — intended for capacity building before full autonomy.

**Major milestone (2021):** QLD Government and Shire Council transferred all shares to community. PICC became 100% Aboriginal Community Controlled.

PICC operates a "whole-of-life" approach — health, justice, housing, and economic status are inextricably linked.

### PICC Centre (ACT-PC)
The physical community centre on Palm Island — the hub for PICC's integrated service delivery. Covers fit-out, maintenance, utilities, and operational costs. The building itself is infrastructure for community control: a place where health, family services, employment, and cultural programs operate under one roof, managed by community rather than external agencies.

### On Country Photo Studio (ACT-PS)
A mobile photography studio for community-controlled storytelling on Palm Island. Connected to [[empathy-ledger|Empathy Ledger]] deployment, the studio provides affordable portrait and documentary photography services while keeping cultural content under community ownership. Part of ACT's sovereign storytelling infrastructure — ensuring images of community are captured, stored, and shared on community terms.

### Digital Platform
PICC operates a digital platform featuring AI chat, a story archive, and voice-sharing — enabling community members to contribute and access knowledge without leaving country.

### Storm Stories
Following the February 2025 floods, PICC documented **60+ stories** from the disaster response — capturing community resilience, mutual aid, and the limits of external agencies.

## Key Innovations

### The "Homely" Safe House
Replaced mainland "dormitory-style" out-of-home care with a homely environment for up to 4 children, keeping them **on country**. Preserves four pillars of identity: family, culture, language, and country. Children returning from mainland care often didn't know their own grandmothers — this model remediates that.

### Workforce Localism
85% Indigenous workforce, 78% local. Staff possess "local Indigenous knowledge" — families don't need to repeatedly explain genealogies to outsiders. Economic sovereignty through employment.

### Social Enterprise Sustainability
Automotive, fuel, and call centre diversify revenue beyond government grants. Only providers on the island — essential services managed locally, not by external contractors. Telstra partnership proves remote communities can participate in the digital economy.

### Integrated Clinical + Social Care
Bwgcolman Healing Service amalgamated PICC's health center with state-run services into a single community-controlled point of care. Uses 19(2) exemption of the Health Insurance Act to bill Medicare alongside state funding.

## Structural Challenges

- **Fragmented funding:** Most contracts only 12 months, even for long-term services
- **Reporting burden:** Overlapping requirements from multiple state/federal funders
- **Data sovereignty tension:** Government metrics don't reflect community priorities
- **Lateral violence:** Being "caught in the middle" between community expectations and government policy
