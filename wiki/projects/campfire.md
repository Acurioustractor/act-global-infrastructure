# CAMPFIRE

> A cultural immersion, fitness, and mentorship program for Indigenous youth in Mount Isa and surrounding communities, founded by Brodie Germaine.

**Status:** Active | **Code:** ACT-CM | **Tier:** Satellite

## Overview

CAMPFIRE is not just a program — it is a passage. Founded by Brodie Germaine, a Pitta Pitta and Wakka Wakka man from Kalkadoon Country in Mount Isa, CAMPFIRE integrates cultural reconnection to Country, physical fitness, and mentorship to interrupt the cycles of youth crime and disconnection that plague Northwest Queensland.

The program's name reflects both method and metaphor: the campfire as a gathering place where stories are shared, wisdom is passed down, and communities remember who they are. It stands as a counter-narrative to the dominant response — approximately $1 million per year to keep a single young person in youth detention — arguing instead that transformation happens through belonging, not punishment.

## Founder: Brodie Germaine

Brodie's own story is inseparable from what CAMPFIRE became. Born in Mount Isa, he grew up navigating the complexity of life in housing commission homes with family members cycling through the justice system. Athletic prowess in rugby league carried him to Queensland state representation and a scholarship to Ignatius Park College in Townsville. At 18, he encountered drugs for the first time and spiralled through a period of addiction and lost purpose.

The turning point came through an invitation — reluctantly accepted after surviving a suicide attempt — to a men's camp led by Aboriginal Elder Ernie Dingo. In a yarning circle on Country surrounded by sixty men, Brodie found his voice for the first time. Under Ernie's mentorship, he began facilitating Camping on Country programs throughout the Gulf. His parallel passion for fitness grew from personal training sessions in a shed during COVID into Brodie Germaine Fitness — a community gym in Mount Isa that functions as a sanctuary away from lateral violence and substance abuse.

Today these twin vocations have merged into CAMPFIRE.

## The Three Pillars

### Cultural Immersion: Remembering Who We Are

The centrepiece of CAMPFIRE is the "Camping on Country" expeditions — seven-day immersions in remote areas without phone reception or modern distractions, conducted with proper cultural protocols and always involving Elders from the specific Country being visited. Activities include traditional hunting, fishing, tool-making, didgeridoo-making (which supports anxiety through breath control), and storytelling.

"Something about Country, that spiritual feeling that really, really helps the mind, body, and soul when you reconnect to land," Brodie shares.

These camps model respect, reciprocity, and relationship with land and community in ways no bureaucratic consultation process can replicate. Brodie notes: "As Aboriginal people, we do consultation naturally. When you meet someone, it's 'Hey, where are you from? Who are your mob? Who's your totem?'"

### Physical Fitness: Embodying Strength and Discipline

The community gym in Mount Isa provides structured fitness programs that build physical strength while developing discipline, goal-setting, and healthy stress management. The gym serves as a doorway — a safe space where young people can belong regardless of circumstances, and a pathway into deeper cultural programs.

"Exercise doesn't always have to be the gym," Brodie says. "It can be going out taking your dog for a walk, walking up a hill, or doing anything. The gym is a good tool to get people in the doors, and then from there we can offer a lot more like our on-Country programs."

### Mentorship: Walking Alongside on the Journey

Each young person is matched with a dedicated mentor who provides consistent support, guidance, and advocacy. The mentoring relationships formed often become lifelines for young people with few positive adult connections in their lives. The intergenerational exchange of knowledge helps heal historical fractures while ensuring cultural wisdom flows forward.

## Program Ecosystem

CAMPFIRE is part of a broader vision for an integrated ecosystem of care:

- **School-based support** — in-class mentoring and fitness activities to keep young people engaged in education
- **Cultural exchange** — exchanges between Mount Isa youth and urban communities
- **Wellness Centre** — Brodie's five-year vision: a 24-hour facility with a gym, recovery space, youth program delivery, education, and a safe space for anyone "feeling low"

All initiatives employ a trauma-informed approach that recognises intergenerational trauma while emphasising cultural pride as a pathway to healing.

## Context

Mount Isa's watchhouses overflow with young Indigenous people. The Queensland government allocates $1.2 billion toward enforcement and incarceration while preventative cultural programs struggle for a fraction of those resources. CAMPFIRE exists in this gap — a living testimony, operating in the same geography as the problem, funded on goodwill and community relationships, and outperforming the system.

Brodie reflects: "As an Aboriginal person, I still don't understand how you measure 'healing' on paper. But maybe the way to measure a young person's healing stages would be... if I take them out bush, and then when they return to community, they start to make changes with their personal lives, with their family, the way they communicate, the way they listen."
