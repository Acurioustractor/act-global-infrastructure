# Judges on Country

> "Our young people don't experience your decisions as process. They experience them as consequences."

**Status:** Active | **Cluster:** [[justicehub|JusticeHub]] | **Location:** Mparntwe (Alice Springs)

## Overview

Judges on Country is not a workshop. Not training. Not a conference breakout session with takeaway slides. It is a controlled moment of moral uncertainty inside a judge's own professional role — 30 minutes that change the way 55 judges from across Australia understand what happens after they hand down a decision.

The program brings judges to Mparntwe to learn from Traditional Owners about Indigenous history, youth justice, and what it means for young people who move through the system. It is led by Traditional Owners — Kristy Bloomfield and Tanya Turner from [[oonchiumpa|Oonchiumpa]] — and by Oonchiumpa itself as a program.

The premise is simple and uncomfortable: the people sentencing these children have never met one outside the dock.

## The numbers that frame the room

Before any experience begins, the numbers need to be in the room:

- **$1.3M+ per year** to detain one child in Australia
- **80%+ reoffending rate** from detention — the system produces the thing it claims to prevent
- **60%+ of detained youth are Indigenous** — in a country where Indigenous people are 3% of the population
- **[[oonchiumpa|Oonchiumpa]]: 95% diversion rate, 97.6% less cost** than detention
- **Operation Luna:** 20 of 21 high-risk youth removed from the list through community-led intervention

These are not arguments. They are the baseline. The experience design puts a human face on the gap between those numbers and what a judge actually knows when they decide.

## The experience design (30 minutes)

This is the core of the program. Every element is deliberate. The pacing is tight. The discomfort is the point.

### 0-5 min — Grounding

Led by Traditional Owners. Not a welcome speech. Not an acknowledgement of country read from a card. Direct:

*"Our young people don't experience your decisions as process. They experience them as consequences."*

The room is oriented. The judges are not here as guests being hosted. They are here as decision-makers being confronted — respectfully, but without ambiguity — with what their decisions produce.

### 5-10 min — Meet the person

Each judge receives a physical card. A photo. A name. An age. A moment. A quote. Just enough — like a court file snapshot, except this time the file has a face looking back.

These are Story Cards from [[#the-card-deck|the card deck]]. The portraits come from [[the-brave-ones|The Brave Ones]] — Phase 1 pilot portraits become the faces judges hold in their hands.

No context about offence history. No risk assessment. No system language. Just a young person.

### 10-15 min — Decide

*"You don't get more time. This is what court feels like. Decide."*

Three lenses are presented on a Decision Point Card:

1. **Offence-first** — what did they do?
2. **Risk-first** — what might they do?
3. **Person-and-context-first** — who are they, where are they, what do they need?

Each judge chooses individually. No discussion. No group consensus. They sit with their decision the way a young person sits with theirs.

### 15-20 min — Undo their certainty

New information arrives. One piece at a time. Pause between each.

*"He hasn't been home in 3 weeks."*

*"There is no transport to the nearest program."*

*"This is his third breach, not third offence."*

Then the question:

*"Would you make the same decision now?"*

The information they didn't have is the information the system routinely doesn't provide. The gap is not an accident. It is structural.

### 20-25 min — The uncomfortable truth

Traditional Owners step back in. Kristy Bloomfield. Tanya Turner. The people who hold the young people after the decision is made.

*"You are asked to decide quickly. Without knowing what matters. And then they live with that decision for years."*

This is not accusation. It is description. The system's design is the problem, not the individual judge's intent. But the individual judge is the one who signs the order.

### 25-28 min — Show the alternative

A real local program. Or the absence of one. Pathway Cards show what happens next under each decision — standard pathway versus supported pathway. Program/Place Cards show what actually exists in the young person's community.

This is where [[justicehub|JusticeHub]] becomes real. Not as a platform pitch. As the answer to the question every judge in the room is now asking: *"What can I actually do differently tomorrow?"*

Every QR code on every card opens JusticeHub — filtered to that region, showing real programs, eligibility, who to contact. The alternative exists. But only if you know it's there.

### 28-30 min — Leave them unsettled

Final words from community:

*"We are not asking you to fix everything. But next time you see a young person like this, know that what you don't know matters."*

No neat ending. No resolution. No action items or feedback forms. The discomfort is the deliverable. A judge who leaves this room certain has missed the point.

## The card deck

Four card types, physically printed. Designed to be held, turned over, kept. Not slides. Not PDFs. Objects that stay in a judge's chambers.

### 1. Story Cards

Photo on one side. On the reverse: a quote, 2-3 lines. The person, not the file.

The portraits are from [[the-brave-ones|The Brave Ones]]. The connection is direct: the same visual discipline — high-contrast B&W, devotional close framing, the dignity register — that the counter-mugshot series uses for galleries and exhibitions is what a judge holds in their hand at the moment of decision.

### 2. Decision Point Cards

*"Which lens is guiding your next step?"*

- Offence-first
- Risk-first
- Relationship-and-context-first
- Explore alternatives

No right answer is marked. The card forces the judge to name their frame, not hide behind "standard procedure."

### 3. Pathway Cards

What happens next under each choice. Two columns: standard pathway versus supported pathway. The divergence is stark. One column ends in a cycle. The other ends in a person with a plan.

### 4. Program/Place Cards

Real alternatives. The physical card carries a short summary — program name, location, what it does, who it's for. The QR code on each card opens the live [[justicehub|JusticeHub]] entry for that program: current availability, eligibility criteria, contact details, community context.

These cards are the bridge between the experience and the platform. The moment in the room becomes a tool in the courtroom.

## Connection to The Brave Ones

The portraits ARE the Story Cards. [[the-brave-ones|The Brave Ones]] Phase 1 pilot portraits — the first five young people photographed in Mparntwe, Bourke, and one other community — become the faces judges hold in their hands during the experience.

This is the design loop: portraits shot with [[empathy-ledger|Empathy Ledger]] consent, printed as Story Cards for Judges on Country, exhibited as gallery pieces in [[contained|CONTAINED]] rooms, published as JusticeHub nodes. One sitting, four surfaces, every one consent-governed.

## Connection to JusticeHub

Every QR on every card opens [[justicehub|JusticeHub]]. The platform answers the question the experience creates: *"What can I actually do differently tomorrow?"*

JusticeHub filtered to a region shows real programs, real eligibility, real contact details. It shows where the system works and where it doesn't. It shows gaps — places where the only option is detention because no alternative has been funded.

The card deck makes JusticeHub tangible. The platform makes the card deck actionable.

## Connection to Empathy Ledger

The six postcard stories from the Mparntwe community — Kristy and Tanya together, Jackquann and Nigel together, Jackquann alone, Nigel alone, Laquisha, and Fred with Xavier — syndicate as [[empathy-ledger|Empathy Ledger]] articles at `/stories/empathy-ledger/{slug}`.

These are not promotional materials. They are first-person accounts held in the consent layer, surfaced where they are needed: in a judge's hand, on a gallery wall, in a JusticeHub profile, in a funder deck. Same story, same consent, different surface.

## Connection to Three Circles and Staying

Judges on Country operationalises the [[three-circles|Three Circles]] methodology at the judicial education layer. The experience design follows Listen (grounding, meet the person), Curiosity (decide, undo certainty), Action (show the alternative), and the card deck is the Art — the artefact that persists after the moment.

The [[staying|Staying]] methodology matters here because the card deck is not a one-off conference handout. The Program/Place Cards update as JusticeHub updates. The Story Cards grow as [[the-brave-ones|The Brave Ones]] cohort grows. The experience is designed to be repeated — new judges, new cohorts, new communities — because the team stays.

## Cultural protocol

All cultural protocol for Judges on Country follows the same non-negotiable framework as [[the-brave-ones|The Brave Ones]]:

1. **Kristy Bloomfield and Tanya Turner** at [[oonchiumpa|Oonchiumpa]] co-design the experience and lead it. This is not a guest appearance. They are the authority in the room.
2. **No young person's story is used without [[empathy-ledger|Empathy Ledger]] consent** — including right of withdrawal at any point
3. **Story Cards featuring subjects under 18** require guardian and cultural authority sign-off
4. **Traditional Owners are compensated** as the expert facilitators they are, not as cultural consultants on a day rate
5. **The experience does not happen without community leadership present** — if Kristy and Tanya are not available, the session does not run with substitutes
