# Diagrama

> Spain's leading therapeutic detention model as an alternative to Australia's punitive justice system — studying, learning from, and working toward an ethical, Indigenous-led transfer of therapeutic detention knowledge to Australia.

**Status:** Active | **Code:** ACT-DG | **Tier:** Satellite

## Overview

Diagrama refers to ACT's engagement with **Fundación Diagrama**, a Spanish organisation that has developed one of the world's most respected therapeutic detention models for young people in conflict with the law. Spain's youth justice system, with Diagrama as a central implementing partner, has achieved remarkable outcomes — reduced reoffending, improved mental health, and the genuine rehabilitation of young people — through an approach grounded in therapeutic relationships, dignity, and care rather than punishment and containment.

The Foundation was established in **1991** and has spent over 35 years operating youth re-education centres across Spain. In 2002, Diagrama founded the **International Juvenile Justice Observatory (IJJO)** in Brussels, which provides the research and policy framework for international knowledge transfer.

ACT's interest in Diagrama sits within [[justicehub|JusticeHub]]'s broader mission: identifying and adapting justice alternatives that could reduce Australia's catastrophic rates of Indigenous incarceration, particularly among young people.

## Why Diagrama Matters for Australia

Australia's youth justice system remains one of the most punitive in the developed world, with Indigenous young people massively overrepresented at every level of contact. The system as currently designed does not rehabilitate — it cycles young people through trauma, disconnection, and deepening criminal justice involvement.

Diagrama demonstrates that a fundamentally different model is possible. Its therapeutic detention approach:
- Treats the young person as a full human being, not a risk to be managed
- Centres therapeutic relationships as the primary mechanism of change
- Focuses on reintegration and community connection rather than punishment and isolation
- Produces measurably better outcomes across all indicators

## The Diagrama Model in Detail

### Philosophy: Re-education, Not Punishment

Diagrama operates what it calls **re-education centres** — a deliberate rebuke of the concept of a detention facility. The founding premise is that young people in conflict with the law are still young people first. They need mentors, not guards. Education, not isolation. Belief in their future, not punishment for their past.

As Jesús Teruel, Territorial Director for Murcia, explained during ACT's visit: *"We aren't jailers. We're not just enforcing a sentence to pass the months until they leave. We're here to work with them, to transmit values, to show them there's a different way."*

### Staffing: Mentors, Not Guards

The role of staff in Diagrama centres is structurally different from conventional detention. Staff carry no weapons, wear no uniforms, and display no badges of authority — they are present as humans connecting with humans. They know each young person's story, their triggers, their aspirations, and they navigate conflicts with genuine curiosity and respect rather than power and control.

Low staff turnover is a defining feature of the model. People who work within Diagrama's framework tend to stay — because the model works and because the work is meaningful. Staff who have been with particular young people for 15 or more years are not unusual. This continuity of care creates the stable relationships that underpin rehabilitation.

The **staff ratio is effectively 1:1** — a figure that seems impossible in the Australian context until you understand that it reflects a fundamentally different theory of change. When relationship is the mechanism, staff are not a cost centre; they are the intervention.

One staff member shared during the ACT visit: *"Before we can expect them to change, they need to feel that someone genuinely cares about their future. Not because it's our job, but because we see their worth."*

### Daily Life and Therapeutic Structure

Young people in Diagrama centres receive **formal education for 30 or more hours per week** alongside structured vocational training. Programs cover practical skills including horticulture, carpentry, arts, and digital skills. The physical environment — open spaces, natural light, communal areas designed for connection — reflects and reinforces the therapeutic philosophy.

A **graduated autonomy system** shapes behaviour through reward rather than punishment. Young people earn increasing freedom and responsibility as they demonstrate progress. This structure creates intrinsic motivation: a sense that there is something to work toward, and that the future is genuinely open.

Holistic support is built into the program. Mental health care, substance use intervention, and family engagement are not add-ons — they are core elements of each young person's individual plan. Family involvement is specifically encouraged because reintegration into community is the goal from day one.

### Vocational and Employment Outcomes

More than **70% of young people leaving Diagrama facilities continue in education or secure employment within six months** of release. This is not accidental — the vocational programs are explicitly designed as bridges to life after the centre, not as occupation during detention.

### The Numbers

The data provides the clearest illustration of the two paradigms:

| | Australia | Diagrama (Spain) |
|---|---|---|
| Cost per child/year | $1,100,000 | ~$31,000 |
| Reoffending rate | 84.5% | 13.6% (over 6 years) |
| Staff model | Guards | 1:1 mentors |
| Program completion | — | 98% |
| Youth suicides in custody | Ongoing | Zero |
| Social return on investment | — | €5.64 per €1 |

Australia spends approximately **$1,500 per young person per day** — roughly $1.1 million per year — and achieves an 84.5% reoffending rate. Diagrama spends approximately A$31,000 per young person per year and achieves a 13.6% reoffending rate over six years. Every euro invested returns €5.64 in social value. No young person has died by suicide in a Diagrama facility. These are not aspirational targets — they are 35 years of documented results.

## Learning Trip Observations

### The Visit

ACT undertook a **five-day immersion visit** to Diagrama youth detention centres in Spain, guided by **David** (CEO of Diagrama). The visit was structured to enable direct engagement with staff, young people, and the physical environments of multiple centres — not as an inspection but as a genuine learning relationship.

### La Zarza and What Was Seen There

At Diagrama's **La Zarza centre**, in the morning light filtering through the windows, the scene was described as "a quiet revolution." Young people — many convicted of serious offences — were engaged in conversation with staff who carried no weapons, wore no uniforms, displayed no badges of authority. Just humans connecting with humans.

The immediate physical impression was striking: corridors that felt more like a school than a detention centre. Laughter. Positive energy. Strong, visible relationships between young people and their educators. *"From the moment I stepped into each Diagrama center, I was struck by the positive energy, the laughter, and the strong relationships between the young people and their educators."*

### Talking With the Young People

One of the most significant moments of the visit was sitting with young people and discussing the conditions in Australian youth detention. They struggled to understand how such a punitive and dehumanising approach could be considered acceptable or effective. They spoke about their own hopes and dreams, and about the trust they had in their educators to support them. They viewed their time in the centre not as a punishment but as an opportunity for growth and transformation.

The contrast with Australian visits was stark. In Australia, every visit to a youth detention centre leaves a sense of depletion — an oppressive atmosphere, a lack of compassion and engagement between staff and young people, an overall punitive approach that communicates hopelessness. In Spain, the atmosphere communicated the opposite.

### Staff Who Stayed

The longevity of staff relationships was observed directly — staff who had worked with young people for 15 or more years, who remained because the model creates the conditions for meaningful work. This is not a characteristic of systems designed around control; it is a product of systems designed around relationship.

### The CONTAINED Connection

The learning trip is the direct origin of **Room 2 of the CONTAINED experience** — ACT's immersive installation that asks visitors to physically experience the contrast between Australian detention and a Diagrama centre. The photographs on Room 2's walls are from this visit. The data projected overhead is drawn from this research. The question the room poses — *"What if we spent $1.55 million per child on healing instead of harm?"* — now has a 35-year answer.

## Spain vs Australia: A Comparative View

### Foundational Differences

Spain's and Australia's youth justice systems reflect different foundational assumptions. Spain's system is oriented toward **social reintegration** — addressing the root causes of offending behaviour and supporting young people in making positive changes. Australia's system has historically leaned toward **punitive accountability** — detention and legal consequences as the primary response to youth crime, informed by an English legal legacy and influenced by the US model of punishment over support.

The difference is not marginal. It reflects a different answer to the question of what a young person who offends actually is: a risk to be managed, or a human being in need of intervention.

### The Cost Inversion

The financial comparison is counterintuitive for those accustomed to Australian policy framing. Diagrama's model — with its 1:1 staffing ratios, therapeutic programs, and educational components — costs dramatically less per young person per year than Australian punitive facilities. The explanation is that Australian facilities require extensive physical security infrastructure, adversarial management, and the ongoing costs of cycling young people back through the system repeatedly. Diagrama's investment in relationship pays dividends across a lifetime.

Traditional youth detention in Australia costs approximately **$1,500 per young person per day**. Diagrama estimates a return of **€3.80 to €5.64 for every €1 invested** (depending on timeframe), while Australia's system produces an 84.5% reoffending rate that ensures those costs compound indefinitely.

### Recent Reform Trajectories

Spain's youth justice legislation — the Ley Orgánica de Responsabilidad Penal de los Menores (LORPM, 2000) — explicitly frames the response to youth crime as educational and rehabilitative rather than punitive. The court system is oriented toward welfare assessments and individualised plans. Detention is a last resort. Diagrama operates within this framework and embodies its intent.

Australia is in a period of contested reform. Restorative justice practices and community-based interventions are increasing, but against significant political and institutional resistance. The gap between the two systems remains vast.

### Indigenous Overrepresentation — The Australian Specificity

Any comparison between the two systems must acknowledge the particular dimension of Australia's crisis: the catastrophic overrepresentation of Indigenous young people at every level of the justice system. This is not a phenomenon that exists in Spain in comparable form, and it means that any transfer of the Diagrama model must be deeply transformed rather than simply transplanted. An effective model for Australia is not Spanish therapeutic detention applied to an Australian context — it is a model grounded in Indigenous knowledge, culture, and leadership that draws selectively on what Diagrama has proven.

## European Context

### A Spectrum of Approaches

European youth justice systems occupy a wide spectrum, but the strongest performers share characteristics with the Diagrama model: rehabilitative frameworks, restorative justice practices, community service, and educational initiatives oriented toward reintegration. Countries with the lowest recidivism rates consistently employ rehabilitative approaches rather than punitive ones.

Recidivism rates across European youth justice systems serve as a primary indicator of effectiveness. Spain's national recidivism rate in traditional youth detention runs between 30–50%; within Diagrama centres specifically, the six-year rate is 13.6% — well below any comparable European benchmark.

### Common Challenges in Cross-National Learning

Cross-national comparison of youth justice systems is complicated by differences in legal frameworks, cultural contexts, and socio-economic conditions. What works in one context does not automatically transfer to another — a caution that ACT holds explicitly in its approach to learning from Diagrama.

Cost-effectiveness is a consistent concern across European systems. The challenge of demonstrating that upfront investment in rehabilitation produces long-term savings — in reoffending, in health costs, in social welfare — is as much a political communication challenge as an evidential one. Diagrama's documented social return on investment is one of the strongest available cases anywhere in the world.

### Spain's International Reach

Diagrama's influence now extends well beyond Spain. The **International Juvenile Justice Observatory (IJJO)**, founded by Diagrama in Brussels in 2002, has become a significant international research and advocacy body. The **Diagrama Foundation UK** (led by David McGuire) bridges the Spanish model with English-speaking contexts and has engaged directly with ACT around the Australian opportunity. An academic comparative study of English custodial versus Spanish re-educational centres (D. Romero McGuire, 2018) is one of the more rigorous academic assessments of the model's international transferability.

## The Transfer Challenge

Simply importing a Spanish model to Australia would be inadequate — and potentially harmful. The goal is not wholesale adoption but **ethical, culturally appropriate transfer** of the therapeutic detention knowledge to an Australian context, with the following commitments:

### Culturally Appropriate Adaptation

Any therapeutic model applied to Indigenous young Australians must be grounded in Indigenous knowledge, culture, and leadership — not adapted from a European framework and applied without transformation.

### Indigenous-Led Implementation

The target for meaningful implementation is **Indigenous-led by 2027**. This means not just consultation but genuine authority and ownership by Indigenous communities over the therapeutic model, its design, and its delivery.

### The Blueprint for Change

In 2020, **Danila Dilba Health Service** — one of the Northern Territory's leading Aboriginal community-controlled health organisations — partnered with Diagrama to produce the *Blueprint for Change NT*, an adaptation report specifically designed for the Australian context. The report maps how the Diagrama model could be adapted to work within Indigenous communities, respecting cultural authority while drawing on 35 years of proven methodology.

**Diagrama Australia** already exists as an entity (diagramaaustralia.org). The IJJO provides the research and policy framework. The pieces are all there. What's missing is political will and public pressure.

### JusticeHub Learning Trips

[[justicehub|JusticeHub]] has incorporated visits to Diagrama centres in Spain as part of its Learning Trips program — enabling Australian practitioners, advocates, and community leaders to experience the model firsthand, ask hard questions, and begin the work of understanding what is transferable and what must be reimagined.

### Next Steps

The concrete pathway forward includes:

1. **A formal delegation** — Australian state and territory youth justice ministers visiting Diagrama centres firsthand. Not a briefing paper. Walk the halls. Talk to the young people. Feel the difference.
2. **Expand the Blueprint for Change** — the NT adaptation report needs to be picked up, funded, and extended to Queensland and Western Australia, where Indigenous young people are most overrepresented.
3. **Pilot programs** — one community-controlled facility operating on Diagrama principles, with rigorous measurement from day one.
4. **Connect via David McGuire** at Diagrama Foundation UK, who bridges the Spanish model with English-speaking contexts and can facilitate introductions to senior leadership in Spain.
