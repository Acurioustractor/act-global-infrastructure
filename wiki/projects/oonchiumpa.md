# Oonchiumpa

> Aboriginal-led youth program in Mparntwe (Alice Springs) — grounding young people in culture, Country, and identity across seven language groups of Central Australia.

**Status:** Active | **Code:** ACT-OO | **Tier:** Satellite

## Overview

Oonchiumpa is a 100% Aboriginal-led youth civic agency based in Mparntwe (Alice Springs, NT), working across homeland communities within a 150km radius. The program serves young people from seven language groups — including Arrernte, Alyawarra, Luritja, and Warlpiri — connecting them to Country, culture, and civic agency through mentorship, on-Country healing, and cross-community exchange.

The name and program operate under the cultural authority of Aunty Bev and Uncle Terry. The central site is Atnarpa homestead on Loves Creek Station — the Bloomfield family's reclaimed ancestral Country, approximately 80km east of Alice Springs.

Oonchiumpa is one of ACT's two primary community partnerships alongside [[picc|Palm Island Community Company (PICC)]]. It is also a key proof point within [[justicehub|JusticeHub]] for the effectiveness of community-led alternatives to youth detention.

### [[kristy-bloomfield|Kristy Bloomfield]]

Kristy Bloomfield is a Central Arrernte, Eastern Arrernte and Alyawarra woman and a Traditional Owner of Mparntwe. She grew up in Alice Springs and carries, in her own words, the stories of sacred sites and burial grounds that only her family would know. Her grandfather — a Stolen Generations man taken from his parents to the Bungalow in Alice Springs — went to extraordinary lengths to keep his children from the same fate, marrying Kristy's grandmother in a church in Tennant Creek after police threatened to take the children away. The history she carries is not abstract. It is personal and living.

Kristy holds responsibility for Atnarpa homestead on Loves Creek Station — the primary on-Country site for cultural camps.

### Tanya Turner

Tanya Turner is an Eastern Arrernte woman who took a law degree at the University of Western Australia, worked as an associate at the Supreme Court of Victoria, and ran a family mediation practice with a 95% resolution rate. One day on a tram in Melbourne, she felt the pull home and resigned two weeks later. That impulse — to bring expertise back where it belongs — is part of the architecture of Oonchiumpa.

As co-director, Tanya frames the program's purpose in systemic terms: "Our young people are just collateral in a bigger issue. The issue doesn't sit with them. It sits on a much broader level and with adults, not with children."

### Fred Campbell

Fred Campbell is a youth case worker who married into the Bloomfield family. His role, in his own description, is direct: case planning, care team meetings, and giving young people rides to school. He stays present when the pressure is highest — which is precisely what distinguishes Oonchiumpa from services that create distance when relationships become hard.

## Outcomes

| Metric | Result |
|--------|--------|
| Diversion from the justice system | **95%** |
| Youth re-engagement with school | **72–95%** |
| Youth retention in programs | **90%** |
| Active young people in programs | 21 |
| Language groups served | 7 |
| Homeland community radius | 150km |
| Cost relative to detaining one child | **97.6% less** |

The 95% diversion rate is Oonchiumpa's headline outcome — cited in [[justicehub|JusticeHub]] as a proof point that community-led alternatives outperform the $1.1–1.3M per child per year cost of youth detention. Australia's system-wide reoffending rate from detention sits above 80%; Oonchiumpa operates at a fraction of the cost with inverse results. Indigenous young people make up more than 60% of those detained nationally.

Operation Luna — a Northern Territory Police targeted response to twenty-one high-risk young people in Alice Springs — saw Oonchiumpa work alongside police throughout the year. By December, twenty of those twenty-one young people were off the high-risk list.

## Programs

### Cultural Camps at Atnarpa

On-Country healing at the Bloomfield family's homestead on Loves Creek Station, approximately 80km east of Alice Springs. Cultural camps connect youth to Country, language, and identity across Central Australian homelands under Elder guidance from Aunty Bev and Uncle Terry.

Young people go out to Country, work with Elders and Traditional Owners, and return to something that is harder to name than a program outcome but that Fred, Kristy, and Tanya all point to as foundational: knowing who you are, knowing where you come from, knowing who your family is.

### True Justice (with ANU)

A program run in partnership with Professor Helen Milroy and the Australian National University that transforms law students, future magistrates, and policymakers through deep listening on Country. Law students come to Mparntwe and participate in structured learning with community leaders — building institutional understanding of community-led justice while generating research documentation. The goal, as Tanya Turner frames it, is not just to serve Alice Springs better, but to demonstrate what is possible when Aboriginal people with cultural authority are given the resources to lead.

### Youth Mentorship

21 active young people in programs designed by community. Mentorship is intergenerational and culturally grounded — not a Western service model, but a relational process rooted in obligation to Country and kin. The consistency of the relationship is the program: Oonchiumpa picks young people up, takes them to school, takes them to the cinema. It stays when other services leave.

### Cross-Community Exchange

Oonchiumpa runs structured cross-community exchange programs. On-Country visits to Atnarpa welcome young people from other language groups onto Eastern Arrernte Country — giving them direct experience of what it looks like when peers are embedded in their own land and culture, and building the aspiration for similar programs in their own homelands.

In June 2026, eight Oonchiumpa staff will travel to South East Queensland to visit community organisations including [[picc|Palm Island Community Company]], share approaches, and build cross-community relationships. Oonchiumpa also participates in the ILA Grant knowledge exchange hosted at Atnarpa homestead.

### Partnership Programs

Oonchiumpa participates in broader community programming, including the Yipirinya sunset holiday program. On 23 September 2024, four Oonchiumpa young people — Latarna, Bonika, Tyereesa, and Hudson — joined Yipirinya's cooking class and met Miss Dan, who shared stories about her hometown in China and what her culture means to her. Miss Dan and the team then took the group to Ling's supermarket and Woolworths to pick up ingredients, and the young people learned to fold and prepare dumplings together. Cultural exchange ran in both directions.

## Stories from the Program

### Xavier and the Stretch Bed

Xavier was one of Oonchiumpa's first clients. He has a disability. When his relationship with other service providers became difficult, those services created distance — Fred Campbell uses that word deliberately, because it says everything about how those institutions understood their role. Oonchiumpa stayed.

Xavier was later released from detention. He has not been back in trouble. He built a Stretch Bed from recycled plastic — a portable, durable bed with immediate practical use in community: for ceremony business, for bush trips, for sleeping off the ground away from dogs that carry scabies, for families practising sorry business who need to move away from a place after a death.

"He just was so proud showing them that he can build it," Fred said. "He's quite capable of building that on his own and sharing that onto other kids."

Formal reports on Xavier had described his limited "ability to understand." Xavier had a different version of his own capability. Oonchiumpa bet on his version.

### J, N, and L — What the System Misses

Three young people connected to Oonchiumpa — referred to by initials — illuminate what the justice system is designed around and what it misses.

J is fourteen. He lives with his grandfather at Upper Camp. He likes basketball. He has been inside the Alice Springs Detention Centre. When asked what detention is like, he said: "At six o'clock you get locked down. You wait till tomorrow." When asked what would stop him getting into trouble: "Looking after my family." That is not the answer the justice system is built around. It is probably the most important answer in this story.

N is fourteen. He lives at a station in Double Camp, goes to school, and wants to play AFL. He knows, with the clarity of someone who has worked it out himself, that going to school every day is the path to that goal. What he lacked was not motivation. He lacked someone to walk the path with him.

L is sixteen. She manages a complex home situation with a steadiness remarkable in someone twice her age. She was sent to Darwin Youth Detention — 1,500 kilometres from Alice Springs — and was allowed twelve-minute phone calls with two-hour waits between them. "I don't like going to Darwin because I have no family there." When asked why young people get into trouble, she answered in one word: "Oppression."

Across different conversations, all three named the same thing as the most painful part of detention — not the loss of freedom, but the loss of family.

### CB: Becoming a Leader

CB is a young man in Oonchiumpa's program who describes a shift in his own understanding. Since becoming a man, he recognises his increased responsibility and his role as a model and leader in the Town Camp — something he had not understood the importance of before. He is now able to reflect on his past actions with remorse. He knows he can be a leader. This shift — from not understanding cultural role to actively inhabiting it — is what Oonchiumpa's intergenerational mentorship is designed to produce.

### Jackqwann: Art as Identity

Jackqwann is a young man in the Alice Springs music scene whose rap lyrics engage directly with themes of cultural heritage, identity, and belonging. His work addresses social justice and the experience of standing at the crossroads of two worlds — traditional and contemporary. His story illustrates a dimension of Oonchiumpa's work that extends beyond case management: supporting young people to find and use their voice in forms the wider world can hear.

### The Girls Trip to Atnarpa

On 3 July, Oonchiumpa youth worker Katrina took a group of young women out to Atnarpa Homestead as part of the on-Country exchange program, as guests of the Atnarpa Traditional Owners. Two of the young women were from Luritja Country; being welcomed onto Eastern Arrernte Country by young Traditional Owners was, in the program's own words, a privilege. The experience is designed to show young people what it looks like when peers are deeply embedded in their own land and culture — and to build the aspiration to pursue that connection in their own homelands.

### A: From Guarded to Self-Advocate

A's mother passed away and she was left destitute, living between multiple family homes with no involvement from her father. She had been on Operation Luna for an extended period and was very guarded, lacking engagement with services.

Since working with Oonchiumpa, A is forthcoming with her needs and wants, often seeking out support and assistance independently. Other services have noted the transformation — Oonchiumpa has been called into school specifically to support A (and other young people) to engage. The shift from guarded disengagement to articulate self-advocacy is among the clearest markers of what consistent, culturally grounded presence can produce.

### M: From Homelessness to Independent Living

After the passing of her father, M was living in a town camp with her Aunty, occasionally travelling to Yuendumu to be with her mother. She had no room of her own, no personal belongings — anything she did have was often stolen by transient adults passing through. Her mother was on a 10-year wait list for housing.

Since working with Oonchiumpa, M and her mother are living independently with their own accommodation. M has her own income and is actively seeking employment. The trajectory — from housing instability and grief to independence and agency — illustrates Oonchiumpa's holistic approach: addressing the conditions around a young person, not just their behaviour.

### MS: From Disconnected Youth to Tourism Entrepreneur

MS initially said he was from Alice Springs, but after talking with Oonchiumpa about family connections, language, and cultural ties, he discovered he had family throughout Central Australia. Oonchiumpa linked him with family members he hadn't known, and he developed close, positive relationships with them.

MS had a history of offending and was disconnected from family, culture, and identity. He had disengaged with all service providers and was regularly on search lists. After engaging with Oonchiumpa and earning an on-Country trip, he experienced how other Aboriginal people are building cultural tourism enterprises on their own country. He stated he wanted to do the same with his mob. Through Oonchiumpa's support, he is now working on a tourism venture on his own country. His file has been closed — he hasn't reoffended since.

## The Model: Why This Works

The clearest way to describe Oonchiumpa's model is to describe what it is not.

Most youth interventions are designed at a distance and delivered locally. They treat young people as recipients. They measure outputs. When the relationship becomes difficult, they document it and move on.

Oonchiumpa operates from a different premise: that the young people they work with already know their world, that they have capability the system rarely recognises, and that the most effective thing an adult can do is stay present when the pressure is highest.

The system was built to manage consequences. Oonchiumpa addresses causes. Australia spends more than $1.3 million per year to hold one young person in detention — more in the NT — with reoffending rates above 80%. Oonchiumpa runs at 97.6% less cost than detaining one child for one year, and produces opposite results.

This is not a complicated idea. It is, however, a rare one.

## ACT's Role

ACT's relationship with Oonchiumpa spans several workstreams:

### Empathy Ledger Deployment

Oonchiumpa is one of the two primary deployment sites for [[empathy-ledger|Empathy Ledger]] — ACT's consent-first storytelling platform with sovereign data control. Oonchiumpa leaders use it to document youth mentorship and on-Country healing across seven language groups, with data fully under community control. Stories of young people in the program — including those cited in published articles — are drawn from Empathy Ledger transcripts with consent protocols in place. Names are used with permission; initials are used for those under 18.

### JusticeHub Evidence

Oonchiumpa's outcomes are featured as a local proof point in JusticeHub's evidence base — alongside BG Fit (Mount Isa) and Confit Pathways (Sydney) — demonstrating the 78% average success rate of community programs versus 15.5% for detention. The 95% diversion rate and Operation Luna outcomes are the headline citations.

### Goods on Country Manufacturing

A two-year co-design process between ACT and Oonchiumpa explored an Alice Springs production facility for [[goods-on-country|Goods on Country]] containerised manufacturing. Xavier's Stretch Bed — built from recycled plastic with direct community utility — is an early proof of concept for this work. The partnership is central to the REAL Innovation Fund EOI (submitted March 2025 to DEWR), seeking $1.2M over four years for a production facility pathway.

### R&D Tax (Supporting Activities)

Oonchiumpa is coded as project `ACT-OO` in ACT's financial systems. International research into social enterprise models (Portugal/Spain) conducted in relation to Oonchiumpa is classified as supporting R&D activity under ACT's R&D tax incentive claim.

## Place: Mparntwe (Alice Springs)

Mparntwe is the Arrernte name for Alice Springs, in the Northern Territory. It is the traditional Country of the Central and Eastern Arrernte peoples. The town sits at the geographic centre of Australia and is surrounded by the homeland communities that Oonchiumpa serves — from Upper Camp and Double Camp in town to stations across 150km of desert.

The MacDonnell Ranges frame the town to the north and south. The stories of the Arrernte people run through the land, inseparable from the work Oonchiumpa does. The Elders who hold cultural authority in Mparntwe — Aunty Bev and Uncle Terry — are not advisors to the program. They are its centre.

Atnarpa homestead (Loves Creek Station) is approximately 80km east of Alice Springs — the Bloomfield family's reclaimed ancestral Country, used as the base for cultural camps and cross-community exchange.

## LCAA Phase

**Action** — Oonchiumpa embodies the Action phase of the [[lcaa-method|LCAA Method]]: young people are walked into their identity and responsibility through culture, on Country, under Elder authority. The program is itself the methodology — not a service delivered to a community, but a living practice of cultural sovereignty.

## Connection to The Third Reality

Oonchiumpa is a primary evidence case for [[third-reality|The Third Reality]] — ACT's framework for proving that community-led alternatives to the current system produce better outcomes at lower cost.

The 95% diversion rate makes the economic and moral case simultaneously: Oonchiumpa achieves outcomes that the $1.3M-per-year youth detention system cannot, at a fraction of the cost, while restoring culture rather than destroying it. Xavier built a bed. J wants to look after his grandfather. N wants to play football. L named the root cause in one word.
