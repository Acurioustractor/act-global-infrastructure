# TOMNET

> A community network dedicated to elder support — connecting older people to each other, to services, and to the social fabric that sustains wellbeing in later life.

## Overview

TOMNET is a community network operating in the tradition of grassroots mutual aid and neighbourhood connection for older Australians. At its core, TOMNET addresses one of the most pervasive and underacknowledged health challenges in aged communities: social isolation.

The network works by creating and maintaining the connective tissue of community — the informal relationships, neighbourhood check-ins, shared activities, and information pathways that formal aged care services cannot replicate and rarely attempt.

## The Problem TOMNET Addresses

Social isolation among elders is not just a quality-of-life issue — it is a significant health risk, associated with cognitive decline, depression, increased hospitalisation, and earlier mortality. Australia's aged care system is built primarily around physical and clinical need. The social and relational dimensions of ageing well fall into a gap that communities, not institutions, must fill.

TOMNET exists in that gap.

## What the Network Does

### Elder-to-Elder Connection
Peer relationships between older people — not just between a worker and a client. The network model means that elders are both receivers and providers of community support.

### Community Infrastructure
Creating the regular touchpoints — social gatherings, phone trees, information sharing, activity groups — that generate the informal support that sustains older people between formal service contacts.

### Service Navigation
Helping elders and their families navigate the often complex landscape of aged care services, entitlements, and community resources.

### Listening as Foundation
TOMNET's approach starts from attending to what elders actually need and value — not what services have decided to offer. This deep listening orientation aligns it with ACT's [[lcaa-method|LCAA Method]].

## LCAA Phase

**Listen** — TOMNET's primary mode is attentive community presence: being there, paying attention, building the trust that lets people ask for and offer help. In a system that frequently treats elders as passive recipients of services, this listening stance is itself an intervention.
