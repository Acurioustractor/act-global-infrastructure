# Barkly Backbone

> A backbone organisation doesn't do the work — it makes the work possible. In the Barkly, that distinction is the difference between communities thriving and communities burning out.

**Status:** Ideation | **Code:** ACT-BB

## What It Is

Barkly Backbone is an early-stage concept for a shared operational infrastructure serving community organisations in the Barkly region of the Northern Territory. The Barkly — centred on Tennant Creek, approximately 500km north of Alice Springs — is one of Australia's most remote and geographically dispersed regions, with a predominantly Aboriginal population spread across vast distances and served by a small number of chronically under-resourced organisations.

The concept draws from the "backbone organisation" model developed in collective impact theory: a dedicated entity that provides shared services — administration, technology, data, logistics, governance support — so that frontline community organisations don't have to build those capabilities themselves. Every hour a Tennant Creek youth worker spends on compliance paperwork is an hour not spent with a young person. Barkly Backbone is the concept that addresses that equation.

## The Barkly Region

Tennant Creek (Jurnkurakurr) is the largest town in the Barkly region, with a population of approximately 3,000. It is the administrative and service centre for a catchment that extends hundreds of kilometres in every direction. The region has some of Australia's highest rates of child welfare intervention, youth justice involvement, and housing instability — alongside a rich Aboriginal cultural landscape and a number of organisations working hard with minimal infrastructure.

Key facts grounding the concept:
- The Barkly is in the SPSP (Stronger Places Stronger People) network — confirmed in ACT project_knowledge (March 2026), which notes Tennant Creek as a SPSP site
- PLACE ($38.6M backbone fund, First Nations-majority board) specifically identifies Tennant Creek in its network — and has no data platform, a gap ACT's justice evidence infrastructure is positioned to fill
- CONTAINED's national touring schedule includes a planned Tennant Creek stop (June 2026, $75K budget) — the highest per-stop budget, reflecting the cost of cultural safety and local facilitation in remote settings

## The Problem Barkly Backbone Is Designed to Solve

Small community organisations in remote Australia face a structural paradox: they are closest to the people who need support, and furthest from the administrative infrastructure that funders require. The result is a perpetual capacity trap — organisations spend disproportionate time on reporting, acquittals, and compliance rather than the relational work that actually produces outcomes.

This is not a problem unique to the Barkly. But the Barkly's remoteness amplifies it. There are no consultants down the road. Broadband is unreliable. Staff turnover is high. Organisations doing transformational work often can't demonstrate their outcomes in the formats funders require — which means they don't get funded, which means the work stops.

A backbone organisation in the Barkly would provide:
- **Shared technology infrastructure** — community-appropriate digital tools, data systems, reporting templates
- **Administrative capacity** — grant writing, financial reporting, acquittals
- **Data and evidence support** — helping organisations document outcomes in formats that funders and policymakers recognise
- **Logistics and connectivity** — practical support for organisations operating across vast distances

## Connection to ACT's Ecosystem

Barkly Backbone connects to several ACT workstreams in ideation:

### JusticeHub
JusticeHub is the ACT platform built to document and surface what community programs actually achieve. In the Barkly, JusticeHub would function as the evidence layer for a backbone — helping local organisations prove their outcomes without building their own reporting infrastructure. The PLACE governance research (March 2026) explicitly identifies the Barkly as a target geography where that kind of evidence layer fills a genuine gap.

### Oonchiumpa
Oonchiumpa operates across Central Australia from its base in Mparntwe (Alice Springs), with some reach toward the Barkly. Its model — community-led, Elder-governed, outcome-documented — is the type of organisation a backbone would serve. The Oonchiumpa evidence base (95% diversion, Operation Luna) shows what becomes visible when organisations get the support to document their work.

### Goods on Country
Goods on Country has identified Tennant Creek as a potential manufacturing site for containerised production. A backbone organisation in the Barkly could provide the local anchoring infrastructure that makes a Goods on Country node viable — shared admin, local relationships, governance capacity.

### CivicGraph
CivicGraph maps funding flows and organisational relationships across the Australian social sector. The Barkly, as a named geography in PLACE's SPSP network, is already embedded in the data layer. Barkly Backbone would be the field-level complement to that intelligence.

## The Backbone Model

The backbone concept comes from the collective impact framework developed by FSG and Kania & Kramer (2011). The argument: complex social problems require coordinated action across multiple organisations, and coordination requires dedicated infrastructure. Without a backbone, organisations work in parallel rather than together, duplicate effort, compete for the same funding, and lose the cumulative learning their work generates.

In the Barkly context, the specific backbone functions most needed are:
1. **Governance support** — helping community-controlled organisations meet their legal and compliance obligations without diverting frontline capacity
2. **Shared data** — consistent outcome measurement across organisations, feeding both internal learning and external accountability
3. **Funder interface** — aggregating the evidence base of multiple organisations to make the collective impact legible to government and philanthropy
4. **Local infrastructure** — the physical and logistical backbone for organisations operating in very remote settings

## Current Stage

This is a concept in early ideation. No funding has been committed. No organisational partner has been confirmed in available records. The wiki has not yet absorbed field research or community consultation for this project. What is documented here is the problem the concept addresses and the ecosystem logic that makes it coherent.

The project requires:
- Community consultation in Tennant Creek and the broader Barkly — this must be led by local organisations, not imposed by ACT
- Identification of existing backbone functions already operating in the region (to complement rather than duplicate)
- A governance model that keeps local organisations in control
- Funding — backbone infrastructure is chronically under-funded nationally, and the Barkly's remoteness multiplies costs

## LCAA Phase

In the [[lcaa-method|LCAA Method]], Barkly Backbone sits primarily in the **Action** phase — it is a backbone-organisation concept designed to remove the structural friction that keeps community work in the Barkly under-funded and under-evidenced. Because the project is still in ideation, the **Listen** phase is its current and necessary work; community consultation in Tennant Creek must precede any operational design.

LCAA arc for Barkly Backbone:
- **Listen** — community consultation in Tennant Creek and the broader Barkly, led by local organisations rather than imposed by ACT; mapping existing backbone functions already operating in the region
- **Curiosity** — adapting the collective-impact backbone model (FSG / Kania & Kramer) to the specific remoteness, dispersion, and capacity-trap conditions of the Barkly
- **Action** — shared technology, administrative capacity, evidence and data support, logistics and connectivity for organisations operating across vast distances; complementing rather than duplicating what already works
- **Art** — making the cumulative impact of small Barkly organisations legible to funders and policymakers in a form that respects local governance

Barkly Backbone is the field-level complement to [[civicgraph|CivicGraph]]'s national funding-flow intelligence: the platform shows where the gaps are; the backbone makes it possible for Barkly organisations to act in those gaps.
