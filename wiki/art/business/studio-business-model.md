# Studio Business Model

> The Studio does not need to behave like a SaaS business, but it does need a clear economic logic. The point is to fund the commons, deepen the field, and keep artistic work strategically alive.

## The Core Principle

ACT's art field is not valuable only if it sells objects.

The Studio's business model is a mixed model. It creates value through:

- direct revenue
- strategic leverage
- ecosystem strengthening
- documentation and syndication
- new partnership pathways

Success is therefore broader than "sold more works this quarter," but it still has to be legible in practical terms.

## Revenue Pathways

### 1. Touring and licensing

Some works behave like deployable public infrastructure.

Example:
- [[contained|CONTAINED]] can tour, be hosted, and be licensed into institutional or public contexts

This pathway can generate:

- venue fees
- touring fees
- facilitation fees
- documentation fees

### 2. Commissions

ACT can be commissioned to create works for:

- public institutions
- conferences
- cultural organisations
- councils
- community partners
- justice and social innovation settings

The right commission is one that strengthens the field, not just the invoice line.

Projects like [[gold-phone|Gold.Phone]] also point toward a hostable communication-installation format, while [[cars-and-microcontrollers|Cars and Microcontrollers]] points toward education, workshop, and maker-lab pathways that still sit inside the Studio logic rather than outside it.
[[the-confessional|The Confessional]] also sits here as a mobile therapeutic-art format that could be hosted or commissioned into remote and underserved contexts without losing its artistic logic.

### 3. Residencies and fellowships

The Studio can earn through:

- artist residencies
- research residencies
- field labs
- regional fellowships

These can link strongly to [[black-cockatoo-valley|Black Cockatoo Valley]] and [[the-harvest|The Harvest]].
This is the logic behind [[regional-arts-fellowship|Regional Arts Fellowship]] — not as a side program, but as one of the repeatable ways the Studio can support regional practice and earn while deepening the field.

### 4. Artwork sales and editions

Some pieces or derivative outputs can generate revenue through:

- original artwork sales
- editions
- prints
- objects
- documentation artefacts

This matters especially where cultural authority and artist value need to stay visible, as with [[uncle-allan-palm-island-art|Uncle Allan Palm Island Art]].

### 5. Strategic spillover

The Studio also creates downstream revenue by strengthening:

- ACT's public identity
- project trust and visibility
- partner confidence
- future commissions
- grant narratives
- ecosystem ventures

This spillover is real and should be named, even when it is not booked as "art revenue."

## What Success Looks Like

The Studio succeeds when it is strong across four dimensions.

### Artistic success

- the works are coherent, memorable, and publicly legible
- the Studio line feels like a real body of work, not disconnected outputs

### Strategic success

- works strengthen ACT's wider ecosystem arguments
- works open conversations that policy or products alone cannot open
- works bring the right people into the field

### Economic success

- works generate earned income through touring, commissions, residencies, sales, or licensing
- the Studio is not permanently dependent on grant subsidy alone
- revenue helps sustain ACT's wider commons and cultural production

### Ecosystem success

- EL gains stronger photo/video/story material
- the websites gain stronger public surfaces
- flagship projects gain cultural depth
- ACT's philosophy becomes easier to communicate through public work

## How To Measure It

The Studio should track a blend of:

- hosted works / tours / placements
- commission value
- residency and fellowship income
- artwork / edition sales
- media and story assets captured in [[empathy-ledger|Empathy Ledger]]
- partner enquiries and repeat hosts
- related ecosystem outcomes opened by the work

## What Not To Do

The Studio should avoid:

- flattening every work into a product pitch
- chasing commissions that weaken the field
- treating strategic spillover as an excuse for having no revenue logic
- separating art from community, place, and ecosystem context

## Website Implication

The website should help the business model, not hide it.

That means the `Works` layer should be able to show:

- what the work is
- how it can be hosted, commissioned, or toured
- how it links to a broader ACT field
- enough photo/video/story evidence to make that invitation real
