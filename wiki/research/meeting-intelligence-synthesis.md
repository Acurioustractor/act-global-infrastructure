## The Meetings as a Corpus

The 77 non-MISC meeting records in the ACT project_knowledge table span February 2023 to March 2026 — three years of ACT's operational life. They are not evenly distributed. The largest cluster is around The Harvest (17 meetings), then Empathy Ledger (12), JusticeHub (11), SMART Recovery (7), Goods on Country (6), Oonchiumpa (5), and Diagrama (4).

The Oonchiumpa records are almost entirely empty (sparse transcripts, no AI-processed content). The ACT-MISC records (30 meetings, excluded) are student team logistics. What remains is the core of ACT's strategic and relational life over three years.

This article doesn't summarise those records — the individual research articles do that. This article reads across them for pattern.

## Pattern 1: ACT Builds Before It Has Funding

Across every project in the record, the same sequence appears: a relationship forms, work begins, platform or methodology is built or prototyped, and then funding is sought.

- **JusticeHub**: TYSON Bank workshop (May 2023) → student team build (2023–2024) → $500K funding ask (2026)
- **Empathy Ledger**: BK/Joe relationship (Mar 2023) → student team iterations (2023) → government partnership (Dec 2024) → platform deployed
- **SMART Connect**: First meeting (Aug 2024) → Tim brief (Sep 2024) → Chris Gimpel design deep-dive (Dec 2024) → pilot launch (Apr 2025)
- **The Harvest**: Vision brainstorm (Feb 2026) → lease signed (Feb 2026, eight days later) → Phase 1 construction commenced

The funding conversation is always downstream of evidence of momentum. ACT never goes to a funder with a blank concept. It goes with a working prototype, a relationship with a community partner, or both. This is strategic, not accidental — the meetings show BK and NM consistently making commitments to partners before securing dedicated resources, creating accountability that pulls the work forward.

The risk this pattern carries: burn rate before revenue. The reward: the relationship and proof of work exist before the pitch, which makes the pitch credible.

## Pattern 2: Data Sovereignty as a Recurring Challenge

Across three distinct projects and three years, the question of what happens to community data after it is collected appears repeatedly — and each time it surfaces it gets more sophisticated.

- **SMART Connect (Dec 2024):** Chris Gimpel raises Minjerribah Elders governance as a model for how recovery data could be looped back to participants
- **SMART Connect (Feb 2025):** Sally explicitly frames this as "decolonising research" — moving from extractive survey-monkey-and-nothing to community-owned feedback loops
- **Empathy Ledger (throughout):** The entire platform is a response to this question — the consent architecture, TK Labels, the five consent types, elder review workflows
- **JusticeHub (genesis):** The TYSON Bank workshop's "citizen sovereign data model" is the earliest articulation — data as infrastructure the citizen controls, not the state

What is striking is that this question recurs in a non-Indigenous organisation (SMART Recovery Australia) as well as in Indigenous-adjacent contexts. The question is not only about First Nations data sovereignty — it is about the broader principle of community members having ownership and reciprocity around data they generate. ACT carries this as a value and surfaces it in partnership conversations regardless of context.

By 2026 ACT has a product (Empathy Ledger), a methodology (transcript analysis, consent infrastructure), and a track record of raising this question in partner conversations. The question has matured from a challenge into a competitive differentiator.

## Pattern 3: The Farm as Relational Infrastructure

Three separate meeting threads converge on ACT's property (farm/Harvest) as something more than a physical asset.

**Sally Grimsley-Ballard (SMART Recovery CEO)** disclosed in February 2025 that her time on the ACT farm had broken a burnout cycle she hadn't been able to shift. She stopped vaping. She came back to work differently. BK's response was immediate: name the opportunity explicitly — a "mini Silos-based experience" for NFP sector leaders. Rest, reset, strategic coaching, cultural immersion. Brief stays, not full programs.

**The Justice Hub farm gathering:** In the same conversation, BK pitched the idea of bringing Brodie (BG Fit/Mount Isa), Stratty, Palm Island, and Brisbane justice organisations to the farm for a peer mentoring and capacity-building convening. Sally accepted enthusiastically.

**The Harvest's vision (NM Brainstorm):** The Compendium explicitly names the farm and The Harvest as places where LCAA becomes physical. The garden teaches. The table connects. The commons builds the hinterland economy. Beautiful Obsolescence is not an abstract concept but a design practice embedded in the soil.

The farm is appearing across the meeting record as a **relational infrastructure layer** — the place where partnerships deepen, leaders recover, communities convene. This is not in any strategy document; it is visible only in the meeting record, in the texture of what people say when they are not pitching.

## Pattern 4: The "Repair Mindset" Critique

A phrase appears in both the 2023 TYSON Bank workshop notes and in Olga's 2025 Diagrama conversation: the "repair mindset." The argument is this: if you enter a broken system with the goal of repairing it, you are trapped within the system's logic. You cannot design what great looks like from inside the repair frame. You need solution creation, not repair.

In the TYSON workshop, Paul (the youth homelessness founder) articulated this: *"You cannot create anything — you can't see past the problem. Million dollars in consulting on how to repair broken systems."* The antidote was a prompt engineering question: *"What would great look like if it works?"*

Olga's Diagrama conversation echoes this: the legislative and institutional barriers to reform are not primarily about evidence. They are about a system that is designed to manage existing problems, not to imagine different outcomes.

This shared diagnosis — across a 2023 workshop and a 2025 NT sector elder conversation — suggests it is a genuine ACT intellectual commitment, not just a rhetorical move. It shows up in platform design (JusticeHub builds evidence for what works, not just what's broken), in the Harvest (it is not a repair of the hinterland's lack — it is a new commons), and in EL (not a transparency tool but a sovereignty platform).

## Pattern 5: Strategic Relationships Are Built Through Extended Conversation

The most consequential relationships in the meeting record were not formed in a single meeting. They were built through sustained, open-ended conversation.

- **BK and Joe (Confit Pathways):** Meeting 2 in the record is already a continuation — ACT is inside Joe's vision, not just consulting on it. The Snow Foundation pitch is a shared project.
- **Chris Gimpel (Clean Slate Clinic):** The Chris interview is 55,000 characters. It ranges across platform architecture, data sovereignty, NZ media strategy, SMART Global politics, and a anecdote about axolotl-loving GPs. This is not a scoping call. It is a relationship being built through shared intellectual exploration.
- **Olga (Diagrama/NT):** The meeting is 52,000 characters. Olga's backstory takes up significant space — not because it is small talk, but because understanding where someone comes from is how ACT understands what they know.
- **Sally Grimsley-Ballard (SMART):** The BK/Sally/April meeting is 48,000 characters. It ends with Sally sharing a personal epiphany about burnout and BK naming it as strategic.

The length and depth of these meetings is not inefficiency — it is the ACT methodology. The [[lcaa-method|LCAA Method]] starts with Listen. The meeting record shows what listening actually looks like: it is slow, it is open-ended, it allows for the unexpected (Sally's burnout confession, Olga's Foreign Affairs years), and it generates knowledge that no structured interview would produce.

## Pattern 6: Convergence Points Appear Unexpectedly

Several moments in the meeting record show two previously separate threads converging in real time.

- **The Woodford/Harvest convergence:** In the Diagrama May 2024 meeting, David and the ACT team float running a Diagrama-model program *"out of somewhere we are already building."* Woodford is adjacent to Witta, where The Harvest lease was being negotiated in parallel. A youth justice therapeutic facility and a regenerative community space in the same geography — the meeting record captures the moment this connection was first made.

- **The justice orgs + farm convergence:** BK's idea in the February 2025 SMART meeting — bring Brodie, Stratty, Palm Island, and Brisbane justice orgs to the farm for peer mentoring — connects SMART Recovery's decolonising research interest, JusticeHub's community capacity-building vision, and the farm's healing-space function in a single suggestion.

- **The JVT/Goods/storytelling convergence:** The John Villiers Trust meeting (February 2026) connected the FRRR Conference, Goods on Country bed delivery, and ACT's storytelling methodology in a single commission — 6 videos for the Philanthropy Australia Conference, filmed on Palm Island. Three projects, one moment of integration.

These convergences are not planned in advance — they appear in conversation. The meeting record is where ACT's strategic synthesis actually happens.

## Pattern 7: The Commercial Structure Lags the Vision

Across the record, commercial and legal structuring is consistently the last thing addressed.

- **JusticeHub:** The vision is clear in 2023; the Standard Ledger commercial structure conversation is February 2026 — three years later
- **The Harvest:** The vision (Compendium) is written on February 8, 2026; the lease is signed on February 16; the Phase 1 budget ($117,800) appears in the same week
- **SMART Connect:** The pilot is running before the full data security architecture for member invitations is resolved (the BK/Sally/April meeting explicitly flags this as outstanding)

This is not negligence — it reflects ACT's approach of building trust and demonstrating the vision before formalising the commercial relationship. But it creates real risk: partnerships can advance to the point of commitment before key terms are settled, and the urgency of fixing what's already moving can crowd out the space to get the structure right.

## What 2023–2026 Looks Like in Aggregate

The meeting record tells a story of an organisation that began three years ago with workshops, ideas, and university student teams, and by 2026 has:

- A signed property lease and construction underway
- A pilot community platform in its first live cohort
- A $500K funding ask for an evidence platform with $94.6B in data tracked
- An international therapeutic justice model moving toward an Australian pilot
- A government partnership channel in Canberra
- A sovereign storytelling platform in deployment

None of these outcomes are announced in the meetings that preceded them. They accumulate. The relationships deepen. The convergences appear. The visions get ground.

The meeting record is not evidence of a strategic plan being executed. It is evidence of an organisation that builds trust first and structure second, and that the trust — consistently, across project after project — eventually holds the weight.

## Key Meetings to Flag for the User

Three meetings from this corpus deserve specific attention:

1. **TYSON Bank Workshop (May 2023, ACT-JH):** JusticeHub's founding document. The citizen-sovereign data model, the 7-year-old Aboriginal girl thought experiment, the avoided-cost argument — all present. If ACT ever needs to articulate its origin story for youth justice work, this is it.

2. **Chris Gimpel Interview (December 2024, ACT-SM):** The richest single design conversation in the record. The 20–30% crisis support architecture insight is a genuine constraint that shapes the entire platform. The data sovereignty thread directly connects SMART to EL methodology. Worth reading in full.

3. **NM Brainstorm / The Compendium (February 2026, ACT-HV):** The Harvest's founding narrative. Written eight days before the lease was signed. The "Barry Rodgerig's shed as archaeology" passage and the Quandamooka oyster cycle are the most articulate statements in the entire record of what ACT believes about place, memory, and what it means to build something worth handing over.
