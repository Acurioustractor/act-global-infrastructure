# The Cure Already Exists

> Op-ed by Benjamin Knight arguing that the evidence for youth justice reform is in — community-led programs already work, and the only missing ingredient is political will and funding.

## Overview

Published in 2026, this piece was written as a launch essay for the [[contained|CONTAINED]] national tour. It makes the case that Australia's youth justice crisis has a known cure — not in theory, but in operating programs across Alice Springs, Palm Island, and Mount Druitt — and that the task now is making these alternatives visible, funded, and undeniable.

The essay weaves together personal testimony, comparative international evidence (Spain's Diagrama Foundation), First Nations program outcomes, and the work of [[goods-on-country|Goods on Country]], building toward a call to experience CONTAINED.

**Author:** Benjamin Knight (co-founder, A Curious Tractor)

## The Core Argument

Australia spends more than a billion dollars a year detaining children. The Productivity Commission's Report on Government Services puts the average cost at $1.3 million per child per year ($3,600/day). The return on that investment: 85 percent of young people who come out go straight back in.

First Nations children bear this disproportionately — they are 21 times more likely to end up in detention than their non-Indigenous classmates, and comprise over 60 percent of the detained population.

The essay's central claim: **this is not a problem without a solution. The cure already exists.** It lives in communities. The question is whether the rest of Australia will catch up.

## The Evidence: Programs That Work

### Oonchiumpa (Alice Springs)
- 100% Aboriginal-led. Founded by Kristy Bloomfield (Central Arrernte, Eastern Arrernte and Alyawarra woman, Traditional Owner of Mparntwe) and Tanya Turner (Eastern Arrernte woman, UWA law graduate, former Associate at the Supreme Court of Victoria)
- 95% diversion success rate
- 97.6% cheaper than detention
- NT Police Operation Luna: worked with 21 high-risk young people; by December, only one remained on the list
- Works with 32 partner organisations across Central Australia
- 72% of disengaged youth returned to education through their programs
- Being studied by Flinders University; running ANU's True Justice program

Kristy Bloomfield: *"These aren't 'bad kids', they're children without basics many Australians take for granted. The system responds with detention that costs millions and changes nothing. We respond with cultural connection, practical support, and the authority that comes from being their Elders, their Traditional Owners, their family."*

See: [[oonchiumpa|Oonchiumpa]]

### PICC — Palm Island Community Company
- 197 staff delivering services across North Queensland
- 78% diversion rate
- Philosophy: culture is protection. When young people know who they are, where they come from, and who their family is, they make different choices.

### Mounty Yarns (Mount Druitt, Western Sydney)
- Youth-led storytelling — young people writing and publishing their own stories, running their own media
- 30% of participants now pursuing media careers
- *"They are not waiting for someone to give them a platform. They are building their own."*

See: [[mounty-yarns|Mounty Yarns]]

## The International Comparison: Diagrama Foundation (Spain)

Benjamin Knight visited the [[diagrama|Diagrama Foundation]] in Spain — an organisation that runs youth detention centres with a fundamentally different philosophy.

Key contrasts:

| Metric | Spain (Diagrama) | Australia |
|--------|-----------------|-----------|
| Recidivism rate | 13.6% | 85%+ |
| Annual cost per young person | ~$31,000 | $1.3 million |
| Social return (per University of Valencia) | 5.64 euros per euro invested | — |

What makes the difference:
- Staff are called "social educators", not guards. They stay for years, sometimes decades.
- Carmen, one staff member, has been there fifteen years and knows every young person by name, story, and what keeps them up at night.
- Territorial Director Jesus Teruel: *"We aren't jailers. We're not just enforcing a sentence to pass the months until they leave. We're here to work with them, to transmit values, to show them there's a different way."*
- The model: *"Think of good parenting. A good parent maintains clear boundaries while providing unconditional worth. They balance accountability with support, consequences with care."*

Diagrama has been running since 1991. This is not experimental. It works.

## Goods on Country — The Bed as Justice Argument

The essay makes an unusual link: between a bed and a courtroom.

[[goods-on-country|Goods on Country]] (led by Nicholas Marchesi at A Curious Tractor) manufactures essential goods — starting with a bed made from recycled plastic, galvanised steel, and heavy-duty Australian canvas — with remote Indigenous communities. 400+ beds delivered (v.1 to v.3) across eight communities. A containerised production facility makes ~30 beds a week.

Why this belongs in a youth justice argument:
- Ivy on Palm Island: *"Hardly anyone around the community has beds. When family comes to visit, people sleep on the floor."*
- One in three children in remote communities has scabies at any given time. Scabies → Rheumatic Heart Disease. 59% of remote homes lack washing machines.
- Kids who are sick don't go to school. Kids who don't go to school end up in the system.
- The connection between a bed and a courtroom is *"not theoretical. It is a line you can draw with your finger."*

Linda Turner (Tennant Creek): *"We've never been asked at what sort of house we'd like to live in."*

The cultural backbone of Goods on Country is [[oonchiumpa|Oonchiumpa]]. Elder Dianne Stokes co-designed and named the Pakkimjalki Kari washing machine in Warumungu language. Norman Frank Jupurrurla (Warumungu Elder and housing advocate) guides community engagement.

## JusticeHub as Infrastructure

The essay situates [[justicehub|JusticeHub]] as the data layer making all of this visible:

- 800+ verified interventions, organisations, and programs mapped across every state and territory
- $1 billion in justice spending tracked, line by line
- Shows the gap between what governments spend on detention and what they invest in what actually works
- "All of it public. All of it sourced. All of it designed to make the case that communities already have the answers."
