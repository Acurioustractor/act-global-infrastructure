## The Vision Before the Lease

### NM Brainstorm — The Compendium (February 2026)

The richest document in the Harvest meeting record isn't a meeting in the traditional sense — it's Nicholas Marchesi's brainstorm session that produced "The Compendium," the founding narrative of The Harvest. Structured in nine movements, it captures the complete vision:

**The Silence** — Witta has no gathering place. Two thousand cars pass through every weekend. One cafe. The hinterland is hollowing out; people drive through but never stop. The silence isn't peace — it's absence.

**The Shed** — [[barry-rodgerig|Barry Rodgerig]] is 80 years old. He has been on this land since 1972. Before that, Peachester — 25 years of dairy, timber, and red soil. He drove tractors before he could see over the steering wheel. His shed is full of machines that built the Sunshine Coast hinterland: an AB184 log truck from 1963, ex-army Blitz trucks, a little Italian Valpadana with a Lombardini diesel, a 1957 Land Rover he'd like to restore "if I live long enough." He starts them up sometimes, just to hear them run. *"Rust is a terrible thing. It's just like cancer in humans."* Barry watched the hippies shut down the logging at Crystal Waters. He watched the government finish the job. He watched the hinterland turn from working country into scenic drive. His shed is archaeology — the living memory of what this place was.

**The Seed** — Four beliefs, not services: Eat. Gather. Make. Grow. A cafe is not a business; it's a daily act of showing up. A workshop is not a product; it's an invitation to make something together. A garden is not decoration; it's time made visible.

**The Cycle** — [[shaun-fisher|Shaun Fisher]] is a Goenpul man, one of the three clans of the Quandamooka People (Traditional Owners of Minjerribah/North Stradbroke Island). When colonisers arrived in Brisbane, they blew up the Quandamooka oyster leases for limestone — the Treasury building, the banks, the city's foundations. At The Harvest, Shaun sells oysters direct to community. The shells are collected and worked into benchtops and surfaces. Everything returnable. This is LCAA made physical: Listen → Curiosity → Action → Art → Listen.

**The Canvas** — Gallery, not museum. Nothing permanent. Pop-ups before capital. Oyster nights before a restaurant. Pizza from a trailer before a kitchen. The scaffold pavilion before the permanent structure. *You're not coming to something finished — you're coming to something you can be part of.*

**The Garden** — This was a nursery once. The bones are still here: shade cloth frames, irrigation lines, propagation benches. Witta has the highest homeschooling rate in Australia and no learning infrastructure. The garden changes that. You dig in. Plant a seedling. Pull a carrot. Take home herbs.

**The Table** — Not a meeting room. Not a networking event with name tags. A table with food on it, made from what grows here. The farmer sits next to the architect sits next to the kid who wants a milkshake. Nobody has to explain why they're here. Nobody has to pitch anything. If that works — if people come back, if they bring someone, if they start saying "see you next week" — then everything else follows.

**The Commons** — Not a venue. A platform. The connective tissue of the hinterland economy. Local producers come here to sell, teach, collaborate. Every dollar that moves through The Harvest stays local. The enterprise funds the commons — not shareholders, not landlords, not a head office in Sydney.

**The Handover** — Beautiful Obsolescence. Maybe it's ACT in Year 4. Maybe it's not. Every system built is ready to be handed over. Every role documented so someone local can step in. A Curious Tractor is exactly that — a PTO shaft that transfers power, not holds it.

This document pre-dates the signed lease by eight days. It was written before the contract — because the vision came before the property, not the reverse.

## The Negotiation

### Property Partnership Discussion (December 2025)

The first recorded meeting about the Witta lease is an internal ACT preparation session (BK and NM) before meeting Grant, the property owner. They proposed:

- **$50,000 upfront** to secure the space
- **Revenue share** — a performance-based component, up to an additional $50K/year, so Grant shares in the upside
- Model inspired by the "Queenslander" in Newrybar (micro-providers, seasonal produce, community events)

Key agreement items flagged: insurance, outgoings, subleasing rights for micro-tenants, maintenance responsibilities, modification permissions, and a clear invoicing and review cadence.

This meeting also contains the first explicit statement of what having a property means for ACT: *"What does having a property look like for ACT? $50,000 on the table and revenue share."* That sentence captures the shift from ACT as a mobile design-and-facilitation practice to ACT as a place-based organisation.

The session ended with a list of early activation ideas: *"Little start of the year pop-up dinner — January. Community contribution — being something that you donate — what can you bring."*

### Grant and Nic: Lease and Design Discussion (February 2026)

On 16 February 2026, Nicholas Marchesi sat down with Grant to finalise the lease and design approach. The outcome:

**Staged approach confirmed:**
- Phase 1 total: $117,800
- Phase 2 total: $132,200

**Phase 1 Milestones:**
- Milestone 1 — $40,000 (Feb 2026): Site establishment, mobilisation, community garden works commenced, access and amenities baseline
- Milestone 2 — $33,900 (Apr–Jun 2026): Outdoor builds — pavilion, timber/fire cooking, kids area
- Milestone 3 — $33,900 (May–Jun 2026): Phase 1 soft opening readiness

Grant pushed ACT on two things:
1. **Anchor points** — locals need a reason to come regularly, not just for one-off events. The place needs a consistent heartbeat.
2. **Simplicity first** — an 80/20 approach where "base mode" is simple and repeatable (coffee, straightforward food) before scaling the program. Don't build event-led before you've established daily cadence.

These are not just operational notes — they are design constraints. Grant's push shaped the sequencing: start with the table and the garden, earn the trust of the locals, then build.

The agreement was drafted for signing the next day. This meeting is the closure point for the Witta property arc — the moment the lease became real and the vision acquired a delivery schedule.

## What the Property Arc Means

The Harvest property arc runs from a visionary brainstorm to a signed lease in eight days, but the underlying journey is longer. Several features of this arc deserve attention:

**The vision preceded the site.** The Compendium was written before the agreement was finalised. ACT knew what The Harvest was before it knew where, exactly, it would be. The property gave the vision ground to stand on — it didn't generate the vision.

**The financial model is relational.** A standard commercial lease with Grant would have been simpler. ACT proposed revenue share precisely because it creates shared skin in the game. If The Harvest thrives, Grant benefits. This is the ACT commons model applied to a lease negotiation.

**[[barry-rodgerig|Barry Rodgerig]] is not background.** In "The Shed" section of The Compendium, NM spends significant time with Barry — 80 years old, full of machines, full of stories. The decision to document Barry before the first shovel is moved reflects ACT's LCAA practice: listen before you build, every place has a story already being told.

**[[shaun-fisher|Shaun Fisher]]'s oysters are the LCAA loop made physical.** The Quandamooka shell cycle at The Harvest — eaten, collected, worked into benchtops, returned to the land — is described in the Compendium as *"LCAA made physical."* This is not decoration. It is ACT's methodology manifested in food and material.

**The homeschooling fact is load-bearing.** Witta has the highest homeschooling rate in Australia. There is no learning infrastructure. The Harvest's garden-as-classroom is a direct response to a real gap — not a feature added for brand appeal.
