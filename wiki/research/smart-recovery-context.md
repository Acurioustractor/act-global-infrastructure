# SMART Recovery: The Meetings Behind SMART Connect

> How ACT's partnership with SMART Recovery Australia developed — from a digital brief to a full platform design, a pilot launch, and a GP campaign.

**Compiled:** 2026-04-07 | **Source:** 7 ACT-SM meeting records (5 substantial)
**See also:** [[smart-connect|SMART Connect]], [[smart-hcp-gp-uplift|SMART HCP GP Uplift]], [[smart-recovery-gp-kits|SMART Recovery GP Kits]]

## How ACT Got Into This Space

SMART Recovery Australia is a peer-support addiction recovery organisation — not a 12-step program, explicitly secular, evidence-based. It operates a network of face-to-face and online groups facilitated by trained volunteers, a national app, and a facilitator portal. By mid-2024, the app was live but underperforming, the facilitator community was not cohering digitally, and SMART was looking for a partner who could help them think differently.

ACT came in through NM's network and the Orange Sky connection — Nicholas Marchesi's background as co-founder of Orange Sky gave him immediate credibility with organisations doing peer-support and dignity-centred work. The first meeting on record is 27 August 2024.

## First Catch Up with Tim — SMART Digital (September 2024)

Tim Steele manages all of SMART's digital infrastructure: the website, the app, the project management tools. Meeting on 4 September 2024.

Key context Tim provided:
- The app had been live for ~2.5 months; built at an initial cost of $500,000
- First priority had been getting something out — they had been "trying to please a lot of people" during development, resulting in a good starting point but no clear engagement hook
- The core need: a way for facilitators to communicate often and engage with each other. Once trained and running groups, most facilitators disengage from SMART as an organisation. They don't feel like they need to.
- The goal: a **community of practice for facilitators**
- Participants are a longer-horizon opportunity — the open forum model is high-risk (moderation, privacy, crisis support) for people at varying stages of recovery

Tim's framing of the engagement problem was direct: *"Participants at the moment have a very low touchpoint with SMART as an organisation — they go to meetings and that's about it."*

ACT's brief from this meeting: design a community engagement platform (SMART Connect) starting with facilitators, with participants as a later phase. Formal proposal for app uplift to be submitted that week.

One significant structural note from Tim: the distinction between participant and facilitator in SMART is not fixed. Many facilitators were once participants. The design should reflect this — graduated pathways, not hard walls.

## Chris Gimpel Interview — Clean Slate Clinic (December 2024)

The most analytically rich meeting in the ACT-SM record. Chris Gimpel runs Clean Slate Clinic — a digital healthcare service supporting people in recovery. He was identified as a key design partner for SMART Connect's participant-facing features.

The interview ran deep on several questions:

### The Safety Architecture

Chris's primary design concern: *"I need to just get my own head and others would need to get their own heads around just the care of the people in it [...] the key things in my mind are how can somebody easily access immediate help."*

His estimate: 20–30% of users in recovery groups are in a headspace where they need additional support mechanisms. The other 70–80% can guide themselves. This ratio shapes every architectural decision — the platform can be self-directed most of the time, but it must have clear pathways to crisis support built in. Not as an afterthought, but as structural infrastructure.

### Platform Differentiation

The conversation worked through what SMART Connect needs to be that Reddit and Facebook are not:
- Reddit: asynchronous, thoughtful, indexed — not suited to real-time peer support
- Facebook: too broad, too distracting, too public for vulnerable conversations
- SMART Connect: synchronous and asynchronous, explicitly bounded community, recovery-context-aware moderation

Chris identified Hello Sunday Morning as the type of platform SMART should aspire toward — behavioural change platform built for a specific recovery context, not repurposed from general social media.

### Data Sovereignty and Decolonising Research

Chris raised the intersection of peer support and data sovereignty — informed by his work at Minjerribah with Elders. The insight: community members need to receive data back. Not as a surveillance report but as a mirror: *"90% of you left with a seven-day plan. 50% of you felt more supported."* At the moment, outcomes data is collected (survey monkey at the end of an online meeting) and then nothing happens with it. SMART Connect could turn data collection into a reciprocal loop rather than extractive measurement.

This is the "decolonising research" thread that Sally would take up in February 2025: community-based, collective, sovereignty-first data practice applied to a non-Indigenous organisation.

### Danny and Encapsulator

Chris had introduced ACT to Danny from Encapsulator — a recovery-tech product — and then managed expectations. *"He went through a recovery journey and wants to be a SMART facilitator. He's really committed."* But ACT's message was clear: SMART is at a point of consolidation, not addition. Understanding what you have before building more.

### The SMART Global Context

Chris explained the landscape: SMART Recovery International (SRI) and SMART Recovery Global (SRG) — Tony funding both. SRG is the digital arm, US/UK/Indonesia/Australia. The ED of SRI had stepped down, creating a leadership vacuum. James from Yama (developer of the facilitator portal) was reconsidering his investment, potentially offering ACT the keys to the existing portal for free. Chris confirmed he would connect ACT with James.

The facilitator portal handover question was live at this point: if Yama transfers the portal to SMART, ACT's build scope could change significantly. April Long would need to understand the full digital ecosystem before deciding whether to build new features or activate the existing infrastructure.

## What the SMART Arc Reveals

**ACT entered as community designers, not app developers.** From Tim's first meeting onward, the brief was about facilitator community and engagement culture — not feature lists. The app already existed. The problem was belonging.

**The crisis support architecture is non-negotiable.** Chris Gimpel's 20–30% figure — the proportion of users who will need active support mechanisms — is the constraint that shapes everything. Any platform that ignores this will fail the people who most need it.

**The data sovereignty question runs through the whole arc.** From Chris's Minjerribah Elders reference in December 2024, through Sally's decolonising research conversation in February 2025, to BK's OCAP governance loop suggestion — the question of what happens to the data after it's collected recurs as a values challenge, not a technical one. SMART is a non-Indigenous organisation operating in addiction recovery spaces with significant First Nations participation. Getting data governance right is how you build trust.

**The Yama handover changes the build scope.** If SMART inherits the facilitator portal from Yama, ACT is no longer building from scratch. This was unresolved as of the February 2025 meeting. The outcome shapes the entire platform roadmap.

**The farm is a strategic asset for sector relationships.** Sally's burnout epiphany on the farm, and BK's subsequent pitch of "bring justice leaders to the farm," shows the ACT farm and Harvest property as infrastructure for relationship capital — not just a physical asset. The farm earned trust with Sally in a way that no amount of pitching would have.
