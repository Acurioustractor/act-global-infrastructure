## The proposition in one paragraph

Minderoo funds a three-year program that does three things simultaneously: (1) builds the **Justice Hub + CivicScope data spine** so every existing model, framework and dataset on youth justice in Australia lives in one queryable, community-owned place; (2) backs **10 anchor communities** with deep, low-reporting, untied support to do longitudinal storytelling, model documentation and cross-community exchange; and (3) convenes them — and international peers — into a **national framework for community-led oversight** that replaces the current detention-default with evidence the public, magistrates and Treasury can't ignore.

This is not 10 grants and a website. It is one program with three loops that feed each other.

---

## The three circles (the model from today's call)

**Circle 1 — Knowledge & Data Spine (Justice Hub + CivicScope)**
A single, local-AI-powered system that contains every framework, every funding flow, every program, every Hansard mention, every ABN, every evaluation, every story consented for sharing. Magistrates, funders, communities and journalists query it directly. No more $250k consultancies producing PDFs that go nowhere. The infrastructure exists today (1,081 programs, 587k entities, 1.5M relationships, $114.9B in flows already mapped) — this funding hardens it, opens it, and embeds local AI agents per region so a community in Tennant Creek doesn't need a data team to know its own context.

**Circle 2 — 10 Anchor Communities (Dunbar's number, applied)**
Ten places where we go deep for two years. Untied support, minimised reporting, maximised relationship. Longitudinal storytelling with young people (tracked through trust, not interviews). Model documentation so what works is legible to other communities and to funders. **Cross-community exchanges** so Ntumba spends a week at Palm Island, Mountie Yarns spends a week in Tennant Creek — relational impact that no conference produces.

**Circle 3 — National Crescendo (Year 3)**
A doing-focused convening — no keynote speakers, no panels — where the 10 anchor communities become the voices the sector hears. Paired with international learning (the Africa/Europe storytelling tour as a precursor), a published national framework for community-led oversight, and a public-facing campaign built on the stories and data accumulated across years 1 and 2. Builds the case that ACT, Tasmania and South Australia don't need detention — and that the alternative is already running, just hidden.

## The JusticeHub Tour (matching people to learn from each other)

Two layers:

**Domestic exchange (years 1–3):** Each anchor community hosts and visits two others over the program. Cohorts of 4–8 staff. Documented as case studies. This is the "relational impact" — the thing no conference funding line currently buys.

**International learning (year 1, then year 3 return):** The Africa/Europe storytelling tour I mentioned today becomes a structured learning exchange — 3 anchor community leaders travel with me, document what works internationally (restorative justice models in Norway, community courts in South Africa, youth diversion in Portugal), and return as the people who tell that story back to Australian government and philanthropy. Year 3 brings 2–3 international peers *here* to learn from the anchor sites.

## Why this is different from what's already funded

- **Not a service delivery grant** — anchor orgs already deliver. This funds the *visibility, relationships, and evidence* layer that makes their work legible to the rest of the system.
- **Not a research project** — every dollar of analysis sits in a queryable platform that the communities themselves own and use.
- **Not a conference circuit** — the convening in Year 3 is the *output* of two years of relationships, not a substitute for them.
- **Not a single big bet** — 10 anchors + a 10% innovation reserve means the program survives any individual site not working out.
- **Not extractive** — story sovereignty is built into the data spine. Young people own their stories; communities own their data; the framework is theirs to take to government in Year 3.
