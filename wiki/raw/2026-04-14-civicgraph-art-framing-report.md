# **The Aesthetics of Asymmetry: Tactical Data, Power Dynamics, and Revolutionary Campaign Framing**

## **Introduction: The Architecture of Information Asymmetry**

In contemporary socio-political and economic landscapes, information is not merely a byproduct of governance; it is the central architecture of power. The systemic hoarding of data by state and corporate entities generates a profound information asymmetry, functioning as a primary mechanism of subjugation. In this paradigm, marginalized communities, grassroots organizations, and individual citizens operate from a position of chronic disadvantage. They are forced to navigate labyrinthine bureaucratic infrastructures while remaining completely blind to the macro-level flows of capital, political donations, and procurement contracts. The dominant system demands total transparency from the applicant, the citizen, and the marginalized, while remaining entirely opaque itself. The realization that grant writing and compliance reporting act as mechanisms of "asking permission" rather than securing equity underscores this dynamic.

The ensuing friction—between the enforced visibility of the public and the guarded secrecy of the institution—has given rise to new forms of resistance. Artists, musicians, data scientists, and revolutionary strategists have historically converged on a singular tactical objective: mapping the hidden networks of power and weaponizing that data to shift the locus of control. Transforming raw civic data—such as the scraping of 587,000 entities, 1.5 million relationships, and $74 billion in government contracts—into an affective art project requires more than mere statistical presentation. It demands a deliberate campaign of agitation and clarity.

This report provides an exhaustive analysis of the mechanisms by which data visualization, institutional critique, affective computing, and revolutionary rhetoric can be synthesized into a cohesive campaign. By examining the historical precedents of tactical media, the psychological impact of radical transparency, and the communication frameworks established by revolutionary figures such as Malcolm X and Ernesto "Che" Guevara, this analysis establishes a comprehensive blueprint for translating raw civic data into visceral, mobilizing art that tests emotional responses and irrevocably shifts the balance of power.

## **Part I: The Genealogy of Power Mapping and Institutional Critique**

The transformation of raw data into a tool for social justice relies on a legacy of "institutional critique," an artistic movement that emerged in the 1960s and 1970s to expose the invisible forces and relations traversing social and physical spaces.1 The evolution of this practice demonstrates how data can be wielded not merely as an informational asset, but as a mechanism for exposing systemic exploitation and shifting power dynamics.

### **Hans Haacke and the Real-Time Social System**

A foundational pillar of data-driven institutional critique is the work of conceptual artist Hans Haacke. In 1971, Haacke produced a landmark piece titled *Shapolsky et al. Manhattan Real Estate Holdings, a Real-Time Social System, as of May 1, 1971*.2 This work meticulously documented the fraudulent activities and immense holdings of one of New York City's largest slumlord syndicates over the course of two decades.3

Haacke bypassed traditional aesthetic subjects, instead culling data directly from New York City public records.2 The resulting artwork comprised 142 photographs of tenement buildings, accompanied by typewritten data sheets detailing acquisition dates, lot sizes, assessed land values, and ownership structures.2 Furthermore, Haacke synthesized this raw data into complex diagrams that exposed a labyrinthine network of dummy corporations and family ties designed to obscure ownership and secure fiscal advantages.2

The methodology functioned by photographing buildings from street level according to their property boundaries, neglecting traditional compositional considerations to affirm the structures' "commodity character".2 This highlighted the fundamental contradiction between the lucrative real estate market and the appalling living conditions of the city's marginalized residents.2 The sheer power of this factual, data-driven exposure resulted in the cancellation of Haacke's exhibition at the Guggenheim Museum six weeks before its scheduled opening, proving that the visualization of public records possessed the capacity to profoundly disrupt institutional comfort.3 The museum director described the work as an "alien substance" that could not be allowed to enter the museum organism, highlighting the fear institutions harbor toward raw, unmediated data exposure.6

### **Mark Lombardi's Narrative Structures**

In the 1990s, American neo-conceptual artist Mark Lombardi advanced the practice of power mapping through what he termed "narrative structures".7 Moving beyond Haacke's localized real estate critique, Lombardi visualized the sprawling, transnational webs of power, corruption, and financial fraud.8 Utilizing graphite and colored pencil, Lombardi created massive, intricate sociograms—some measuring up to 5 by 12 feet—that charted the relationships between politicians, corporations, and crime syndicates.7

Lombardi's works, such as *BCCI-ICIC & FAB, 1972–91 (4th Version)* and *George W. Bush, Harken Energy, and Jackson Stephens, ca. 1979–1990*, did not function as simplified data visualizations intended for rapid consumption.7 Instead, they embraced immense complexity. Drawing aesthetic inspiration from celestial and navigation charts, Lombardi utilized solid and dotted arcs to represent connections gleaned from reputable media sources, while verifiable court judgments were rendered in stark red ink.7 By making the data aesthetically beautiful and conceptually overwhelming, Lombardi invited the viewer to spend time decoding the "uses and abuses of power," effectively translating raw journalism into an engaging, albeit deeply alarming, visual experience.7 The efficacy of his mapping was underscored when, following the September 11 attacks, the FBI contacted the Whitney Museum to obtain reproductions of his work mapping the financial networks of Osama bin Laden.7

### **Forensic Architecture and Investigative Aesthetics**

The contemporary culmination of this lineage is found in the work of Forensic Architecture, an interdisciplinary agency based at Goldsmiths, University of London.12 Established in 2010, the agency operates on the principle of "investigative aesthetics," utilizing architectural analysis, 3D modeling, fluid dynamics, and data mining to produce spatial evidence of state violence, human rights violations, and corporate ecological crimes.12

Forensic Architecture shifts the traditional paradigm of forensics—which has historically been a tool used by the state to police individuals—into a "counter-hegemonic practice" that turns the forensic gaze back onto the state.15 Their methodology insists that aesthetics is not merely the theory of beauty, but the art of sensing and sense-making across all entities, including digital sensors, soil, and buildings.16 By gathering dispersed data points—from satellite imagery to open-source smartphone footage and situated witness testimony—they reconstruct events that state powers attempt to deny or push below the "threshold of detectability".12 Their work demonstrates that exposing the systemic issues kept hidden by a state or institution is vital for eco-social transformation.18

### **Counter-Mapping and The Anti-Eviction Mapping Project**

Complementing these global perspectives are localized, grassroots data projects utilizing "counter-mapping." Counter-mapping is a critical cartography practice that challenges the Eurocentric, capital-driven biases inherent in traditional mapmaking.19 A premier example is the Anti-Eviction Mapping Project (AEMP), a data visualization and digital storytelling collective documenting gentrification, dispossession, and resistance, primarily in California and New York.21

AEMP explicitly rejects the notion of producing data solely for the academy; instead, they produce "data for justice".23 By embedding deeply personal oral histories and video work into a digital geospatial interface, they make the devastating impacts of venture capital and real estate speculation tangible.22 The data bubbles on their interactive maps do not merely represent statistics; they contain audio of displaced residents, synthesizing quantitative geographical data with qualitative human narratives to paint a vibrant, emotional picture of a contested landscape.24 This methodology prevents the human element from being abstracted into a mere bureaucratic metric, ensuring the community retains ownership of the narrative.25

### **The Critical Art Ensemble and Electronic Civil Disobedience**

Running parallel to visual mapping strategies is the theoretical framework established by the Critical Art Ensemble (CAE) in the 1990s. CAE pioneered the concept of "tactical media" and "electronic civil disobedience" (ECD).26 CAE argued that as society digitized, human beings were increasingly bifurcated into a physical body and a "data body"—a digital avatar made of code and information.28

Because state and corporate entities interact primarily with the data body, this digital entity becomes ripe for extraction, manipulation, and predictive logic, rendering the physical individual hypervisible to authorities yet completely opaque to the systems governing them.28 CAE posited that traditional acts of civil disobedience (e.g., physical sit-ins and blockades) were losing efficacy because power had decentralized into digital networks.27 ECD, therefore, involves occupying key digital conduits, databases, and entrances to bring pressure on legitimized institutions, weaponizing data flows to create "semiotic shocks" that disrupt authoritarian culture.26

### **Indigenous Data Sovereignty and Visual Resistance**

In the Australian context, the struggle for data ownership is encapsulated by the Indigenous Data Sovereignty (ID-SOV) movement, which demands that Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples possess the right to own, control, and govern their own data.29 The historical extraction of Indigenous data has frequently been weaponized against these communities, necessitating new frameworks for data governance.29

This dynamic is starkly visible in the collaboration between Aboriginal AFL player Adam Goodes and artist Baden Pailthorpe. Throughout his career, Goodes generated over 20 million data points via GPS trackers logging his speed, endurance, and movements.30 While this data was extracted by corporate sports entities, Goodes recognized that the data inherently captured his emotional and spiritual connection to the *yarta* (land) he played upon.30 Their collaborative artwork reclaims this bodily data, questioning who collects it, who controls it, and how it intersects with power and identity.30

Similarly, Aboriginal artist Richard Bell utilizes institutional critique to claim physical and conceptual space. His installation, *Embassy*, features a large military-style canvas tent surrounded by protest signs reading "White Invaders You Are Living on Stolen Land".31 Acting as a roving satellite of the original 1972 Aboriginal Tent Embassy, Bell's artwork travels globally to prestigious museums, establishing a public, sovereign space for Indigenous activism, screening documentaries, and building a transnational archive of resistance right inside the institutions of the colonial elite.31

Furthermore, Iranian-Australian artist Hoda Afshar utilizes 3D printing and complex photographic recording in her work *Agonistes* to create marble-like busts of Australian whistleblowers. This grants monumental, heroic visibility to those who expose institutional corruption regarding immigration detention and youth justice, while carefully protecting their identities.34

## **Part II: Acoustic Sovereignty and Musical Interventions**

Just as visual artists reclaim spatial and statistical data, musicians have increasingly recognized that digital platforms and algorithms function as modern gatekeepers. The music industry serves as a microcosm of broader systemic inequality, where platforms perpetuate structures of oppression by extracting immense value from creators while offering fractional compensation.37 To combat this, artists are utilizing technology and data to reclaim power and agitate for social change.

### **Holly Herndon and the Governance of Identity**

The advent of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has sparked widespread anxiety regarding the unauthorized scraping of artistic data. However, artist and musician Holly Herndon provides a revolutionary model for digital identity ownership with her project *Holly+*.38 Recognizing that all modern media functions as AI training data, Herndon created a "digital twin" using machine learning.38 She coined the term "spawning" to describe generating new music based on training data, distinguishing it from traditional sampling.40

Rather than defensively hoarding her intellectual property or relying solely on restrictive copyright law, she established a Decentralized Autonomous Organization (DAO).38 Hundreds of people hold partial governance over her voice model; any profit generated from approved works is split between the creator, the DAO, and Herndon herself.38 This "Identity Play" represents a permissive, artist-led intervention that establishes a radical new protocol for data ownership, consent, and revenue sharing in the AI era, shifting power directly back to the creator.38

### **Agitation Through Hip-Hop and Popular Music**

In the realm of direct political agitation, hip-hop artists like Boots Riley (The Coup) and the Australian Indigenous duo A.B. Original use their music to deliver uncompromising systemic critiques.42 Riley integrates punk urgency and anti-capitalist ideology into danceable beats, actively utilizing his platform to educate and organize mass movements against corporate exploitation and systemic racism.42 On tracks like "Pimps (Freestylin' at the Fortune 500 Club)," Riley assumes the personas of historic capitalists to expose the satirical theatricality of wealth inequality.44

Similarly, A.B. Original integrates Indigenous knowledge into the hip-hop framework, protesting systemic issues such as police brutality, deaths in custody, and the celebration of Australia Day.43 Their album *Reclaim Australia* forces uncomfortable sociopolitical realities onto mainstream airwaves, deliberately removing the public's ability to claim ignorance regarding Aboriginal issues.46 By delivering their message in a language common to all Australians, they utilize the cultural reach of music to map and expose power imbalances.43

The mobilization of musicians extends beyond lyrical content. Artists are increasingly leveraging their platforms to educate fans on carbon footprints, utilizing mobile technologies to engage concertgoers in activism, and pushing for legislative changes, such as the Protect Working Musicians Act, to combat algorithmic monopolies.47

## **Part III: Empathy, Affect, and the Psychology of Radical Transparency**

To build a campaign that moves a populace, the mere presentation of data is insufficient. Raw data is rational and dispassionate; however, political mobilization is driven fundamentally by emotion.49 Understanding the psychological response to data exposure—navigating the tension between the fear of surveillance and the empowering clarity of sudden comprehension—is essential for designing effective data art.

### **The Duality of Radical Transparency: Fear and Betrayal**

The psychological response to data transparency is highly polarized. On one hand, marginalized groups have a justified fear of data exposure, recognizing that technologies and datasets are not neutral and often uphold the very forms of discrimination they combat.50 The unauthorized exposure of personal or systemic data triggers profound anxiety. Research into cybersecurity breaches reveals that individuals experiencing involuntary data disclosure exhibit strong negative affective stress reactions, feelings of vulnerability, and psychological distress.51

This fear is compounded by the phenomenon of "institutional betrayal," a concept developed by psychologist Jennifer Freyd.53 When individuals realize that the institutions tasked with protecting them—be it universities, healthcare systems, or government funding bodies—are actually exploiting them or hoarding vital information, it results in severe psychological trauma.53 Furthermore, the "Illusion of Transparency" bias causes individuals to overestimate the external visibility of their internal states, leading to heightened self-consciousness, anxiety, and a tendency to conform to perceived expectations out of fear of exposure.54

However, "radical transparency"—when utilized intentionally to shift power—can foster empowerment rather than fear. Radical transparency requires an environment of psychological safety; it means proactively sharing important systemic information to empower participants to make informed decisions.55 By reconfiguring the visibilities of control, radical transparency transitions from being a tool of state surveillance to a mechanism of democratic accountability, replacing authority built on distance with credibility built on truth.55

### **The Psychology of the "Aha\!" Moment**

When individuals are presented with data that suddenly connects disparate pieces of their lived experience, they experience an "Aha\!" or Eureka moment. Psychologically, the AHA experience is characterized by a moment of deep understanding, accompanied by feelings of certainty, pleasure, and sudden surprise during problem-solving.58

Recent cognitive frameworks link this experience to "meta-cognitive prediction error".58 This occurs when a problem is solved, or a systemic truth is revealed, in a manner that completely subverts the individual's prior predictions regarding the solvability of the issue.58 The element of surprise acts as the primary catalyst. In the context of a data campaign, presenting a community organization with the exact statistical proof of funding disparities they have long suspected (e.g., the revelation that 873 local government areas suffer from high disadvantage and low per-capita funding) triggers this prediction error. It validates their lived experience, transforming ambient frustration into acute, actionable clarity.58

### **Affective Data Visualization**

To bridge the gap between abstract statistics and the "Aha\!" moment, practitioners employ "Affective Data Visualization"—the intentional integration of design elements to communicate and influence emotion.60 Unlike traditional data visualization, which prioritizes rapid, frictionless analytical comprehension, affective visualization prioritizes emotional resonance and self-reflection.49

Research demonstrates that intentionally integrating art with data can bridge ideological and political divides that raw data alone cannot penetrate; in fact, stark data graphs can often exacerbate polarization.61 Successful affective visualization uses realistic or metaphorical imagery to appeal to the senses.60 For instance, a study on climate change communication found that Diane Burko's painting *Summer Heat, 2020*, which overlaps red and orange motifs of wildfires with a graph of global atmospheric carbon dioxide levels, successfully made viewers stop and reflect.61

By using metaphors—such as depicting data points as physical weight, flowing water, or restrictive barriers—affective visualization transforms the passive viewer into an active participant. It provides the reflective space necessary to process systemic shocks, ensuring the viewer connects the quantitative reality to their personal, embedded meanings.49

## **Part IV: The Architecture of Agitation: Malcolm X and Che Guevara**

To elevate a data visualization project into a mobilizing campaign, one must look to the communication strategies of revolutionary historical figures. Both Malcolm X and Ernesto "Che" Guevara understood that marginalized groups cannot successfully petition an entrenched, asymmetric system; they must strategically defeat it.63 Their methodologies for organizing, educating, and shifting power provide a masterclass in revolutionary framing.

### **Che Guevara and the Foco Theory of Asymmetric Warfare**

Guevara's experiences in the Cuban Revolution culminated in his *Foco* theory (*foquismo*), a strategic doctrine designed for asymmetric conflict against heavily entrenched state powers.64

The primary tenet of the Foco theory is the rejection of the traditional Marxist-Leninist idea that revolutionaries must wait for the objective socioeconomic conditions to be "perfect" before initiating a struggle.64 Instead, Guevara argued that a small, highly disciplined vanguard (the *foco* or focus) could act as a catalyst; their immediate, decisive actions would expose the vulnerability of the state and actively *create* the conditions necessary for a broader revolution.64

Key principles of Guevara's strategy applicable to a data-activism campaign include:

1. **The Vanguard as Catalyst:** The Foco does not attempt to take control of the state apparatus immediately. Instead, it strikes swiftly to raise popular discontent, demonstrating to the broader public that the monolithic power structure can indeed be challenged and mapped.64  
2. **Mobility and Elusiveness:** Tactical rigidity is fatal.63 Guevara emphasized absolute flexibility, stating that the vanguard must "invent his own tactic at every minute of the fight," utilizing the element of surprise against the rigidity of classical institutional methods.65  
3. **The Fish in the Water:** The vanguard must be entirely supported by and respectful of the local populace.63 Survival depends on integrating with the community, treating them with respect, and addressing their immediate needs, thereby turning local populations into an impenetrable shield against state reprisal.65  
4. **Bypassing Information Monopolies:** Recognizing the state's control over information, Guevara and Fidel Castro established *Radio Rebelde* in the Sierra Maestra mountains.67 This served as a strategic communication tool to bypass state censorship, broadcast rebel victories, educate the peasantry, and agitate the population directly, proving that control over the flow of information is as critical as physical terrain.67

### **Malcolm X: The Anti-Jeremiad and the "Ballot or the Bullet"**

If Guevara provides the structural mechanism for the vanguard strike, Malcolm X provides the rhetorical framework for radicalizing and unifying the populace. Malcolm X recognized that the dominant white culture utilized the media as a "science" to rhetorically construct African-American self-perception and project them as criminals.68 His speeches were explicitly designed to correct this information asymmetry, educate the grassroots, and reclaim the right of self-definition.69

His seminal 1964 speech, *"The Ballot or the Bullet,"* serves as a masterclass in strategic framing. Rather than relying on the poetic, biblical allusions utilized by integrationist leaders like Martin Luther King Jr., Malcolm X utilized a pragmatic, down-to-earth vernacular to present a stark, uncompromising ultimatum.70

Key components of Malcolm X's rhetorical strategy include:

1. **The Anti-Jeremiad:** While traditional American oratory relies on the *Jeremiad*—lamenting society's current state but calling for a return to its essentially "good" founding principles—Malcolm X utilized the *Anti-Jeremiad*.72 He fundamentally renounced the dominant ideals of the United States, exposing the "American Dream" as an "American Nightmare".72 By doing so, he stripped the oppressor of any moral high ground.  
2. **Reclaiming Victimhood for Agency:** Malcolm X employed a brilliant rhetorical inversion by leaning into the concept of victimhood.72 He pointed out that Black Americans were victims of systemic economic exploitation and political oppression.73 However, by explicitly labeling his audience as "chumps" if they continued to participate in a rigged system, he shifted the responsibility and agency back onto the oppressed.72 This reclamation of the victim label ironically diminishes the stigma and increases the community's perception of its own latent power.72  
3. **Submerging Differences to Identify the Common Enemy:** To build a unified movement, Malcolm X urged his audience to submerge their petty religious or political differences (e.g., Methodist vs. Baptist, Democrat vs. Republican).70 He presented a singular, unifying reality: they were all "catching the same hell from the same man".73 He utilized the 1954 Bandung Conference as a historical model, educating his audience on how dark nations from Africa and Asia united by excluding the white oppressor.70  
4. **Breaking the Racial Binary:** Malcolm X utilized the strategy of binary opposition—historically a tool of colonialism—against the power structure.74 By constantly projecting contrasts (e.g., the "House Negro" vs. the "Field Negro"), he forced listeners to take a side, exposing internalized oppression and compelling them to reflect critically on their societal roles.70

## **Part V: Synthesizing the Campaign: Structuring a Data-Driven Revolutionary Art Project**

The overarching objective is to transform a massive dataset (e.g., scraping government contracts, ACNC records, and Hansard transcripts) into a visceral, affective art project that tests human emotional responses, provides systemic clarity, and violently shifts power dynamics. Drawing upon the methodologies of Forensic Architecture, Mark Lombardi, Malcolm X, and Che Guevara, this campaign must be structured not as a passive museum exhibit, but as a tactical vanguard action.

The campaign can be modeled in four distinct phases:

### **Phase 1: The Foco Strike (The Data Scrape)**

In alignment with Guevara's Foco theory, the initial action does not require the permission of the state, nor does it wait for "perfect" conditions of data transparency.64 The grueling, multi-year process of scraping, cleaning, and joining disparate datasets from a rural farmhouse constitutes the vanguard strike.

The current philanthropic and governmental system relies on information asymmetry; the government funder sees the whole pool, while the community applicant sees only a keyhole. By forcefully connecting 1.5 million relationships and tracing $74 billion in government contracts against 301,000 political donations, the artist-researcher bypasses the gatekeepers.67 This act of extreme data aggregation is akin to establishing a digital *Radio Rebelde*—creating an independent infrastructure that broadcasts the hidden truths of the establishment directly to the marginalized, thereby creating the conditions for systemic change.66

### **Phase 2: Investigative Aesthetics and the "Aha\!" Moment**

Raw data, however, can overwhelm the public. To induce the meta-cognitive prediction error that leads to an empowering "Aha\!" moment 58, the data must be subjected to investigative aesthetics.

Drawing heavily from Mark Lombardi, the project should visualize the 58x return on investment enjoyed by political donors not through sterile bar charts, but through sprawling, intricate, and beautiful narrative structures.7 Animations should demonstrate the flow of capital out of the 873 highly disadvantaged local government areas, visually representing the drain of resources.

Furthermore, employing affective data visualization techniques 60, the animations must target the visceral senses. If 87.5% of procurement goes to 10 entities, the animation should physically oppress the viewer with the scale of that monopoly. It should utilize spatial audio or overwhelming visual mass to induce the very real feelings of claustrophobia and "permission-asking" that grassroots organizers feel daily when reshaping their language to fit a funder's arbitrary KPI framework.49 The design must intentionally provoke the discomfort of radical transparency, forcing the viewer to confront the uncomfortable reality of the system without offering easy platitudes.55

### **Phase 3: The Anti-Jeremiad (The Framing)**

The presentation of this data must reject the polite, conciliatory language typical of NGO reports and corporate product reveals. Applying Malcolm X's rhetorical strategy, the framing must utilize the *Anti-Jeremiad*.72 The narrative must explicitly state that the system is not "broken" and in need of minor reform; the system is functioning exactly as designed—to extract capital and enforce compliance.

The rhetoric accompanying the visualizations must be urgent, unapologetic, and stripped of puffery. It must highlight the shared struggle: whether an organization focuses on youth justice, mental health, housing, or Indigenous affairs, they are all "catching the same hell" from the same bureaucratic monopolies and the same five consulting firms.73 By identifying this common enemy, the campaign unites disparate community sectors.70 It validates their long-held suspicions (reclaiming their victimhood to generate agency) and shifts the blame entirely onto the state's deliberate opacity, emphasizing the gap between a press release and a purchase order where trust dies.72

### **Phase 4: Counter-Mapping and Acoustic Sovereignty**

The final phase moves from systemic critique to community reconstruction. If the state views citizens purely as a "data body" to be managed 28, the campaign must return humanity to the data.

Applying the methodology of the Anti-Eviction Mapping Project 21, the data visualizations must be layered with human testimony. The sterile numbers regarding underfunded local government areas must be filled with the localized, situated audio testimonies of the people living in those 873 postcodes.

Simultaneously, the project must establish permanent data sovereignty. Just as Holly Herndon utilized a DAO to give governance of her digital voice back to the community 38, and as Richard Bell established the *Embassy* to claim physical space 31, platforms acting as justice evidence layers or storytelling ledgers must act as communal repositories. They must be presented as tools handed directly to journalists and community leaders, allowing them to own their stories and track the efficacy of their own community-led models, completely independent of state validation.

## **Conclusion**

The intersection of exhaustive data science and aesthetic visualization possesses a latent revolutionary capacity. When institutions engineer information asymmetry to maintain control, the act of independent data aggregation ceases to be a mere academic exercise; it becomes a profound act of electronic civil disobedience.

By anchoring a massive civic data-scrape within the investigative aesthetics of Forensic Architecture and Mark Lombardi, the abstract flows of billions of dollars are rendered visceral and undeniable. Furthermore, by adopting the uncompromising, catalyst-driven strategies of Malcolm X and Che Guevara, the creator transcends the role of mere documentarian to become an active vanguard. The resulting campaign does not ask the system for reform, nor does it ask the funder for permission. It utilizes the emotional shock of radical transparency and affective visualization to completely subvert the established power dynamic, handing the architecture of truth directly back to the communities it was historically designed to exclude.
