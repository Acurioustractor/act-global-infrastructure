# The Three Circles — Executive Summary
### A community-led advocacy infrastructure for youth justice
*Minderoo Foundation · FY2026–FY2028 · $2.9M over three years*

---

![Three Circles Diagram](/images/proposals/minderoo/three-circles-diagram.png)

## The proposition

Australian youth justice is not failing for lack of evidence, frameworks, or community work. It is failing because **the evidence, the frameworks, and the community work all live in different rooms — and the rooms don't talk to each other**. Reports get written and shelved. Community organisations get funded and forgotten. Frameworks get developed and never tested in country. Stories get told and never connected to the data that proves them.

We propose a three-year program that holds these things together — long enough for community-led models to be seen, trusted and copied. It is built around three concentric circles, anchored by infrastructure that already exists, and produces a single national artifact in 2028 that the country cannot ignore.

## The Artifact

![The Country We Are Building — Triptych](/images/proposals/minderoo/the-artifact-triptych.png)

Every three-year program needs something the people inside it are working *toward*. We propose **one artifact in three forms**:

- **A 90-minute documentary** tracking the ten communities, co-directed with each, premiered at the Year 3 convening, then released nationally.
- **A long-form book** with a major Australian publisher — each community gets a chapter on its own terms; community organisations retain rights and royalty share.
- **A living public platform** — the Centre opened in full at Year 3 launch. Searchable, citable, permanent.

This is what world-class advocacy campaigns look like. Australian youth justice has never had one. We are proposing to build it.

# Worked examples — how this actually works

The proposal is abstract until you see it in motion. Four short examples drawn from things we have already done or are about to do.

## Example 1 — The Queensland 70% exposé, made queryable

**What I already did, by hand, over three weeks:**

I scraped every ABN ever issued in Australia. I scraped Queensland Hansard speeches across multiple years. I ran fuzzy search to match organisation names against ABNs and government appropriations. I cross-referenced against the Queensland Family and Child Commission's youth report — the one where they said they couldn't find all the youth justice organisations running programs in the state. **I found them.** And the answer was: 70% of Queensland youth justice funding goes to three organisations — 54 Reasons (Save the Children), Mission Australia, and a third large administrator. The smaller "Kickstart" programs that were supposed to be the innovation tier are mostly failing. The large organisations keep funding because they speak the language and tick the boxes — not because they work.

**What this becomes inside the Centre:**

A journalist in Cairns types *"who receives Queensland youth justice funding"* into the platform. In 30 seconds they see:

- Total state spend, broken down by line item, sourced from Hansard and budget papers
- The three concentration recipients, with exact dollar figures, contract dates, and the parliamentary moments where the appropriations were made
- The named smaller programs, their evidence ratings from the Evidence Scatter, and whether they have published outcomes
- The board overlap between the three large recipients and other state contracts (via the Network Explorer's 1.5M relationships)
- A map of which LGAs receive the spending and which are funding deserts
- Stories from the Empathy Ledger, with consent envelopes, of young people who have been through these programs

**Without the Centre**, this exposé takes a journalist three weeks (and they probably never start). **With the Centre**, it takes thirty seconds, and every Queensland community organisation, every magistrate, every funder and every journalist has the same view at the same time. Concentration becomes politically expensive in a way it currently is not.

The data for this example exists today. What is missing is the surfacing layer and the local AI agent that makes it queryable in plain language. **That is what Year 1's $180k Centre line buys.**

## Example 2 — A magistrate in Alice Springs, before sentencing

**Tuesday next week:** 55 judges and magistrates including a High Court judge and Indigenous magistrates are coming to Alice Springs with Oonchiumpa. I'll be there filming and using JusticeHub to demonstrate how a community-led service directory could change sentencing.

**What we want a magistrate to be able to do, by FY2028:**

A 16-year-old appears before her on a property offence in Alice Springs. Before deciding on a community-based order versus detention, she opens the JusticeHub magistrate-facing tool on her tablet and types in the postcode.

In ten seconds she sees:

- **Available community-led programs within 200km**, with verification status (real programs only — the 279 AI-generated and unverified entries are filtered out automatically by the `.neq('verification_status', 'ai_generated')` rule)
- **Evidence ratings** for each, drawn from the Evidence Scatter — so she can see that Oonchiumpa's hub model has a stronger evidence base than a more expensive non-Indigenous alternative
- **Cost per young person per year** for each option, alongside the $1.33M annual detention cost (ROGS 2024–25)
- **Cultural authority** — whether the program is Indigenous-led, who the elders are, and the consent status for the young person's mob
- **Real outcome stories** with consent — anonymised but specific — from young people who have been through similar programs
- **Capacity right now** — is there a place available this week, or is the program at capacity

She is not making a guess. She is making a decision against the same evidence base as every other magistrate in the country, the funder who supported the program, and the journalist who might write about the case afterwards. The political cover for the detention default narrows.

**What is needed to make this real:** the Centre's magistrate-facing tool (Year 2 line item, $160k bracket). The program inventory and evidence ratings already exist. The missing piece is the user interface designed for the bench, and the consent and verification protocols built with Indigenous magistrates rather than for them.

## Example 3 — How a story moves through the system, with sovereignty preserved

**The current state:** A young person at Mountie Yarns shares their story with a worker over six months of trust-building. The story is powerful. It would change minds. It almost never leaves the community — because if it does, the young person loses control of where it goes, who quotes it, and what context it appears in. The result: the most important evidence in youth justice — actual lived trajectories — is the evidence the sector has the least of.

**The journey we are proposing, with the Empathy Ledger v2 sovereignty layer:**

1. **Capture in country.** The embedded storyteller (one of the 2 FTE in Year 1) sits with the young person and the Mountie Yarns worker. The story is recorded — audio, transcript, optional video — using the Empathy Ledger v2 capture tools.

2. **Consent envelope.** The young person decides, element by element, what can be shared and where. *"My name can appear in the documentary but not in the book. My face can appear in the platform but only after I turn 18. My quote about my dad cannot be separated from my quote about my uncle. I want to review every public use before it goes live."* The envelope is stored with the story and travels with it everywhere.

3. **Centre integration.** The story enters the Centre as a structured record — connected to Mountie Yarns the organisation, to the funding flows that support the program, to the LGA, to the relevant Hansard mentions, to the evidence rating of the program model. It is **searchable but consent-bound** — anyone querying it sees only what the envelope permits, and any usage is logged.

4. **Cross-community resonance.** When a worker at Oonchiumpa is documenting their own model in Year 2, the Centre surfaces the Mountie Yarns story as a relevant pattern — *"this trajectory matches three others in your network"*. Workers can learn from each other without extracting from each other.

5. **Artifact use.** In Year 3, the documentary director and the book editor each request use of the story. The young person — now 18 — reviews each request. They approve the documentary use (they want their mum to see it on a cinema screen) and decline the book use (they don't want it sitting on a shelf in a library forever). Both requests are honoured automatically by the system. The decline is not a problem. The story finds its right home.

6. **Royalty and recognition.** When the book sells and the documentary screens, royalty share flows to Mountie Yarns and to the young person directly through the consent envelope's payment hooks. The story is not extracted. It is hosted.

**Without this layer**, stories either stay in country (and the political case is never made) or get extracted (and the harm is repeated). **With this layer**, stories become the most powerful evidence in the program, on terms the storytellers set.

The Empathy Ledger v2 sovereignty layer already exists and is in production. **Year 1's $180k storytelling team line is the people who do this work alongside the communities.**

## Example 4 — Three years inside one community: Oonchiumpa, Alice Springs

The most concrete way to see the program is to walk through what happens for one of the ten over three years. Oonchiumpa is the right example because the relationship is the deepest, the model is the clearest, and Tuesday next week is Day Zero.

### Year 1 — Foundation (FY2026)

- **Q2 2026:** $35,000 untied transfer to Oonchiumpa. No project plan required. Quarterly relationship visit replaces acquittal.
- **Q2 2026:** Embedded storyteller (North-based) begins six-month placement. First documentation: the visit by 55 judges and magistrates. Cultural protocols set with Kristy Bloomfield, Tanya Turner and the elders (Aunty Bev, Uncle Terry).
- **Q3 2026:** Oonchiumpa's hub-and-spoke youth justice model is documented inside the Centre — the formal structure *and* the daily elder governance that funders typically miss. Communication infrastructure built: a JusticeHub presence, a simple media kit, a referral page that magistrates can find.
- **Q3 2026:** First story batch consented and entered into the Empathy Ledger with full sovereignty envelopes. Three young people. Trajectories begin to be tracked through the workers, not through interviews.
- **Q4 2026:** Ben + 3 community leaders from across the ten travel to Norway and Portugal on the international learning tour. Christine joins for the Portugal leg. Returns with first international peer correspondence in place.
- **Year 1 outcome:** Oonchiumpa is visible nationally for the first time. Their model exists in writing on their own terms. Three young people's trajectories are being held longitudinally. They have relationships with two other anchor communities. The magistrate visit is on film.

### Year 2 — Deepening (FY2027)

- **Q1 2027:** Operational support steps up to $50,000. Oonchiumpa uses part of it to bring in a part-time finance officer — the kind of unsexy capacity that makes the difference between an organisation that can grow and one that can't.
- **Q2 2027:** **First cross-community exchange.** Eight Oonchiumpa staff travel to Palm Island Community Company for a week. Lateral violence work is shared in both directions. Documented in the Centre as a relational impact case study. (This is the South East Queensland tour Christine and I have already discussed.)
- **Q3 2027:** Oonchiumpa's model is tested against the Frameworks Institute language work being developed with the Victorian VRU. Christine and Andrea Davidson are introduced through the program. The framework starts being *contextualised* to Indigenous experience rather than imported.
- **Q4 2027:** Oonchiumpa is invited to speak at three sector forums where they would not previously have been on the program. Their communication infrastructure (built in Year 1) means they have media assets ready. Their story is no longer being told *about* them.
- **Year 2 outcome:** Oonchiumpa is in active relationship with two other anchor communities. They are being cited in Victorian framework work. Their longitudinal story tracking now covers eight young people across two years. They have a finance officer and a website that does their model justice.

### Year 3 — Crescendo (FY2028)

- **Q1 2028:** Oonchiumpa hosts an international peer (Portuguese youth diversion practitioner) for two weeks. Documented in the Centre, filmed for the documentary.
- **Q2 2028:** Christine speaks at the Doing Convening — not as a panellist, as a convening lead. She runs a workshop on hub-and-spoke models for 40 other community organisations. No keynotes. Nick and I are in the back of the room.
- **Q3 2028:** Oonchiumpa is one of ten chapters in *The Country We Are Building*. The chapter is co-written with Christine and the elders. They retain rights and a royalty share.
- **Q3 2028:** The eight tracked young people, now followed across three years, become the most concrete trajectory evidence in the documentary. With consent envelopes preserved throughout. Their stories carry weight because they are not snapshots — they are arcs.
- **Q4 2028:** The framework, now contextualised, is published with Oonchiumpa's model named as one of the three proof points. The Victorian VRU adopts it. The Justice Reform Initiative uses it in its advocacy. The political cover for detention default in the NT narrows by a measurable amount.
- **Year 3 outcome:** Oonchiumpa is a national voice. The model is documented, tested, framework-named, internationally connected, on film and in print. The eight young people have been held in trust through three years of trajectory and their stories are the most powerful evidence the program has produced. **The relationship continues after the program ends, because it was never structured as a project.**

## What success looks like by FY2028

- **One queryable system** holds every framework, grant, evaluation, story and organisational relationship in Australian youth justice. Open, owned by no government, used daily by magistrates, funders, journalists and communities.
- **Ten community organisations** are nationally visible, financially stable, in deep relationship with each other, and speaking for themselves.
- **A documentary, a book and a platform** — *The Country We Are Building* — exist as a single national artifact celebrating Indigenous-led and place-based models.
- **The frameworks work** is grounded in real case studies, contextualised, and published as Australian rather than imported.
- **The Justice Reform Initiative** has the place-based grounding it currently lacks; factional tensions become less consequential.
- **The political cover** for detention-default has narrowed. ACT, Tasmania and South Australia have a credible, evidenced, story-led case for community-based alternatives that the public, the bench and Treasury cannot ignore.
- **The young people** at the centre of all of this have been tracked, trusted and represented across multiple years — not interviewed once and forgotten.
