# Spain Diagrama Trip Reflection

<p>As I reflect on my 5-day experience visiting the Diagrama youth detention centers in Spain, guided by David, the CEO of Diagrama, I am filled with a mixture of emotions and a strong sense of conviction. The stark contrast between what I witnessed in these centers and what I have experienced in Australian youth detention centers is both eye-opening and deeply troubling.</p>

<figure><img src="/images/articles/spain-diagrama-trip-reflection-2.jpeg" alt="Young people and educators at a Diagrama centre in Spain" /><figcaption>Young people and educators at a Diagrama centre in Spain</figcaption></figure>

<p>In Australia, every visit to a youth detention center leaves me feeling depleted and haunted by the experiences of the young people there. The oppressive atmosphere, the lack of compassion and engagement between guards, educators, and young people, and the overall punitive approach all contribute to a sense of hopelessness and despair. It's clear that the current system, built on an English legacy and influenced by the US model of punishment over support and understanding, is failing our young people and our society as a whole.</p>

<p>However, my experience in Spain has shown me that there is another way. From the moment I stepped into each Diagrama center, I was struck by the positive energy, the laughter, and the strong relationships between the young people and their educators. It was evident that the young people were not just being punished, but were being given the opportunity to build resilience, develop new skills, and work towards a brighter future. The Diagrama staff had high expectations for these young people and treated them with respect, compassion, and a genuine belief in their potential.</p>

<figure><img src="/images/articles/spain-diagrama-trip-reflection-3.jpeg" alt="Inside a Diagrama youth detention facility" /><figcaption>Inside a Diagrama youth detention facility</figcaption></figure>

<p>One of the most powerful moments of my visit was sitting down and talking with the young people themselves. As we discussed the conditions in Australian youth detention centers, they struggled to understand how such a punitive and dehumanizing approach could be considered acceptable or effective. They spoke about their own hopes and dreams, and the trust they had in their educators to support them on their journey. It was clear that they viewed their time in detention not as a punishment, but as an opportunity for growth and transformation.</p>

<figure><img src="/images/articles/diagrama-youth-justice-spain-1-e034920e.jpg" alt="Diagrama youth justice centre in Spain" /><figcaption>A Diagrama re-education centre — mentors, not guards</figcaption></figure>

<p>The interviews and analysis of the Diagrama model only reinforce my belief that this approach can and must be implemented in Australia. The quantitative data, such as the significantly lower recidivism rates in Diagrama centers compared to Australian youth detention centers, provide compelling evidence of the model's effectiveness. The stories and experiences shared by the young people, educators, and staff offer powerful examples of how this approach can transform lives and communities.</p>

<figure><img src="/images/articles/spain-diagrama-trip-reflection-4.jpeg" alt="Diagrama centre grounds and facilities" /><figcaption>Diagrama centre grounds and facilities</figcaption></figure>

<h2>The Numbers That Should Change Everything</h2>

<p>The data makes the case undeniable. Australia spends <strong>$1.1 million per child, per year</strong> to lock young people up — and achieves an <strong>84.5% reoffending rate</strong>. Diagrama spends roughly <strong>A$31,000 per young person per year</strong> and achieves a <strong>13.6% reoffending rate</strong>. That's not a marginal improvement. That's a completely different paradigm.</p>

<p>Every euro invested in the Diagrama model returns <strong>€5.64 in social value</strong>. Every young person completes their program. No young person has died by suicide in a Diagrama facility. These aren't aspirational targets — they're 35 years of proven results.</p>

<h2>Why CONTAINED Has a Diagrama Room</h2>

<p>This trip is the reason Room 2 of the <a href="/contained/experience">CONTAINED experience</a> is dedicated entirely to Diagrama. The room asks one question: <em>"What if we spent $1.55 million per child on healing instead of harm?"</em></p>

<p>Spain already answered that question. They've been answering it for 35 years. The Diagrama Foundation, founded in 1991, has operated youth re-education centres across Spain using a model built on one radical premise: that young people in conflict with the law are still young people first. They need mentors, not guards. Education, not isolation. Belief in their future, not punishment for their past.</p>

<p>When visitors walk through Room 2 of CONTAINED, they experience the contrast physically — the cold concrete of Australian detention on one side, and the warmth of a Diagrama centre on the other. Photos from this very trip line the walls. The data is projected overhead. The question hangs in the air: <em>why aren't we doing this?</em></p>

<h2>The Opportunity for Australia</h2>

<p>This isn't theoretical. The pathway already exists.</p>

<p>In 2020, <strong>Danila Dilba Health Service</strong> — one of the Northern Territory's leading Aboriginal community-controlled health organisations — partnered with Diagrama to produce the <em>Blueprint for Change NT</em>, an adaptation report specifically designed for the Australian context. The report maps how the Diagrama model could be adapted to work within Indigenous communities, respecting cultural authority while drawing on 35 years of proven methodology.</p>

<p><strong>Diagrama Australia</strong> already exists as an entity (<a href="https://www.diagramaaustralia.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">diagramaaustralia.org</a>). The International Juvenile Justice Observatory (IJJO), which Diagrama founded in Brussels in 2002, provides the research and policy framework. The pieces are all there.</p>

<p>What's missing is political will and public pressure. That's exactly what CONTAINED is designed to create.</p>

<h2>What Needs to Happen Next</h2>

<p>The conversation between Diagrama and Australia needs to move from inspiration to implementation. Here's what that looks like:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>A formal delegation</strong> — Australian state and territory youth justice ministers need to see Diagrama centres firsthand, as I did. Not a briefing paper. Not a webinar. Walk the halls. Talk to the young people. Feel the difference.</li>
<li><strong>The Blueprint for Change</strong> — the NT adaptation report needs to be picked up, funded, and expanded to other jurisdictions. Queensland and Western Australia, where Indigenous young people are most overrepresented, should be next.</li>
<li><strong>Pilot programs</strong> — start with one community-controlled facility operating on Diagrama principles. Measure everything. Let the data speak.</li>
<li><strong>Connect with David McGuire</strong> at <strong>Diagrama Foundation UK</strong>, who bridges the Spanish model with English-speaking contexts and can facilitate introductions to the senior leadership team in Spain.</li>
</ol>

<h2>A Future Worth Fighting For</h2>

<p>Implementing the Diagrama model in Australia will undoubtedly require a significant shift in mindset, policy, and practice. It will require us to confront and challenge the deeply ingrained beliefs and systems that have perpetuated the current harmful approach to youth justice. However, the potential benefits are too great to ignore. By embracing a model that prioritizes education, rehabilitation, and positive relationships, we can create a youth justice system that not only reduces recidivism and enhances community safety but also helps young people to heal, grow, and thrive.</p>

<p>As I reflect on my experience in Spain, I am filled with a sense of hope and determination. I know that change is possible, and that the Diagrama model offers a roadmap for how we can achieve it. By sharing my experiences and the insights gained from this visit, I hope to inspire others to join me in advocating for a more compassionate, effective, and just approach to youth justice in Australia.</p>

<p>We owe it to our young people, our communities, and our society as a whole to do better. The time for change is now, and the Diagrama model shows us that a brighter, more hopeful future is within reach. Together, we can create a youth justice system that reflects our values, honours the potential of every young person, and helps to build a stronger, more resilient, and more equitable society for all.</p>
