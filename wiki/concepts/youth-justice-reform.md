# Youth Justice Reform

> ACT's thesis on why Australia's youth justice system fails, what actually works, and how to shift the system — synthesised across evidence, policy submissions, and field experience.

**Status:** Active | **Type:** Concept

## Overview

Australia spends more than a billion dollars a year locking up children. The Productivity Commission puts the average cost at $1.3 million per child per year — over $3,600 a day. In Victoria it is $2.6 million. The system's "success rate" is 15.5%: 84.5% of young people reoffend within 12 months of release.

This is not a disputed finding. Every inquiry has said it. Every report confirms it. The question — ACT's animating question across [[justicehub|JusticeHub]], [[contained|CONTAINED]], and the satellite programs it supports — is not whether the approach is broken. The question is what you do about it.

ACT's answer is grounded in the evidence: **the cure already exists**. Community-led alternatives operate at 95% lower cost than detention and achieve 78% success rates. Programs like [[oonchiumpa|Oonchiumpa]] in Alice Springs run entire operations — cultural mentorship, on-Country camps, school re-engagement, justice navigation, family support — at 97.6% less cost than one child in detention. The problem is not knowledge. It is political will and funding allocation.

## The Costs and the Gap

The numbers that power ACT's advocacy are drawn from JusticeHub's tracking of 145,000+ justice funding records:

| Approach | Annual Cost per Child | Success Rate |
|----------|-----------------------|-------------|
| Youth detention (national avg) | $1.1–1.3 million | ~15% |
| Community-led alternatives | ~$58,000 | ~78% |
| Diagrama Foundation (Spain) | ~$31,000 | 86.4% |

Queensland spends over $760,000 per youth annually in detention. The state has simultaneously committed $225 million (Staying on Track and Regional Reset programs) to rehabilitation and early intervention — but the allocation between grassroots organisations and large administrative bureaucracies remains a critical pressure point. Small community organisations, which are most effective, risk being passed over for established agencies whose administrative overhead consumes a significant share of grant funds before they reach young people.

## Who Is Most Affected

Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander young people are dramatically overrepresented. They comprise roughly 60% of those in detention despite being approximately 6% of the youth population. First Nations children are 21 times more likely to end up inside than their non-Indigenous peers (some analyses put this at 24 times). The 1991 Royal Commission into Aboriginal Deaths in Custody made 339 recommendations; most remain unimplemented.

This overrepresentation is not incidental — it is the direct and ongoing consequence of colonial dispossession, the removal of cultural authority, and the deliberate underfunding of community-controlled responses in favour of state enforcement mechanisms.

## The "Cure Already Exists" Argument

The most direct statement of ACT's thesis: the solutions are not waiting to be invented. They are already running, often on volunteer hours and short grant cycles, in communities across Australia.

**[[oonchiumpa|Oonchiumpa]]** — Alice Springs, 100% Aboriginal-led (Kristy Bloomfield and Tanya Turner). When NT Police ran Operation Luna targeting 21 high-risk young people, Oonchiumpa worked with them; by December, only one remained on the list. 95% diversion success. 72% of disengaged youth returned to education. Working with 32 partner organisations across Central Australia.

**PICC (Palm Island Community Company)** — 197 staff, 78% diversion rate. Philosophy: culture is protection. When young people know who they are, where they come from, and who their family is, they make different choices.

**Mounty Yarns** — Mount Druitt, Western Sydney. Youth-led storytelling and media. 30% of participants pursuing media careers. Young people building their own platforms rather than waiting for someone to hand them one.

**[[campfire|CAMPFIRE]]** — Mount Isa. Cultural immersion, fitness, and mentorship led by Brodie Germaine. Documented reduction in youth police contact. Operating in the same geography as some of Australia's worst watchhouse overflow.

**[[confit-pathways|ConFit Pathways]]** — Fitness and boxing-based program using mentors with lived incarceration experience. 60% reduction in recidivism among participants.

**[[diagrama|Diagrama Foundation]]** — Spain. Recidivism rate 13.6%. Cost ~$31,000 per young person per year. Staff called "social educators", not guards. A woman named Carmen has worked there for fifteen years; she knows every young person by name, by story, by the thing that keeps them up at night. This model has been running since 1991. It is not a theory. It exists.

## Gold-Standard Program Design

ACT has articulated what a genuinely effective youth justice intervention looks like — drawn from evidence synthesis and the Queensland Kickstarter grants context:

**Evidence-based strategies** — built on approaches proven to reduce offending or its risk factors, including family therapy (which has cut reoffending by 25–50%), cognitive-behavioural techniques for decision-making, and school re-engagement.

**Holistic and multi-faceted** — no single issue leads a young person to crime; no single intervention pulls them out. Gold-standard programs combine family engagement, education support, mentoring, mental health services, and positive recreational outlets.

**Early and targeted** — using data-driven risk assessment to focus intensive support on highest-risk young people at critical turning points (school transitions, first minor offence, family breakdown).

**Intensive and sustained** — not one-off workshops. International best practice shows results come from daily or weekly contact, individualised to each young person, sustained over months or years. Better to thoroughly transform 100 high-risk young people than to briefly touch 1,000 with no lasting change.

**Youth-centred and culturally informed** — for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander youth, this means working with Elders and community leaders, centering cultural identity, and recognising that community-controlled responses consistently outperform externally designed programs on every measurable outcome.

**Co-designed** — local non-profits and youth workers know what resonates. Involving young people with lived experience in program design is not a courtesy; it is how you avoid designing programs for the wrong problem.

## Why the System Persists

The youth justice system is not irrational from the perspective of those who benefit from it. Political incentives run toward "tough on crime" policies that are legible to voters shaped by media sensationalism. The narrative that punishment equals safety has deep cultural purchase even where the evidence clearly contradicts it.

The system is also self-reinforcing: high staff-to-budget ratios in detention facilities create institutional constituencies for their own continuation; procurement processes favour large established agencies over grassroots community organisations; and measuring "success" by detention rates rather than community health makes invisible the programs that actually work.

ACT's framing, drawing on Plato's allegory of the cave, argues that most Australians are looking at shadows: "youth crime waves," "tough on crime" policies, dehumanising narratives that reduce young people to their worst moments. [[contained|CONTAINED]] is the mechanism for turning people away from the wall — making the real cost of the current system felt in the body, then showing what exists beyond it.

## The Queensland Context

Queensland has been the focus of significant ACT advocacy activity. Key pressure points:

- Queensland's recidivism rate among released young people is 84–96% within 12 months — among the worst in Australia
- The state spends over $760,000 per youth annually in detention
- $100 million was committed to "gold standard early intervention" without adequate design criteria for what counts as gold standard
- The Making Queensland Safer Bill 2024 presented an opportunity for fundamental reform toward rehabilitation; ACT made a formal submission arguing for adaptation of the Diagrama model, a dedicated 12-month post-detention transition program, a Youth Justice Co-Lab, and refocusing resources from new detention infrastructure toward community-based programs

The submission explicitly named [[campfire|CAMPFIRE]] (BG Fit, Mount Isa) and the Stradbroke Islander Justice Reinvestment program as models worth replicating — programs already working that need funding to scale, not programs that need to be invented.

## The Role of JusticeHub

[[justicehub|JusticeHub]] is ACT's intelligence platform underpinning this reform thesis. It has mapped more than 800 verified interventions, organisations, and programs across every state and territory. It tracks where public money goes — more than $1 billion in justice spending, line by line. It shows the gap between what governments spend on detention and what they invest in what actually works. All of it public. All of it sourced.

JusticeHub makes the argument that communities already have the answers. The data layer — 145,242 justice funding records, 1,357 evidence-based interventions in ALMA, 57,435 crime statistics records — is the empirical foundation for everything CONTAINED campaigns for.

## The Role of CONTAINED

[[contained|CONTAINED]] is the campaign layer: an immersive shipping-container experience that translates these numbers into felt experience. Three rooms, thirty minutes:

- **Room 1** — what the current system does to a child. You sit in it.
- **Room 2** — what works instead. The Diagrama Foundation. The proof.
- **Room 3** — the community already doing it, in whatever city the container has arrived in.

The chalk question wall: *"What could $1.55M do instead?"* Communities write their own answers. It photographs. It travels. It shifts the conversation in rooms that matter.
