# How does the Tractorpedia second-brain pattern compound knowledge over time?

> Synthesised from the wiki on 2026-04-07, enriched 2026-04-13. This article was generated by a query and saved back into Tractorpedia so the next question can build on top of it.

## Answer

Tractorpedia is built on the [[llm-knowledge-base|Karpathy second-brain pattern]]: raw sources land in `wiki/raw/`, get summarised into `wiki/sources/`, feed into entity articles in `wiki/projects/`, `wiki/people/`, `wiki/concepts/`, and most importantly questions feed back as `wiki/synthesis/` articles. The compounding loop: each query that produces a non-trivial answer becomes a permanent synthesis page that the next query can build on. After 100 questions, the 101st has 100 answers worth of context to draw on.

## The Four-Layer Model

The pattern operates in four layers, each feeding the next:

1. **Capture.** Raw material enters the system: transcripts, exports, scraped documents, meeting notes, financial snapshots. These land in `wiki/raw/` and are immutable. The system never modifies its sources.

2. **Compile.** An LLM reads raw material and produces durable wiki articles: project pages, concept definitions, people profiles, decision records. Each article is named, classified, and linked. This is where ACT decides what something *is*.

3. **Synthesize.** Questions asked of the wiki produce answers. Non-trivial answers get saved as `wiki/synthesis/` pages. Each synthesis article becomes part of the context available to the next question. This is the compounding layer.

4. **Publish.** Compiled knowledge and synthesis articles flow downstream through the [[act-knowledge-ops-loop|ACT Knowledge Ops Loop]] into public surfaces: the ACT website, Tractorpedia viewer, and project-specific sites. The websites compose from the wiki rather than inventing their own truth.

## How Synthesis Compounds

The key insight is that synthesis articles are not ephemeral query results. They are permanent wiki pages that future queries can reference. This creates an editorial flywheel:

- A question produces an artifact (this page, for instance).
- The artifact enriches the context available to the next question.
- Better context produces richer answers, which produce richer artifacts.
- The cost of answering each successive question falls, because more of the groundwork already exists.

After a year of operation, a question like "what is ACT's relationship with Palm Island?" does not start from scratch. It draws on existing synthesis pages about [[picc|Palm Island Community Company]], the [[../concepts/third-reality|Third Reality]] methodology, community archive patterns, and sovereign data principles. The answer is deeper and faster than it could have been at month one, and it in turn becomes a reference for the next grant application or board paper that touches the same topic.

## Concrete Example

Suppose a grant application asks ACT to describe its knowledge management approach. Without the compounding pattern, someone would need to explain the wiki, the ops loop, the relationship between Empathy Ledger and the knowledge base, and how synthesis works, all from memory.

With the pattern, the grant writer (human or agent) can reference this page, the [[llm-knowledge-base|LLM Knowledge Base]] concept page, and the [[act-knowledge-ops-loop|ACT Knowledge Ops Loop]] technical page. Each claim is traceable to a source article. The grant narrative writes itself from compiled knowledge rather than being invented fresh.

## What Makes It Different from RAG

Traditional retrieval-augmented generation re-derives knowledge on every query. It searches, retrieves chunks, and synthesizes an answer that evaporates after the conversation ends. The Karpathy pattern inverts this: the wiki is the persistent synthesis layer. Queries leave behind durable artifacts. The system gets smarter over time instead of resetting to zero.
