## The Living Organism: CivicGraph as Art

### What it is

Not a dashboard. Not an analytics platform. A living data organism that breathes, drifts, clusters, and reveals. An artwork made from 587,000 Australian civic entities and the 1.5 million relationships between them, rendered as something you can stand inside, explore, print on a wall, or hold in your hand.

The organism has three states:

**The Network** — the full entity graph. Charities, foundations, government bodies, companies, political donors, procurement recipients. Connected by money, governance, geography, and power. Rendered as a force-directed galaxy where related entities drift together and clusters form organically — not sorted into neat categories but finding their own gravity.

**The Map** — 873 funding deserts. Dark voids where disadvantage is high and funding is low. Not a choropleth with traffic-light colours but an inversion: the deserts are the subject. Funded areas glow. Deserts are absence. The viewer sees what isn't there.

**The Flow** — money in motion. Contracts flowing from government to recipients. Donations flowing from donors to parties. Grants flowing from foundations to communities. Hansard promises floating above, connected to purchase orders (or not) below. The gap between a press release and a funded program, animated.

### The tech stack

**Scale problem:** 587K entities and 1.5M edges. Most JS graph libraries cap at 10-50K. The answer is GPU-accelerated rendering.

| Layer | Tool | Why |
|-------|------|-----|
| Graph engine | Graphology | Graph operations, community detection, centrality metrics. Pre-compute server-side. |
| Full network renderer | Cosmos.gl | GPU-powered force layout. Demonstrated at 1M+ nodes in-browser. The only library that genuinely handles ACT's scale. |
| Curated sub-views | Sigma.js v3 | WebGL renderer, mature plugin ecosystem. For focused views of 10-50K node subgraphs (justice sector, foundation network, etc.) |
| Geographic layer | deck.gl + Mapbox GL JS | Funding deserts, contract flows by location, dot-density maps. GPU-powered, handles millions of points. |
| Narrative / scrollytelling | Svelte + Scrollama + D3 | 6-episode story. Each episode triggers transitions on a sticky vis. Proven in data journalism. |
| Organic / generative layer | p5.js | Perlin noise for breathing motion. Boids algorithm for organic clustering. The \"always slightly wrong\" principle — imperfection reads as alive. |
| Data aesthetics (Ikeda style) | Three.js + GLSL shaders | Custom fragment shaders for light grids, scan lines, particle fields. Full control over the visual language. |
| Physical output | p5-svg → AxiDraw plotter | Genuine pen-drawn artifacts from data. SVG export for large-format prints. Risograph colour separation. |
| AI style transfer | ControlNet img2img | Programmatic render as control input, Lombardi/hand-drawn style applied as post-processing. AI adds texture, never invents data. |

### The 6 episodes as visual installations

Each episode from The Aesthetics of Asymmetry has a specific visual treatment:

**Episode 1: The Keyhole**
A split view. On the left: what a community org sees when applying for a grant (a form, a deadline, a word limit). On the right: the full Cosmos.gl graph showing every entity in their sector, every competing applicant, every funder's portfolio. The keyhole is literal — a circular mask that constrains one side. Scroll to widen the aperture.

**Episode 2: Resolution**
Start with the full graph at maximum blur (Gaussian shader). As you scroll, resolution increases. Names appear. Amounts sharpen. At full resolution, it becomes uncomfortable — you can see individual donation-to-contract chains. The viewer chooses how deep to look. Steyerl's resolution politics, made interactive.

**Episode 3: The Missing Folder**
The map of 873 funding deserts — but rendered as absence. The funded areas are warm particle clouds. The deserts are black. Scroll triggers reveal what is NOT collected: care labour, kinship networks, informal economy, cultural governance. Each absence appears as a label in the void, like Onuoha's empty folders.

**Episode 4: The Promise and the Purchase Order**
Hansard quotes float at the top of the screen. As you scroll, money flows (or doesn't) connect downward to actual funded programs. Where a promise produced a purchase order, a line connects. Where it didn't, the quote drifts and fades. The gap between rhetoric and reality, animated.

**Episode 5: The Alibi**
Flood the screen with data. Everything at once. Every entity, every relationship, every dollar. The Elahi inversion — if you demand transparency, here is all of it. The viewer drowns. Then: a filter appears. Choose a postcode. Choose a sector. The organism contracts to show only what matters to you. From chaos to clarity — but you had to ask for it.

**Episode 6: CARE**
The final episode inverts the gaze. Instead of \"what does the system look like?\" — \"who owns the story?\" Empathy Ledger storytellers appear as points of light within the network. Their stories are the only nodes with voice (audio playback). The surrounding data is context, not subject. The sovereign narrative is the centre. OCAP principles rendered as architecture.

### Making it feel alive

The organism breathes through:
- **Perlin noise displacement** on node positions — everything gently drifts, nothing is pinned
- **Variable opacity based on data freshness** — recently updated entities are brighter, stale ones fade
- **Organic clustering via boids algorithm** — entities that share attributes flock together without rigid categories
- **Time-based colour temperature** — entities shift warm-to-cool based on when their data was last refreshed
- **Heartbeat pulse** on entities with active Empathy Ledger stories — a subtle throb that draws the eye

### Physical artifacts

Every episode produces a printable artifact:
- Episode 1: A literal keyhole-shaped poster. Full graph visible through the hole.
- Episode 2: A gradient print from blur to sharp. Plotter-drawn at AxiDraw resolution.
- Episode 3: A set of empty folders (Onuoha tribute) labelled with what's missing.
- Episode 4: A two-column broadsheet. Left: Hansard quotes. Right: funded (or unfunded) programs.
- Episode 5: A fold-out poster. Closed: chaos. Open: your local data.
- Episode 6: A postcard with a QR code. Scan to hear a storyteller.

### The image generation pipeline

For exhibition-quality pieces:
1. Generate accurate network/map/flow programmatically (Cosmos, Sigma, deck.gl)
2. Export as high-res PNG or SVG
3. Feed through ControlNet with Canny edge detection as control input
4. Prompt: \"hand-drawn investigation board, string and pin, paper texture, Lombardi narrative structure\"
5. The AI adds aesthetic texture while preserving the real data topology
6. Print at exhibition scale (A0+)

Gemini 2.5 with code execution can also generate p5.js sketches from data descriptions — useful for rapid prototyping of generative treatments before committing to full production.

### What this is really about

The organism is not a tool for analysing data. It is an artwork that happens to be made from real data. The difference matters.

A tool says: here is what the data shows.
An artwork says: here is what it feels like to see it.

The 873 funding deserts are not interesting because the number is 873. They are interesting because 873 communities wake up every morning in a system designed to be invisible to them. The organism makes the system visible — not as a dashboard, but as a felt experience.

That is the Third Reality rendered as art. Systemic data + narrative sovereignty = a shared truth you stand inside and can't unsee.
