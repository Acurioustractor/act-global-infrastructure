# How should ACT apply the Karpathy loop to JusticeHub, the Minderoo pitch, and The Brave Ones?

> Synthesised from the wiki on 2026-04-21. This article was generated by a query and saved back into Tractorpedia so the next question can build on top of it.

## Answer

## The short answer

ACT should not try to apply the Karpathy loop to "youth justice" in the abstract. It should apply it to the narrow internal surfaces that shape how JusticeHub learns, routes, drafts, and follows up.

That means separating the constellation into three classes:

1. **Non-negotiables** — community selection, cultural protocol, consent, cost claims, and source truth. These stay human-held and versioned. They are not auto-optimised.
2. **Harnesses** — prompts, routing logic, draft assembly, card order, follow-up sequences, and verification rubrics. These are the editable surfaces.
3. **Traces** — portraits, voice fragments, QR searches, judge reflections, reply emails, meeting notes, and field observations. These are the fuel.

The loop works when ACT lets agents improve the harnesses against good traces, while refusing to let the metric eat the human work.

## What the wiki already has

The important thing is that ACT is not starting from zero. The wiki already contains two loops that rhyme with the Karpathy pattern.

First, the [[act-knowledge-ops-loop|ACT Knowledge Ops Loop]] already formalises a constrained rhythm: capture, compile, lint, sync, publish, learn. That is already an edit-run-check loop for knowledge. Second, JusticeHub already carries the same field logic in its "Witness · Document · Verify · Publish" claim: notice something that works, write it down properly, validate it with the right authority, then publish it back into the system.

So the strategic move is not to invent a new philosophy. It is to become stricter about which surfaces are editable, which metrics are legitimate, and which traces get preserved.

## The JusticeHub triplets

The safest way to operationalise this is to define one Karpathy triplet per loop: one editable surface, one metric, one time budget.

### 1. JusticeHub program-routing loop

**Editable surface:** the search and routing harness — query rewriting, ranking logic, filters, follow-up prompts, and the wording around "no local option found".

**Metric:** did the user reach a relevant next step? Not raw clicks. Better metrics are successful service discovery, human verification that the result was actually useful, and the percentage of searches that surface a real gap signal instead of a dead end.

**Time budget:** nightly or weekly in a sandbox, never live-editing the production experience without review.

This is the cleanest first loop because it is operational, bounded, and measurable.

### 2. Minderoo narrative loop

**Editable surface:** the draft harness for the pitch — claim order, opening sequence, evidence-selection rubric, follow-up cadence, and the email/call structure around the ask.

**Metric:** qualified progress, not vanity. The right metric is not "did Lucy open the email". It is "did the conversation move to the next concrete step" — reply, meeting booked, request for supporting artefacts, or internal forwarding.

**Time budget:** one send-review-rewrite cycle per week. Slow enough for judgment, fast enough to compound.

This matters because the pitch should become sharper every time ACT sees where a funder leans in, hesitates, or asks for proof.

### 3. Judges on Country loop

**Editable surface:** the postcard sequence, QR handoff flow, room order, and post-experience reflection prompts.

**Metric:** scans, time on the JusticeHub destination, follow-up requests, and the quality of post-experience reflections captured with consent.

**Time budget:** one iteration per event or cohort, not continuous optimisation during a live experience.

This is where JusticeHub becomes a local hard takeoff in the useful sense: each round makes the next round more legible without changing the core ethic of the room.

### 4. The Brave Ones loop

The Brave Ones is not the thing to auto-optimise. The portraits themselves are not a benchmark.

**Editable surface:** the exhibition sequencing, caption scaffolds, companion text, invitation flow, and the way portraits connect into JusticeHub and Empathy Ledger.

**Metric:** subject approval, quality of consented follow-up reflection, funder or judge response after standing in front of the work, and whether the portrait meaningfully improves the next conversation.

**Time budget:** one exhibition or publication cycle at a time.

This distinction matters. If JusticeHub optimises only on dashboards, it will drift toward sterile proof. The Brave Ones is the trace layer that keeps the system honest.

## Why The Brave Ones gets more important under this theory

The user transcript is right about one thing that most organisations will miss: traces matter as much as scores. JusticeHub already has plenty of quantitative traces — funding rows, program records, QR scans, search events. What it lacks, unless ACT protects it deliberately, is a durable qualitative trace layer that explains why something landed.

That is what [[the-brave-ones|The Brave Ones]] contributes.

The portraits, words, and follow-up reflections are not decorative brand assets. They are the high-value traces that stop the system from optimising for the wrong proxy. They let ACT ask not just "did the page convert?" but "what did the judge see?", "what made the funder pause?", and "what felt true enough to move money or practice?"

Without The Brave Ones, JusticeHub risks becoming a clever dashboard. With it, the system keeps a human face and a memory of what actually changed in the room.

## How the Minderoo pitch should absorb this without sounding like AI theatre

Minderoo should not be pitched on "self-improving agents". That is not the point, and it would likely cheapen the ask.

The right translation is simpler:

- ACT is building a **living library**, not a static report.
- Every use of the system produces new evidence, new stories, and new routing intelligence.
- The infrastructure gets better because the field keeps teaching it.
- Human judgment, community authority, and consent remain the gatekeepers.

In other words: **the library is the destination, and the loop is how the library stays alive.**

That line fits the existing Three Circles and Minderoo framing better than a direct AI argument ever would.

## The practical order of operations

If ACT wants to make the most of this theory in 2026, the order should be:

1. Start with the **Minderoo narrative loop**, because it is internal, measurable, and low-risk.
2. Instrument the **JusticeHub routing loop** so searches, dead ends, and successful handoffs are traceable.
3. Treat **The Brave Ones** as the qualitative trace infrastructure, not as a separate art flourish.
4. Only then experiment with more autonomous optimisation, because by then the traces, evals, and governance will exist.

That sequence matches the core lesson of the Karpathy loop: the power is not in agent cleverness. The power is in good constraints, fast feedback, and refusing to optimise the wrong thing.
