# How should ACT engage judges after the on-Country experience and what systems capture the learning?

## Answer

## Engaging Judges After the Experience

The 30 minutes on Country is not the end. It is the first touch in a longer relationship. The question is how to keep judges connected to the ecosystem without it feeling like a mailing list.

### The post-experience toolkit

Every judge leaves with:

1. **A postcard set.** The 6 cards they held during the experience, plus any extras. These sit on their desk. Physical reminder every time they look up from a court file.

2. **A JusticeHub login.** Not a generic "visit the website" ask. A saved search filtered to their jurisdiction showing programs they can actually refer to. Bookmarked on their phone during the session.

3. **A reflection prompt.** A single card: "Next time a young person stands in front of you — what will you ask that you didn't ask before?" No email follow-up. No survey. Just the question.

### Three engagement layers (light to deep)

**Layer 1 — Passive (all 55 judges)**
- JusticeHub sends a quarterly digest: new programs added in their jurisdiction, new stories published, gap analysis for their region
- The postcards do the ongoing work — every time they see the photo on their desk, they remember

**Layer 2 — Active (judges who want more)**
- Invite to the next Brave Ones portrait exhibition (when Phase 1 portraits are shot)
- Offer to host a 30-minute session at their court for colleagues — using the same card deck with local Program/Place cards
- Connect with their judicial education coordinator to propose integration into standard training

**Layer 3 — Champions (judges who want to lead)**
- Co-author a reflection piece for the STAY Series journal — their voice alongside young people's voices
- Speak at the next judicial education conference about the experience
- Become a reference for funders: "Ask Judge X what happened when they went to Country"
- Join an informal advisory circle that reviews JusticeHub's program taxonomy from a bench perspective

### What the system captures from each layer

| Layer | What we learn | Where it goes |
|-------|--------------|---------------|
| Passive | QR scan patterns, JusticeHub usage by region | CivicGraph gap analysis, program demand signals |
| Active | Which courts want local sessions, which jurisdictions have no alternatives | Narrative claims, grant evidence, policy advocacy |
| Champion | Judge reflections, changed referral patterns, institutional barriers | EL stories, STAY journal chapters, funder pitch material |

### How this feeds the R&D claim

Every interaction after the experience strengthens the R&D evidence:

- **Development of the card deck and QR system** — novel educational technology (Core R&D under ACT-EL)
- **JusticeHub program taxonomy informed by judicial feedback** — iterative platform development (Core R&D under ACT-JH)
- **Consent-layer refinements based on real deployment** — novel consent architecture (Core R&D under ACT-EL)
- **Measurement of judicial behaviour change** — social impact measurement methodology (Supporting R&D under ACT-IN)

The 43.5% offset applies to all of this. A single judge changing their diversion referral pattern saves the system $1.3M per child per year. The R&D cost to develop the experience is a fraction of that.

### What we don't do

- **No surveys.** Judges get enough surveys. The experience speaks for itself. If they want to share, they will.
- **No marketing emails.** The postcard on their desk is more powerful than any newsletter.
- **No "alumni network" branding.** This is a relationship, not a program. Traditional Owners lead the relationship, not ACT.
- **No published quotes without explicit consent.** A judge saying something powerful in the room does not mean they consented to it being on LinkedIn.

### The learning loop

After each round of Judges on Country:

1. **Debrief with Traditional Owners** — what landed, what didn't, what to change
2. **Review QR scan data** — which cards were scanned most, which programs explored, which gaps surfaced
3. **Update the card deck** — new stories, updated programs, refined decision prompts
4. **Write a synthesis** — /wiki synthesize "what did we learn from the April 2026 judges session?"
5. **Update narrative claims** — new variants, new evidence, deployment log
6. **Feed into The Brave Ones brief** — what visual register resonated most with judges? This informs Phase 1 portrait direction

Every round teaches us something. The synthesis layer captures it. The next round is better.

### Connection to the broader revenue thesis

Judges on Country is not a cost centre. It is a revenue multiplier:

- **R&D offset** — 43.5% back on all development costs
- **Grant evidence** — "55 judges went through it and here's what changed" is the strongest possible evidence for a Minderoo, Ian Potter, or Australia Council application
- **Platform adoption** — every judge using JusticeHub is a proof point for platform licensing
- **Media coverage** — "Traditional Owners taught 55 judges what a court file can't" is a headline that writes itself
- **Referral pipeline** — judges who use JusticeHub surface programs that need funding, which feeds GrantScope, which feeds grant applications

The $200K revenue target gets closer every time a judge picks up a postcard.
