# What are ACT's seven flagship experiences and how does each one convert into ecosystem growth?

> Synthesised from the wiki on 2026-04-13. This article was generated by a query and saved back into Tractorpedia so the next question can build on top of it.

## Answer

## Seven Experiences That Grow the Ecosystem

ACT doesn't market. It makes things people can't unsee. Each flagship experience serves a different audience, creates a different emotional response, and feeds a different part of the ecosystem. Together they form a complete growth engine that runs on word of mouth.

### 1. CONTAINED — the room that changes politicians

**What it is:** A shipping container retrofitted to simulate what youth detention feels like. 30 minutes. Three rooms. You stand where a child stands.

**Who it's for:** Politicians, public servants, funders, media, general public.

**The emotional move:** Moral shock. You walk in thinking you understand youth justice. You walk out knowing you didn't.

**What it feeds:**
- Media coverage (every journalist who enters writes about it)
- Political will (a minister who's been inside can't vote the same way)
- Touring revenue (venue partnerships, festival activations)
- The Brave Ones (portraits live inside the container's walls)
- Narrative claims (strongest confrontational-frame material)

### 2. Judges on Country — the decision that haunts

**What it is:** 30 minutes on Country in Mparntwe. Traditional Owners hand judges a photo and say: decide. Then reveal what they didn't know.

**Who it's for:** Judges, magistrates, judicial educators, law students.

**The emotional move:** Professional uncertainty. They realise they've been deciding without knowing who the person is.

**What it feeds:**
- Judicial referral patterns (more diversion, more community-based options)
- JusticeHub adoption (every judge with a bookmarked search is a user)
- Word of mouth along the bench (judges talk to judges)
- R&D evidence (novel judicial education methodology)
- Narrative claims (moral-frame material for grants and media)

### 3. The Brave Ones — the portrait you stand in front of

**What it is:** Studio-quality B&W portraits of young people who walked out the other side. Counter-mugshot. Dignity register. Life-size prints.

**Who it's for:** Gallery visitors, arts funders, cultural institutions, the general public.

**The emotional move:** Recognition. You see a person, not a statistic. The portrait demands you stay.

**What it feeds:**
- Gallery touring revenue (MCA, NGV, Powerhouse, parliament foyers)
- Arts funding pipeline (Australia Council, Creative Australia, philanthropic trusts)
- Empathy Ledger growth (every portrait = new storyteller, new consent, new story)
- Brave Ones book (Year 3 — publishable IP)
- Lead-funder naming opportunity ("The [Funder] Brave Ones Portrait Series")

### 4. The Harvest — the meal that grounds everything

**What it is:** Community meals at the farm in Witta. Food from the ground you're standing on. Shared tables. No PowerPoint.

**Who it's for:** Local community, neighbours, potential supporters, partner organisations.

**The emotional move:** Belonging. You eat together. You hear a story. You realise this isn't an organisation — it's a place.

**What it feeds:**
- CSA subscriptions and produce revenue
- Farm-gate event income
- Community trust (the neighbours become advocates)
- Empathy Ledger stories (meal conversations become narratives)
- R&D evidence for The Harvest and Farm projects

### 5. Goods on Country — the box that arrives

**What it is:** Physical products made by communities. Each box comes with a story from Empathy Ledger. You know the person who made what you're holding.

**Who it's for:** Conscious consumers, corporate gift buyers, partner organisations.

**The emotional move:** Connection. This isn't a product. It's a relationship with a place and a person.

**What it feeds:**
- Direct product revenue
- Supply chain data (IoT telemetry, marketplace development — R&D eligible)
- Community enterprise income (revenue flows to producers)
- Empathy Ledger stories (every producer is a storyteller)
- Corporate partnerships (branded Goods boxes as staff gifts)

### 6. Empathy Ledger stories — the voice you can't unhear

**What it is:** Real stories told by real people with real consent. Published on EL, syndicated to JusticeHub. Not marketing. Not case studies. Sovereign narratives.

**Who it's for:** Anyone who finds them — funders, researchers, journalists, community members, other organisations.

**The emotional move:** Intimacy. Laquisha saying "Oppression." Nigel saying "I'm panicking." These aren't pull quotes. They're diagnoses.

**What it feeds:**
- Grant evidence (the strongest material in any application)
- Media coverage (journalists quote real voices, not spokespeople)
- Platform proof (EL working at scale with consent = licensable)
- Narrative claims (every story is multiple claims)
- Academic citations (researchers reference EL stories)

### 7. Tractorpedia — the depth that earns trust

**What it is:** 205 articles of genuine knowledge. Searchable. Linked. Every claim sourced. Every project documented.

**Who it's for:** Funders doing due diligence, researchers, potential partners, other organisations, journalists.

**The emotional move:** Respect. "How does a small organisation know this much?" Trust through depth.

**What it feeds:**
- Grant success rate (the wiki IS the application backing)
- SEO and findability (funders google, wiki appears)
- Partnership credibility (organisations see the depth and want to collaborate)
- Platform licensing proof (the knowledge base demonstrates domain expertise)
- Internal clarity (the team knows what it knows)

### How they compound together

\`\`\`
CONTAINED → judge is moved → attends Judges on Country → sees Brave Ones portrait
→ scans QR → finds JusticeHub → reads EL story → shares with colleagues
→ colleague googles ACT → finds Tractorpedia → reads 205 articles
→ contacts ACT → receives Goods on Country box → attends Harvest meal
→ becomes champion → introduces funder → funder reads wiki → funds next round
\`\`\`

No single experience does everything. But each one creates a thread that pulls toward the next. The ecosystem grows not because ACT asks people to engage — but because each experience naturally leads to the next one.

### Revenue attribution by experience

| Experience | Revenue type | Estimated annual |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| CONTAINED | Venue partnerships, touring, activations | Variable |
| Judges on Country | R&D offset on development, grant evidence | Feeds \$109K offset |
| Brave Ones | Gallery revenue, arts grants, book sales (Y3) | Future |
| The Harvest | CSA, produce, farm-gate events | Growing |
| Goods on Country | Product sales, corporate gifts | Growing |
| EL stories | Grant success rate improvement | Indirect but significant |
| Tractorpedia | Grant backing, partnership conversion | Indirect but significant |

The direct revenue experiences (Harvest, Goods) are small today. The indirect ones (wiki backing grants, EL stories strengthening applications, Judges on Country earning the R&D offset) are already producing \$109K/year. The trajectory is toward \$200K through compound growth across all seven.
