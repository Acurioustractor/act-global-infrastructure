# Dianne Stokes

> Tennant Creek Elder whose cultural authority enters ACT's work not as branding, but as language, naming, and co-design.

## Overview

Dianne Stokes is an Elder from Tennant Creek whose public ACT footprint is most visible through [[goods-on-country|Goods on Country]]. The Empathy Ledger candidate queue identifies her as a featured storyteller with two public story records, Bugainuk cultural background, and Tennant Creek, Northern Territory as her location. (Source: `wiki/output/el-people-candidates-latest.json`)

In the current verified wiki, Dianne's clearest documented contribution is to the naming and cultural grounding of the industrial washing machine fleet.

## Goods on Country

[[goods-on-country|Goods on Country]] records that Elder Dianne Stokes named the washing machine in language, and the wider narrative layer goes further: she co-designed and named the Pakkimjalki Kari washing machine in Warumungu language. That contribution matters because it shows how ACT's product work becomes legitimate: through Elder authority, language, and place-based meaning, not just engineering.

Dianne's presence is therefore a concise proof of the ACT claim that products should be culturally grounded rather than merely deployed into community.

## Why She Matters in ACT

Dianne Stokes helps explain the difference between consultation and co-authorship. Her role in naming the washing machine makes the goods line legible as LCAA in practice: listening, co-design, action, and public narrative all held together through cultural authority.
