# Richard Cassidy

> First Nations leader bridging cultural knowledge and modern practice — Manbarra Operations, Palm Island.

## Overview

Richard Cassidy is a First Nations man deeply connected to his grandmother's country on Manbarra land at [[palm-island|Palm Island]]. He carries forward cultural obligations passed from his grandfather, serving as a bridge between traditional knowledge systems and contemporary organisational practice.

Richard leads Manbarra Operations, applying Indigenous governance frameworks to modern business and community development on Palm Island.

## Connection to Country

Richard's identity and purpose are inseparable from Country:

> "Country helps me be, and helps me be me, and helps me understand who I am. And it's out of that, from that base of where I land, that I can work out what my doing is going to be."

His grandfather's instruction anchors his work: "You're always gonna look after your grandmother's country."

## The "Our Story" Philosophy

Richard's approach to cross-cultural relationship building centres on shared narrative. Rather than positioning people as "first people" or "second people," he uses story as the vehicle for human connection:

> "Tell me your story and I'll tell you my story and then we'll try and figure out how my story and your story can become an our story."

This methodology — story as bridge — aligns with the [[lcaa-method|LCAA]] principle of Listen first. It rejects hierarchical framing in favour of mutual recognition through narrative exchange.

## Navigating Two Worlds

Richard describes the daily practice of holding both traditional and Western knowledge systems simultaneously:

> "I navigate those two worlds intuitively inside almost all day every day. So I've got this dominant culture side and I've got this cultural side. And one informs each other."

He uses the metaphor of a two-edged spear — the L-O-R-E (traditional law) and the L-A-W (Western legal systems) — arguing both are needed to "cut a path into this landscape."

## The Pebble Philosophy

A core teaching Richard shares about entering another's country:

> "Don't come to someone else's country as a rock or a boulder, but as a pebble, so that your footprint is small. But when you leave, that person says, 'Oh, that one came, your presence is great, and there's a heart. He came and didn't make a ripple. So he's welcome anytime.'"

When asked about his own legacy: "I want to be the smallest pebble I can be... my footprint would be that no one knew that I was here in the first place."

## Leading Manbarra Operations

Richard applies cultural governance frameworks to organisational development. He uses the concept of "hunting for particular fish" — finding people with specific expertise (finance, business, policy) while grounding the organisation in cultural authority.

He teaches his team: "You've got to navigate these two worlds. But for you to understand these two worlds, you've got to know your own world."

## Future Vision

For the land where he works, Richard envisions restoration: "We're planning to minimise that boulder and the impact of that rock and restore this country... the country is restoring itself and regenerating itself."

## ACT Connection

Richard's storytelling and governance practice was documented through [[empathy-ledger|Empathy Ledger]] field work on Palm Island. His "Our Story" philosophy directly informed ACT's approach to sovereign storytelling and the principle that communities already hold the knowledge — the role of technology is to amplify, not replace.

He is also one of the clearest figures explaining why [[picc|PICC]] matters beyond service delivery numbers. Richard makes visible the governance argument underneath community control: story, authority, and accountability have to be held together. That is why he appears in ACT's wider justice and sovereign-story field as more than a local voice. He helps explain the program logic.
