# Goods on Country

> Manufacturing durable household goods from 100% recycled plastic for remote and marginalised Australian communities — where conventional products fail within weeks.

**Status:** Active | **Code:** ACT-GD | **Tier:** Ecosystem

## What It Is

Goods on Country is ACT's circular economy social enterprise. It designs, manufactures, and deploys indestructible household goods for remote Aboriginal and marginalised communities across Australia, where extreme heat, overcrowding, dust, and limited repair infrastructure destroy standard products in weeks rather than years.

The enterprise operates through A Curious Tractor Pty Ltd (ABN: 21 591 780 066) and trades at goodsoncountry.com.au. It is not an Indigenous-owned organisation — it partners deeply with Indigenous-owned organisations (Oonchiumpa, Wilya Janta) and community bodies. Communities are co-designers, not recipients.

**Internal project code:** ACT-GD

## The Problem It Solves

In remote communities, families sleep on floors. Beds last weeks, not years. Appliances corrode, break, and go unreplaced. Standard consumer goods are not engineered for:
- Extreme heat and UV exposure
- High-density occupancy
- Dust and limited sealed flooring
- Lack of repair services or spare parts infrastructure

The resulting housing inadequacy causes exhaustion in children (affecting school outcomes), deteriorating health outcomes, and constant wasteful replacement cycles that drain community resources.

## Products

### Stretch Bed

The flagship product — co-designed with Elders across Central Australia, the Top End, Palm Island, and Kalgoorlie. Specifications:

- **Material:** 100% recycled HDPE plastic, UV-stabilised
- **Weight:** 26kg
- **Load capacity:** 200kg
- **Assembly:** Five minutes, tool-free
- **Cleaning:** Fully washable with a garden hose
- **Repairability:** Every component individually replaceable
- **Lifespan:** Built to last 10+ years
- **Plastic diverted per unit:** 25kg from landfill

The bed was not designed in an office — it was co-designed with Elders in Tennant Creek, tested in Alice Springs, and refined on Palm Island. Fred Campbell (Oonchiumpa, Western Arrernte man) consults on product design and cultural appropriateness.

**Current economics:** Production cost $550–650/unit at small batch; cost reduction pathway to ~$350/unit at scale (5,000+/year). Institutional pricing $700–850/unit yields gross margins of 50–55% at scale.

### Industrial Washing Machine

A fleet of community-deployed industrial washing machines, IoT-instrumented for remote telemetry. Elder [[dianne-stokes|Dianne Stokes]] (Warumungu) named the washing machine in language. 20 machines deployed across communities to date.

The washing machine fleet serves a dual purpose: providing clean laundry infrastructure to communities, and generating IoT telemetry data that feeds ACT's R&D program (fleet management, remote asset monitoring, anomaly detection).

## Manufacturing Model: Containerised On-Country

The scale vision is not a central factory that ships to communities. It is containerised manufacturing infrastructure that communities own and operate.

Each containerised facility is a self-contained production unit:
- Housed in shipping containers, transportable to regional centres
- Produces 1,000–1,500 durable goods annually
- Employs 4–6 local workers
- Processes locally-sourced recycled plastic as feedstock

**Scale plan:**
- **Year 1 (2026–27):** First facility on Jinibara Country (QLD), producing 1,500 beds, 6 FTE
- **Year 2 (2027–28):** Second facility at Oonchiumpa partnership (Alice Springs). Combined output: 3,500 units, 12 FTE
- **Year 3 (2028–29):** Third facility (Top End or Torres Strait). 5,000+ units/year, ~$3.5–4.2M revenue, 18 FTE

At 5,000 units/year: gross margin ~$1.75M, net operating surplus ~$850K–1.15M.

## Circular Economy Loop

Each facility accepts local plastic waste as feedstock, creating a closed loop — community waste becomes community furniture. Envirobank (Indigenous-owned recycled HDPE supplier) is in active partnership discussions as the supply chain anchor.

The model simultaneously:
1. Diverts plastic from landfill
2. Reduces freight costs for raw materials
3. Creates local employment
4. Keeps manufacturing value on country

## Digital Infrastructure: Asset Register

Every product receives a unique QR code at manufacture, linked to a Supabase-backed asset register (separate Supabase project: `cwsyhpiuepvdjtxaozwf`). The register tracks:

- Deployment location and community
- Condition check-ins (community health workers scan QR codes during visits)
- Repair history and component replacements
- Wash cycle counts (washing machine fleet, via IoT telemetry)
- Support tickets (communities report issues by scanning the QR code)

This telemetry data feeds ACT's broader R&D program — the IoT fleet is an experimental testbed for remote asset monitoring, anomaly detection, and community-managed fleet operations.

The codebase lives at `/Users/benknight/Code/Goods Asset Register/` (GitHub: `Acurioustractor/goods-asset-tracker`). Stack: Static HTML (Netlify), Supabase, CLI tools. Public-facing: product catalogue and community orders. Internal: asset tracking, inventory, delivery management.

## CivicGraph Integration: Goods Workspace

The `goods-workspace` route in [[civicgraph|CivicGraph]] (hosted in GrantScope) provides procurement intelligence for Goods on Country. It operates as a buyer and capital discovery layer — not duplicating what GHL CRM does, but adding automated discovery and scoring on top.

### Five Panels

1. **Thesis** — strategic framing, outstanding commitments, cash recovery signals
2. **Buyer Pipeline** — ranked potential buyers by plausibility score (Centrebuild, Centrecorp, Outback Stores, ALPA, Miwatj, Anyinginyi, QIC, Tangentyere, West Arnhem)
3. **Capital Stack** — ranked funders by capital fit score (Snow Foundation, FRRR, VFFF, SEFA, SEDI, Minderoo, Rio Tinto Foundation, Fortescue, Centrecorp Foundation, General Gumala Trust, Groote Eylandt Trust)
4. **Community Need + Proof** — community demand signals, delivery data, and Empathy Ledger story integration
5. **Delivery + Partner Graph** — supply chain partners, manufacturing partners, community partners

### Search Modes

Four ranking modes: Need-led (start from a community's documented needs), Buyer-led (rank buyers by procurement plausibility), Capital-led (rank funders by fit), Partner-led (map delivery ecosystem). Community-first navigation: select a community, see the full procurement picture.

### NT Remote Community Sweep

A dedicated panel audits official NT community coverage — postcode mapping, buyer presence, and service gaps — to surface communities with high need and no existing Goods relationship.

## R&D Tax Incentive

Goods on Country is classified as **Supporting R&D** under ACT's FY26 Division 355 claim (ITAA 1997). It supports the core R&D activities (ALMA AI orchestration, IoT fleet telemetry) by providing:

1. **IoT telemetry system** — Particle.io devices on 10 washing machines generate real-time data for the ALMA infrastructure experiments
2. **Remote deployment logistics** — iterating on community infrastructure constraints as an experimental testbed
3. **Community handover protocols** — transitioning from ACT-managed to community-managed fleet operations

FY26 R&D eligible spend (estimated): $65,516 direct, with apportioned benefit of $87,355.

Key R&D finding to date: technology handover is easier than relationship handover — community trust transfers to individuals, not organisations.

## Community Narrative Accountability

Communities don't just receive products — they tell the story of what those products mean. Through [[empathy-ledger|Empathy Ledger]], 33 storytellers across 7 communities document their own experiences. Each story is published only with community consent and elder approval where culturally required. Stories syndicate automatically to the Goods on Country website — community voices, not ACT's marketing team, define the public narrative.

This is accountability infrastructure: communities hold the pen.

## LCAA Connection

Goods on Country sits in the **Action** phase of [[lcaa-method|LCAA]] — making things that embody place and cultural knowledge.

But the project spans all four phases:
- **Listen** — Elders in Tennant Creek, Alice Springs, Palm Island shaped every design decision
- **Curiosity** — IoT data and community feedback loops drive ongoing product iteration
- **Action** — manufacturing, deploying, maintaining the fleet
- **Art** — Elder [[dianne-stokes|Dianne Stokes]] naming the washing machine in Warumungu language; community storytellers defining the narrative

It is also ACT's most direct test case for the [[designing-for-obsolescence|Designing for Obsolescence]] methodology — the long-term goal is community-owned and operated manufacturing, with ACT's role progressively diminishing.

## Place-Based Design Philosophy

Goods on Country rejects the model of designing products in offices and shipping solutions to communities. Its principles:

1. **Design with, not for** — every product specification validated through co-design with community members and Elders
2. **Made on country** — containerised manufacturing brings production to community, not community to production
3. **Culturally grounded** — naming, design aesthetics, and protocols shaped by community knowledge holders
4. **Community controlled** — the arc bends toward community ownership of facilities, data, and relationships
5. **Circular by default** — local waste becomes local goods; nothing leaves the loop unnecessarily
