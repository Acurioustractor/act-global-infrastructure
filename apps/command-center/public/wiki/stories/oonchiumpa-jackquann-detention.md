# Detention. That's not my home.

> Jackquann, 14

## Context

**Place:** Mparntwe (Alice Springs)
**Voice Owner:** Jackquann (J), 14, Upper Camp
**Consent Status:** Public

## Story

Jackquann is 14 and from Upper Camp in Mparntwe. He knows what detention looks like from the inside.

> "At six o'clock you get locked down. You wait till tomorrow."

That is the whole day compressed into one sentence. A locked door and waiting. Nothing else to say about it. No program, no rehabilitation, no pathway. Just a door and a clock.

Australia spends more than $1.3 million a year to put a child behind that door. The reoffending rate when they come out is above 80%. The system does not work by its own measures, let alone by the measures of the young people inside it.

When asked what keeps him out of trouble now, Jackquann does not talk about programs or case plans.

> "Looking after my family."

Family. Not compliance. Not fear of consequences. The thing that matters most to a 14-year-old is the thing the system interrupts first. Detention removes a young person from the people they are responsible to and responsible for. It breaks the thread that holds.

[[oonchiumpa|Oonchiumpa]] works because it keeps that thread intact. Jackquann stays on Country, stays connected to camp, stays in the web of responsibility that gives him a reason to show up differently. Not because someone told him to. Because his family needs him.
