# Court is scary because you don't know whether you're getting out or not

> Laquisha, 16

## Context

**Place:** Mparntwe (Alice Springs) / Darwin
**Voice Owner:** Laquisha (L), 16
**Consent Status:** Public

## Story

Laquisha is 16 and from Mparntwe. She was sent to Darwin, 1,500 kilometres from home. There is no youth detention facility in Central Australia. So the system puts children on a plane and sends them north, away from everything they know.

> "I don't like going to Darwin because I have no family there."

No family. No Country. Twelve-minute phone calls. A building full of strangers in a city that is not hers. That is what the justice system calls an appropriate response to a child's behaviour.

When asked what the root cause is, what sits underneath all of it, Laquisha did not hesitate.

> "Oppression."

One word. She is 16 and she named it before most policy papers do. The cycles that bring young people into contact with the justice system did not start with those young people. They started with removal, dispossession, and generations of institutional harm. Laquisha sees this clearly because she lives inside it.

[[oonchiumpa|Oonchiumpa]] exists so that young people like Laquisha do not have to be sent 1,500 kilometres from home. A 95% diversion rate means almost every young person the program works with stays on Country, stays near family, stays connected to the things that actually hold a life together.

The system spends $1.3 million a year to isolate a child. Oonchiumpa spends a fraction of that to keep one whole.
