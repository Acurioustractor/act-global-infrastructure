# ACT Voice Guide

> Write in ACT voice: warm, grounded, humble, visionary, professional yet rebellious.

## Overview

ACT voice is not a style guide for polish — it's a set of commitments about how language shapes power. How we write reflects what we believe. Savior framing, extractive language, and corporate jargon are excluded not because they're unfashionable but because they're incompatible with the work.

## Voice Characteristics

### Grounded Yet Visionary

Hold both at once. Be specific about what's happening now, and paint pictures of what's possible.

- Specific examples over abstract concepts
- Practical next steps alongside big vision
- Acknowledge constraints and challenges
- Real stories, honest about what we don't know
- Long-term thinking (generations, not quarters)

**Example:** "At Black Cockatoo Valley, we're planting 500 native seedlings this year. Each one is a small act toward a future where Glossy Black Cockatoos thrive, Indigenous rangers lead conservation, and communities govern their own land."

### Humble Yet Confident

"We're learning as we go" and "we're clear about our commitments" are not in tension.

- Center community expertise over ACT's
- Acknowledge failures and learning
- Clear and unafraid to name extractive systems
- Bold in vision and methodology

**Example:** "We don't claim to have the solution to youth justice—no single organization does. But we've learned from 15+ communities that when young people design their own accountability systems, recidivism drops significantly. We're confident community-led justice works. We're still learning how to support it best."

### Warm Yet Challenging

Welcoming and honest. Don't soften for comfort.

- Inclusive, appreciative, inviting
- Name extractive systems clearly
- Ask hard questions
- Call out complicity (including our own)

### Poetic Yet Clear

Use metaphor to illuminate, not to obscure. Concrete specifics over beautiful vagueness.

- Rhythm and flow in writing
- Imagery that evokes emotion
- Active voice, short sentences when needed
- Jargon-free or explained

## Farm Metaphors

Farm metaphors are ACT's primary metaphor bank, drawn from the tractor origin story and the regenerative land practice at Black Cockatoo Valley. Use with restraint — one or two per piece, not throughout.

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| **Planting** | Seeds (ideas/projects), soil (knowledge network), germination (early development) |
| **Growing** | Roots (deep relationships), tending (ongoing care), pruning (editing, focusing) |
| **Harvesting** | Yield (impact), saving seeds (documentation), compost (learning from failure) |
| **Ecosystem** | Biodiversity (diversity of approaches), pollination (cross-fertilization), seasons (natural rhythms) |

**When to use:** To make abstract concrete, to emphasize patience and cycles, to connect to place, to frame failure as composting.

**When not to use:** Don't force it, avoid mixed metaphors, not for technical documentation, be careful appropriating Indigenous knowledge.

## LCAA as Narrative Structure

The methodology makes a natural story arc for longer pieces:

> "We listened first — sitting with youth who'd been through the system, elders who remembered different ways, communities experimenting with alternatives. Their stories revealed a truth: punishment doesn't heal trauma.
>
> That listening sparked curiosity: What if young people designed their own accountability? What if communities governed justice locally?
>
> Action meant building it: JusticeHub as platform where programs can be forked like open-source code.
>
> Art translates it into culture: youth stories as valid evidence, imaginative installations showing alternatives to cages."

## Writing Patterns

### Openings

- **Mission/About:** Start with metaphor, then ground it
- **Project pages:** Start with the problem from community perspective
- **Stories:** Start with a specific scene or voice

### Endings

- Call to action with vision: "Join us. Get your hands dirty..."
- Return to metaphor: "Every story is a seed..."

## What to Avoid

### Corporate Jargon
"Leveraging synergies" / "Best in class solutions" / "Disrupting the space" / "Stakeholder alignment" / "Strategic imperatives"

### Savior Complex
"Helping communities in need" / "Bringing innovation to remote areas" / "Saving traditional knowledge" / "Empowering the marginalized"

### Extractive Language
"Harvesting data" / "Capturing value" / "Mining insights" / "Exploiting opportunities"

### Overclaiming
"World's first/only/best" / "Revolutionary solution" / "Guaranteed results"

## Content Types & Tone

| Type | Tone | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| Website Copy | Grounded yet visionary | Clear calls to action, scannable |
| Blog Articles | Story-driven | LCAA structure, specific examples |
| Social Media | Punchy, one idea | Image-driven, invitation to engage |
| Proposals/Grants | More formal, evidence-based | Still avoid jargon |
| Technical Docs | Clear, precise | Less metaphor, more instruction |

## Inclusive Language

- Capitalize: Indigenous, Aboriginal, Torres Strait Islander, First Nations
- Use specific nation names when known (Jinibara, Kalkadoon)
- "Traditional Owners" when referring to connection to Country
- "Young people" or "youth," not "kids" in formal contexts
- Avoid "at-risk" (deficit framing); use "justice-involved" rather than "criminal"
- "Experiencing poverty" not "the poor"
- Ask communities how they want to be named

## Frequently Used Phrases

- "Like a tractor's power take-off, we transfer capacity to communities driving their own futures"
- "Designing our own obsolescence"
- "Story sovereignty through technology"
- "Country as teacher, not resource"
- "Listen, Curiosity, Action, Art"
- "Art as the first form of revolution"
- "Consent-first, always"
- "OCAP principles baked into architecture"
