The architecture

Three teams. Three layers. One Supabase database. ABN as the universal join.

JusticeHub Intelligence is not a single product. It is a stack of three layers built by three teams in one shared Postgres, joined on the Australian Business Number wherever an entity has one, fuzzy-matched and human-verified where it does not. Each layer answers a different question. Asked together, they answer the question the field has not been able to answer for a decade: what would actually change if we funded the right things in the right places?

Layer 1 · The data spine

CivicGraph · civicgraph.app

100,036 entities resolved (charities, Indigenous corporations, government bodies, social enterprises, foundations, ASIC companies) with 94.1% F1 match precision against the Australian Business Register. 199,001 relationships mapped across 30+ public data sources, all unified by ABN: who funds whom, who shares board members, who donates to which political party, who supplies which government contract.

Youth justice is one lens. The same spine carries the full social-policy intelligence layer: NDIS providers and participants by service-district, ROGS justice spending (Productivity Commission, all states, multi-year), crime statistics by LGA (NSW BOCSAR expanding), SEIFA disadvantage by postcode (ABS 2021), AEC political donations 1998–2024, AusTender contracts (672K rows), ATO tax transparency, foundation giving patterns, social enterprise registers, modern slavery statements, lobbying registers, and crossover alerts (contracts of $100K+ where the supplier’s ABN matches a political donor’s). 96% postcode coverage, 95% LGA, 94% SEIFA. The infrastructure that funds youth justice analysis funds child-protection, disability, place-based disadvantage and political-accountability work too.

Layer 2 · The intervention map

JusticeHub + ALMA review · justicehub.com.au

1,155 verified youth-justice interventions documented in ALMA-governed JusticeHub evidence records, reviewed across evidence strength, community authority (Indigenous-first), harm risk, implementation capability, and option value. Linked to 507 operating organisations, 198 evidence items (research papers, RCTs, evaluations) and 1,150 outcomes. Each intervention is bridged to its funders via the alma_intervention_id key in justice_funding (52K rows of QLD, federal and cross-sector spend). Curated by the JusticeHub platform team, governed by a three-tier consent ledger (Public Knowledge Commons / Community Controlled / Strictly Private).

Layer 3 · The consented voice

Empathy Ledger v2 · empathyledger.com

The lived-experience layer. Storyteller corpus held under OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access and Possession; the First Nations Information Governance Centre’s principles for First Nations data sovereignty) and CARE (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics; the Global Indigenous Data Alliance’s principles for Indigenous data governance), with selective consent envelopes, twelve-gate consent chain, and right of withdrawal within 24 hours. Bridged to the intervention map by storyteller and organisation IDs. Today: ~500 storytellers across the consented anchor corpus, 18 named voices on the public Three Circles cast wall, audit trail for every quote, image and theme. The voice that the data spine and the intervention map are accountable to.

The bridge. JusticeHub organisations are linked to CivicGraph entities via ABN where one exists. As of April 2026, 315 of 556 JusticeHub organisations are bridged (57%); the remaining 241 are flagged for fuzzy-match or manual reconciliation. This number rises every week. The honest line is the one the field deserves: bridge live, not complete. Empathy Ledger is bridged at the storyteller-to-organisation edge. Full data verification status →
Stack: Next.js 15 (App Router) on Vercel · Supabase Postgres + pgvector · multi-provider LLM rotation (Groq, Gemini, MiniMax, DeepSeek, OpenAI, Anthropic) for cost-controlled enrichment · Cytoscape + d3-sankey for relational visualisation · CivicGraph MCP server (six tools live, listed below) and a developer REST API.
Beyond youth justice

Same spine. Other questions.

Funding the Three Circles funds the youth-justice intelligence layer. The infrastructure underneath it answers questions in every adjacent field. This matters to Minderoo because Minderoo’s own portfolio runs broader than justice: early childhood, women’s safety, oceans, eliminate cancer. The investment compounds across all of them.

Child protection

$10.2B per year (43% of CoLI24). Bridge ROGS spending to ACNC providers and SEIFA postcodes to ask: which postcodes have the highest child-protection spend and the lowest community-led prevention funding?

Disability · NDIS

Active providers, registered providers, and participants by service-district and disability group. Cross-reference with SEIFA and remoteness to surface where supply does not meet need. Same query pattern, different field.

Place-based disadvantage

SEIFA 2021 by postcode and SA2, postcode centroids, LGA mapping, remoteness classifications. The geographic spine that lets every other dataset answer place-based questions, not abstract ones.

Crossover · accountability

Bulk crossover alerts: every contract of $100K or more where the supplier’s ABN matches a political donor’s ABN. AEC disclosure 1998–2024 cross-referenced with AusTender. The political-accountability layer the field has not had before.

Philanthropy intelligence

10,779 foundations indexed, 30% with full giving-pattern enrichment. Cross-funder visibility so PRF, Snow, Dusseldorp, Ritchie and Minderoo can see what each other is funding without needing to ask. Coordination by infrastructure, not by meetings.

Government engagement

ROGS Productivity Commission spending by state, AusTender contracts, AEC donations, Hansard ingest (QLD live, others next), revolving-door tracking. The argument-quality data layer for engaging Treasury, AGs and DSS with the same evidence base they cite.

The evidence the infrastructure is answering

The Cost of Late Intervention 2024 report (The Front Project, supported by Minderoo Foundation) puts the national late-intervention bill at $22.3 billion every year, up from $15.2B in 2019. Annual growth is 7.7%, more than double inflation. Child Protection alone consumes $10.2B (43%). The number of young people in youth detention is falling while the cost per person per day is rising 50%.

The most-cited pipeline in the report: over half of young people in Victorian youth custody were first subject to a child protection order (Victorian Government 2023). This is the single strongest signal that late intervention is a downstream problem of unfunded early relationship. CoLI24, ALMA, CivicGraph and Empathy Ledger are cross-linked so a question like “where in the country is child-protection spending highest and on-Country diversion funding lowest?” returns evidence in one query.

CoLI24 is cited throughout the Three Circles proposal. The full PDF is at thefrontproject.org.au. The methodology is from the UK’s Early Intervention Foundation, adapted for Australia.

How it is queried

Six agent tools. Live today.

A community in Tennant Creek can ask a question of the system on a local agent without their information leaving their control. A magistrate can ask the same question. A funder can ask the same question. The MCP server exposes six tools today, with more planned.

civicgraph_search

Full-text search across 100K resolved entities, 199K relationships, and 1,155 interventions.

"What programs operate on Bwgcolman and how are they funded?"

civicgraph_entity

Pull a complete entity record: history, board, funding history, intervention links, geographic footprint.

"Show me everything about Palm Island Community Company."

civicgraph_power_index

Network analysis showing which orgs and individuals are central to a sub-graph (e.g. youth justice in QLD).

"Who are the ten most-connected organisations in NSW youth justice?"

civicgraph_funding_deserts

Geographic gap analysis: where is the population of at-risk young people highest and the funded alternatives lowest?

"Map the funding deserts in remote WA against detention rates."

civicgraph_revolving_door

Tracks individuals who move between government, advisory, philanthropy and provider roles. The revolving door of the sector.

"Who has held both an Attorney-General advisor role and a youth justice provider board seat in the last five years?"

civicgraph_ask

Natural language across all datasets at once. Asks the system what it knows, in the words a community uses.

"Has the NT government funded any on-Country diversion programs since 2020?"

Who it is for

Models, advocacy, frameworks. All built on the same spine.

The system is not a destination product. It is infrastructure. Other people’s frameworks, models, advocacy and editorial work plug into it. That is the point.

Communities

Query their own context on a local agent. "What is funded in our region? Who funds it? What works in places like ours?" Information stays local. No extraction.

Magistrates

Find culturally-grounded diversion alternatives by postcode and offence type. The Frameworks Institute work that Lucy raised lives here as a real test case, not another expensive PDF.

Journalists

Open API access from 2028 for fact-checked reporting on funding flows, revolving doors and program outcomes. Citation-grade evidence base.

Researchers

Peer-reviewable data with full provenance. The 1,155 ALMA interventions catalogue replaces ad hoc field scans with a maintained living record.

Philanthropy

PRF, Snow, Dusseldorp, Ritchie, Minderoo can see what each other is funding without needing to ask. Coordination by infrastructure, not by meetings.

Government

Treasury, AGs and DSS see ALMA-grade evidence of cost-effectiveness for community-led alternatives, citing the same source the field cites.

Lived experience

Empathy Ledger v2 holds the storyteller corpus. OCAP and CARE protocols at the data layer. Selective consent envelopes. Right of withdrawal within 24 hours.
