The lenses Minderoo brings to all its work

Minderoo’s 2030 strategy applies First Nations and gender as cross-cutting lenses to every program. The Three Circles applies the same lenses, by design.

First Nations. Every confirmed anchor is First Nations or First-Nations-led: Oonchiumpa (Arrernte governance, Mparntwe), Palm Island Community Company (Manbarra and Bwgcolman), MMEIC (Quandamooka Elders-in-Council, founded 1994), Brodie Germaine Fitness (Pita Pita Wayaka and Kalkadoon, Mount Isa). Story sovereignty sits with the storyteller through OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) and CARE (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) protocols enforced at the data layer.

 Gender. The seven backed in this proposal are run by women and elders as often as by men. The voices that anchor the artefact include Tanya Turner (Eastern Arrernte woman, UWA law graduate, co-director of Oonchiumpa), Kristy Bloomfield (co-director, Traditional Owner of Mparntwe), Aunty Ethel Robertson (Bwgcolman elder), Rachael Atkinson (CEO, PICC), and Laquisha (16, lives with her auntie). Gender equity is built into who holds the work, who is heard in the artefact, and who decides what counts as evidence.

The seven communities are the subject. The Living Brain makes their work visible to the rest of the sector. The Country We’re Building is how their work travels. Remove the seven and the method collapses.

The method

Three circles. One program.

A centre that holds everything. Ten local models held in relationship. An outer ring where philanthropy and international partners plug in. The circles only work as one program, funded together.

Circle One

The Living Brain

A queryable, locally-hosted intelligence system holding everything that exists on Australian youth justice. 1,155 verified interventions in the Australian Living Map of Alternatives (ALMA), 100,036 entities, 6 live AI agent tools. Communities query their own context on local agents.

Seven anchor communities, one from each state and territory, co-selected with Minderoo from a 235-candidate pool. Four confirmed (Oonchiumpa NT, PICC QLD, MMEIC QLD, BG Fit QLD). The artefact · 2028

Three tiers. One ecosystem.

The artefact is not a PDF. It is not even a single book. It is three tiers of work, made together with the young people the program walks alongside. A library that holds the method. An exhibition that holds the bodies. An installation tour that holds the encounter. Each tier feeds the next.

Tier 1 · Publication

Two book families. Different audiences. Same method.

The Stay Series. Seven volumes, one per method (Three Circles, Living Map Loop, Three Ripples, I Have I Am I Can, Field Canvas, Listen · Curiosity · Action · Art / LCAA, Fire Crescendo). Each volume is co-authored across all seven anchor communities, illustrated by their practice. The methods are chosen by the field as the load-bearing frameworks, and they are fluid: each volume is a living text, refreshed as the practice deepens. For libraries, magistrates, funders, teachers, journalists, the public. Sold everywhere books are sold.

The young people’s books. One bound journal per young person who wants one. Their own pages: portraits, transcripts, drawings, the things they decide stay in. Owned by them. Not indexed. Not searchable. Not Minderoo’s, not ACT’s. One archival copy held by ACT only with explicit consent, revocable at any time. Released on the day of their choosing, sometimes inside the exhibition, sometimes never beyond their family.

Inside the books

The method on the page. The country in the margin. The voice taped in.

Tier 2 · Exhibition

A national exhibition curated with the young people.

The Brave Ones portrait series goes on the wall. Studio-quality black-and-white photographs of the young people the system tried to misrecognise. Each portrait paired with the young person’s own words and a documentary video that explains the journey, in their voice, on their terms. The exhibition tours nationally then internationally: MCA, NGV, Powerhouse, regional galleries, ACCO halls, parliament foyers, Diagrama in Spain.

The young people are not subjects of the exhibition. They are its curators. Each opening requires at least one young person, or a designated cultural representative, present. Income from print sales and book sales flows back to the subjects and their nominated community organisations on a transparent published split.

Tier 3 · Interactive installations

Walk-through encounters, built with young people, like CONTAINED.

CONTAINED is a working example: a 40-foot shipping container fitted out as a youth-justice exhibition, currently touring eight confirmed locations. The Three Circles funds a second wave of these, one per anchor community, designed and built with the young people of that anchor as a paid service-work pathway. The container that lands in Mparntwe is built by Mparntwe young people. The container that lands on Bwgcolman is built by Bwgcolman young people. They curate the experience, build the interactive elements, host the visitors, train the next cohort. Skills transfer is the side-product. The encounter is the artefact.

Each install ties back to the data spine: scan a QR inside the container, see the local funding deserts, the local interventions, the local voices. The exhibition becomes a public reading room for the Living Brain.

Tier 1 · Publication

Seven organisation volumes in a slipcase. One bound journal per young person. One 90-minute documentary. The library + the personal record + the film.

Tier 2 · Exhibition

The Brave Ones portrait series + video + spoken word, touring nationally and internationally, curated with the young people, opened only with their presence or representation.

Tier 3 · Installations

CONTAINED-style walk-throughs, one per anchor, built by local young people as paid service work. Hands-on, scan-to-query the data spine inside the container.

Each tier is a different door into the same body of work. The library is for the policy room. The exhibition is for the public eye. The installation is for the encounter. The data spine sits underneath all three so every claim has a citation.

The ask

What it costs to back the seven properly.

$2.9M over three years. Three options. All of them back the seven communities already doing the work. Option 1 is the recommendation because it is what the communities already voted for by showing up.

Option 1 · recommended
$2.9M over three years · FY26 $780K · FY27 $960K · FY28 $1.16M

Back all seven communities. Plus the spine that makes their work visible. Plus the artefact that lets it travel.

Seven anchor partnerships at $35K–$50K per year, untied. Two to three storytelling FTE working alongside the communities. The continuing JusticeHub and CivicGraph build so what these seven do becomes evidence for the next seven. The Stay Series, the documentary, the touring exhibition, the journals. Minderoo’s name on the artefact, the convening and the framework launch in 2028.

Context: one year of detention for a single young person costs $1.33M. Australia’s late-intervention bill is $22.3 billion a year and climbing at 7.7% (CoLI24, The Front Project, supported by Minderoo). This three-year program costs less per year than detaining one child, and is designed to make the case for not detaining the next thousand.
