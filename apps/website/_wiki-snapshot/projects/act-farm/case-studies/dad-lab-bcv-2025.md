# DAD.LAB — the inaugural cohort at BCV, 2025

> Twenty dads. One weekend at Black Cockatoo Valley. A silent walk, a pile of soil, a pizza oven, and a framework small enough to carry home — *I have, I am, I can*. This is the case study of what happens when you take fatherhood out of the living room and put it on Country.

## What happened

The first DAD.LAB ran at [[black-cockatoo-valley|BCV]] in 2025. Twenty dads arrived on a Friday for a weekend that didn't look much like a men's retreat and didn't look much like a conference. It looked like people cooking pizza together, walking in silence, making living tables out of soil, and telling each other the truth about being a dad.

The arc was deliberate: **silent connection with nature → individual storytelling → pattern recognition → problem identification → collaborative response → knowledge sharing and commitment**. Every stage of that arc had a physical object attached to it — a pebble, a poster in soil, a living table of herbs and seeds, a dad-recipe card, a semaphore signal between three groups on a hill.

The framework underneath it was small enough to survive the trip home: **I have. I am. I can.** (HAC). Each dad built a bio that way; the framework doubled as a cookbook template (Ingredients / Flavour profile / Method) that turned values into repeatable recipes — *Phone-Free Dinner, Repair Within 24 Hours, Small-Batch Weekend Connection*.

## What we did

- **We ran the weekend on Country, not in a hotel.** BCV was rated exceptionally high by participants — the venue was doing work the facilitators didn't have to do.
- **We opened in silence.** A guided forest bath down to the stream, breathwork, and four prompts: *Why am I really really here. What has really really changed. What really really needs to change. Bring back an artefact that spoke to you.*
- **We gave the group a framework, not a script.** Dad Bios using HAC — *I have, I am, I can* — let dads speak about themselves as fathers without either performing or confessing.
- **We built living tables.** Soil networks on the tables, passions mapped, clustered, planted out as a visible record of the cohort's shared ground.
- **We ran Knowledge Circles** — deep-dives on discipline, difficult conversations with kids, relationship dynamics, technology and children. Participants later named these as the single most-wanted part of any future DAD.LAB.
- **We made pizza together.** Collaborative cooking was the bonding layer the facilitated activity couldn't force.
- **We closed with a commitment.** Each dad named one thing they would do in the next month. No one left without a concrete next move.

## What the work produced

**A proven format.** 100% of participants said they'd attend or recommend a future DAD.LAB. That number is not a vanity metric — it's the retention signal that tells ACT the Year Two design can amplify, not rebuild.

**Takeaways participants named themselves.** The post-retreat survey surfaced five recurring takeaways, in their words:

> "Permission to be vulnerable with other dads."
> "Realisation that we're all struggling with similar things."
> "Concrete strategies from other fathers."
> "A network of support beyond the weekend."
> "Commitment to regular connection with other dads."

**A reusable method, not just a weekend.** The HAC framework (I have / I am / I can) works as a bio, a recipe, and a mission statement. DAD.LAB now has a 60-minute guided mission flow and a one-page recipe-card worksheet that dads can take back into their households.

**Emerging themes for Year Two.** Vulnerability as a capability. The effect of participants' own fathers on their parenting. Balancing work, self-care, and presence. Navigating privilege while teaching resilience. Technology and children. Partnership alongside parenting. The transition when children become independent. These are the territories DAD.LAB 2026 is designed to sit in.

## What we learned

- **Silence does a lot of the work.** Opening the weekend with a silent forest walk set a contemplative tone the rest of the program ran on top of. Trying to get to the same depth with talking first would have taken hours longer.
- **Objects carry what words can't.** Pebbles, posters in soil, recipe cards, living tables — every deep conversation had a physical anchor. Dads who found it hard to talk found it easier to point.
- **Knowledge Circles were underspent.** The most consistent feedback was *more time here*. Year Two lengthens them and expands the topic set.
- **Pacing matters more than content.** Several activities (ideation especially) felt rushed; some (semaphore) drew mixed reviews. The 2026 design makes non-essential activities opt-in ("vote with your feet") and builds buffer between deep sessions.
- **Follow-up is part of the program, not after it.** Year One ran the weekend and stopped. Year Two builds explicit post-retreat connection pathways, a pay-it-forward sponsorship model, and a resource library.
- **BCV is the right ground for this work.** The combination of Country, quiet, a pizza oven, a swimming hole, a garden, and a circle of soil isn't replicable in a conference venue. The place is doing a share of the facilitation.
