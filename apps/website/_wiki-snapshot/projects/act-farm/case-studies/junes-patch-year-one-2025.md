# June's Patch Year One — the JCF partnership, 2024–2025

> A multi-year partnership between A Curious Tractor and the June Canavan Foundation, named for Dr June Canavan's belief in the health–nature intersection. Year One was supposed to be about nature prescriptions for healthcare workers. What it actually became was a community that runs as much online as on the land, and that is now pivoting into food production as the next form of therapy.

## What happened

[[junes-patch|June's Patch]] was designed as a "nature prescription" program for healthcare workers: therapeutic gardens, time on Country, meals from the land, no disclosure required. The first year of the JCF partnership kept that mission and stretched its shape.

Year One's design centred on residential and garden-based gatherings at [[black-cockatoo-valley|BCV]]. What we learned in the first twelve months: healthcare workers' schedules don't bend to a weekend-retreat calendar — nights, shifts, leave blocks, exhaustion. If the program only exists in person, most of the people it's for can't reach it. So we expanded.

Online community groups became 24/7 support networks that sat alongside physical gatherings. Hybrid engagement — in-person at BCV, digital between — turned out to be the format the cohort actually needed. Friendships and peer support structures formed that have outlasted the program window.

Meanwhile, the pilot site at BCV was established, partnerships strengthened with the Sunshine Coast Hospital Foundation and University of the Sunshine Coast, and ACT started treating healthcare-worker wellbeing as a long-horizon problem the partnership is equipped for: not a retreat product, a practice.

## What we did

- **We kept the mission but loosened the format.** The "no disclosure required, the work is the rest" promise stayed. The insistence that this had to be a residential weekend softened — a healthcare worker with nothing left at the end of a shift needs somewhere to go *that night*, not a retreat ticket in three months.
- **We built the online layer.** Dedicated community groups gave healthcare workers a 24/7 peer channel in a cohort that actually understood the rhythm of their work. This wasn't a Facebook group we threw up; it was facilitated with the same care as a residential session.
- **We ran physical gatherings at BCV** as the anchor. The pilot site was established; trips and cohort visits are running quarterly across the Sunshine Coast region.
- **We partnered with researchers.** The University of the Sunshine Coast is measuring wellbeing outcomes across the program. Bond University sits alongside through the adjacent Supporting NFP Leaders and Social Entrepreneurs study. This is not "we did a survey" evidence — it's properly designed research, on a timeline.
- **We strengthened the institutional ring.** Sunshine Coast Hospital Foundation deepened as a sector partner. Potential auspicing conversations advanced. Local farms, producers, and the Witta community moved from supportive bystanders to integrated parts of the program.
- **We documented the evolution.** The Annual Report to JCF is transparent about where the year went — not a stock "on-track" narrative, a real account of how the program changed shape and why. JCF's trust-based, multi-year philanthropy is what made that honesty possible.

## What the work produced

**A hybrid operating model for healthcare-worker wellbeing.** June's Patch now runs as online community + periodic physical gatherings + research partnership, rather than as a residential-retreat program. That model scales where the residential-only version couldn't.

**A cohort that stays.** Healthcare workers who came through the program have formed lasting friendships and peer-support structures that don't depend on the program to continue. That's the difference between a retreat and a network.

**A testimony that captures what the work feels like.** One Registered Nurse from the Sunshine Coast:

> "June's Patch gave me a space to breathe and reconnect with why I became a nurse. The garden became my sanctuary between shifts."

**An Annual Report that tells the truth.** JCF's trust-based funding let us report what actually happened rather than what the original proposal said would happen. That honesty is rare in philanthropy and it's what makes the Year Two pivot credible.

**A research baseline.** Year One lays down the evidence base — wellbeing measures, participation data, partnership structures — that Year Two and Three can demonstrate real movement against.

## The Year Two pivot: June's Patch Planting Projects

Year One proved that healthcare worker wellbeing responds to nature, food, and community. Year Two takes that finding and extends it into community food security.

**June's Patch Planting Projects** is the market-garden evolution. Healthcare workers who garden therapeutically also grow produce for local schools, community centres, healthcare facilities, and food-relief partners. The same plant that supports a nurse's wellbeing feeds a classroom.

Rationale:

- Healthcare workers have told us they want *purpose beyond clinical roles* — the market garden gives them that without asking them to do more care work
- Sunshine Coast food insecurity is a live problem — producing for local schools and food relief partners matters here
- The program becomes more durable when it produces something beyond its own cohort — schools, families, and food-relief partners become stakeholders in June's Patch continuing

Immediate priorities (Year Two):

- Garden infrastructure — raised beds, irrigation, storage
- Partnership formalisation with local schools and community centres for produce distribution
- Volunteer recruitment across healthcare workers, community members, families
- Educational programs connecting gardening, nutrition, and wellness

## What we learned

- **Format flexibility outperforms format purity.** A residential-only program would have reached fewer healthcare workers and reached them shallower. Hybrid is not a compromise; it is the right shape for this cohort.
- **Trust-based multi-year philanthropy lets programs evolve.** Year One shifted meaningfully from the original design. A single-year, output-locked grant would have forced us to either fake delivery or break the relationship. JCF's multi-year commitment made the honest pivot possible.
- **"Therapy" shows up as purpose, not just rest.** Healthcare workers told us repeatedly that contributing to something beyond their clinical work — a garden, a community, a meal — was as therapeutic as the quiet. Year Two is built on that finding.
- **Research partnerships are program partners, not reporting overhead.** The University of the Sunshine Coast work isn't happening so we can show charts — it's shaping the design.
- **The BCV pilot is necessary but not sufficient.** One site proves the pattern. Fifteen sites (the stated scope) will test whether the pattern travels. Year Two is the scale-out.
