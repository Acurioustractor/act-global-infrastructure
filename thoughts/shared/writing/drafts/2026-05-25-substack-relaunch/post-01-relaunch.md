## The work that pulled focus

Since January, Nicholas Marchesi and I have been doing three things in parallel: scaling CivicGraph past 597,000 entities and 1.5 million relationships into a real intelligence platform for Australian civil society; rebuilding JusticeHub as the practice layer for community-led justice work; and turning Empathy Ledger from a storytelling product into the accountability infrastructure that lets other civic AI tools earn the right to exist. Each of those needed our full attention for weeks at a time. None of them was a writing-friendly task.

About six weeks ago a conversation with a colleague forced me to name what was actually being built across all three. Until then we had been talking about them as a portfolio. Three products. Three pitches. Three roadmaps. The honest read was different. They were three layers of the same thing.

That thing has a name now. The civic cerebellum.

## What the civic cerebellum means

Most of the AI being built for civil society in 2026 is trying to become the CEO. Agentic systems that strategise, decide, plan, advise. There is some value in that work. It is not the work we should be doing.

Civic systems do not usually fail because they lack strategy. They fail because the basic reflexes break down. The follow-up does not happen. The consent is not logged. The referral gets lost. The evidence is not captured. The report is written too late, from memory, after the funding decision has been made on smoothed numbers. The community feedback never makes it back into the system.

Those failures are not glamorous. They are not what you pitch a foundation about. They are also where most of the harm lives. If you have ever been on the receiving end of a service that promised to follow up and then did not, you already know this.

The cerebellum is the part of the brain that handles reflexes. It is not the part that decides what kind of person you want to be. It is the part that means you do not fall over walking down stairs while you are thinking about what kind of person you want to be. It is essential, unglamorous, and easy to forget about until it stops working.

ACT is building that layer for civic work. The thesis is: automate the boring-but-essential work reliably, so the humans doing the actual work get to spend their time on care, relationships, judgment, and the decisions that AI should never make.

## What that looks like in three products

It looks like CivicGraph mapping who funds whom, who contracts what, and where the funding deserts are, so that a community organisation does not have to discover every quarter from scratch which foundations are aligned to its work. CivicGraph is the intelligence layer. It sees the field.

It looks like JusticeHub holding the catalogue of community-led alternatives that work, scored on evidence and cultural authority, so that a practitioner running a diversion program in Mparntwe (Alice Springs) can look up what has been tried in Murcia (Spain) or Glasgow (Scotland) without having to fly there. The Australian Living Map of Alternatives, ALMA, is the catalogue. JusticeHub Atlas is where it gets read. JusticeHub Practice (launching at the Reintegration Conference in late June) is where it gets used operationally. JusticeHub is the practice layer. It supports the work.

It looks like Empathy Ledger holding consent at intake, logging every AI inference that touches a storyteller's content, and gating publication through an audit trail that the storyteller can read. Empathy Ledger is the accountability layer. It holds the trust.

The three layers call each other in code. That part matters. The civic operating system is not a marketing line. CivicGraph writes provenance events to Empathy Ledger's accountability API. JusticeHub verifies consent against Empathy Ledger before surfacing a story. The wiring is real, not aspirational.

## What this Substack will be

Field Notes runs in four streams. You will see the labels on each post.

**What Communities Already Know.** Long essays, monthly. The cerebellum thesis applied to whatever the sector is breaking that week. This post is one of those.

**Field Notes from Elsewhere.** From late June through early August I am visiting five communities in Africa and Europe that have figured out something Australian philanthropy and government have not. Six weeks. Five stops. One per week from the road. The first post in this stream lands the week before I fly.

**State of Civic Money.** Quarterly. The first edition lands September, in time for Philanthropy Australia. It will draw directly from CivicGraph: the funding deserts, the revolving doors, the cross-system patterns that get smoothed over in annual reports. The data will be public. The opinions will be mine.

**Receipts.** Short observations, weekly when I am in country. One chart, one number, one story, one decision worth naming. The first one drops next week.

You can subscribe to any combination. Each stream has its own opt-in.
