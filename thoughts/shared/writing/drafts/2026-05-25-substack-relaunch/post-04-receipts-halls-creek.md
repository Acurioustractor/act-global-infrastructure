# Receipt #2 — Halls Creek, WA

**Title:** Halls Creek (Jaru, Kija, Gooniyandi Country) has 40 community-controlled organisations on the public registry. They share $574,000 in recorded funding.
**Subtitle:** The funding-desert story is not always "no one is doing the work." Sometimes the work is being done and the money is not arriving.
**Suggested send:** Week 2 after relaunch.
**Section:** Receipts
**Word count:** ~430

---

Halls Creek Shire sits in the East Kimberley, on Jaru, Kija, and Gooniyandi Country. Roughly 3,000 residents. SEIFA decile 1. Very Remote. The country around it has been Aboriginal-led for far longer than any Australian funding cycle.

A Curious Tractor's Funding Deserts Atlas places Halls Creek in the top 10 LGAs by desert score. Score: 165, tied with Kimba (SA) and Port Augusta (SA) and a handful of others.

What makes Halls Creek interesting is the second number alongside it.

**Indexed civic entities: 40.**

That is the number of legal organisations active in Halls Creek that show up in the public registers CivicGraph pulls from. Forty organisations doing the work. The Atlas tracks $574,000 in recorded funding flowing to them.

$574,000 divided across 40 entities. The arithmetic is not the headline. The pattern is.

## Two different failure modes

The funding-desert conversation often gets reduced to a single story: "no one is doing the work in remote Australia." It is a comfortable story for funders to tell because it implies the problem is supply, not allocation.

Halls Creek is the other story. There are forty organisations on the public record. There are obvious gaps in funding allocated to them. The work exists. The money is not arriving.

This pattern shows up in the Atlas wherever you find a Very Remote LGA on Aboriginal Country with a non-trivial number of community-controlled organisations. Halls Creek is not unique. It is one of a class.

## What the data does and does not say

The Atlas measures recorded funding from sources CivicGraph can see: federal contracts, justice funding programs, philanthropic donations, foundation grants, the ALMA intervention catalogue, NDIS flows. It does not measure state government health and education spending in the same line. It does not measure mining royalties or native title benefits that move through structures the public registers do not reach.

What it does measure is whether the formal civic philanthropy infrastructure of Australia, the foundations and the named funding programs, is reaching the organisations doing the work in places like Halls Creek. The answer at scale is that it is not.

## The decision worth naming

If you fund Aboriginal-led work in remote Australia, the question to ask your team is not "are there organisations we could fund in Halls Creek." There are forty. The question is: "if we look at our last five years of grant decisions, how many of those forty have we backed, named, called, met, or written about." The honest answer is the strategy.
