# Receipt #3 — Carrathool, NSW

**Title:** New South Wales has funding deserts too. Carrathool (Nari Nari, Mutthi Mutthi, Wiradjuri Country) is one of them.
**Subtitle:** The Atlas's eighth-worst-scored LGA is in a state most philanthropy decks assume is well covered.

A pattern in Australian philanthropy decks: the funding deserts are in the Northern Territory, the Kimberley, the South Australian outback. New South Wales gets put in a different column. The assumption is that NSW philanthropy is closer to its communities, that the population density and the Sydney head offices and the longer civic history mean the worst gaps live somewhere else.

A Curious Tractor's Funding Deserts Atlas shows the assumption is wrong.

The eighth-worst-scored LGA in the Atlas right now is **Carrathool**, in the Riverina region of southwestern NSW. On Nari Nari, Mutthi Mutthi, and Wiradjuri Country, depending on which part of the very large shire you are standing in. Very Remote classification. SEIFA decile 1. Desert score 160.

Indexed civic entities: 60. Total recorded funding from every source CivicGraph can see: **$2.66 million.**

## What that looks like on the ground

Carrathool Shire covers an area larger than several European countries and has fewer than 3,000 people. Towns include Hillston, Goolgowi, Rankins Springs. There is irrigation agriculture, cotton, citrus. There is one of the oldest continuous Aboriginal cultural landscapes in NSW. There are organisations doing health, education, cultural, environmental, and farming work in and around the shire. Sixty of them show up in public registries.

$2.66 million divided across 60 organisations over the period the Atlas measures is not a starvation number. It is a sub-survival number for any one organisation trying to do durable work. It is what happens when an LGA gets categorised as "rural NSW" rather than "remote Australia" and the funding maps draw their lines accordingly.

## The pattern this names

Carrathool is one of three NSW LGAs in the Atlas top 20. The others are similarly remote, similarly classified, similarly underfunded. None of them are in places that show up in standard philanthropic strategy papers.

The state-level framing hides this. Aggregated up to "NSW philanthropic giving," the Riverina deserts disappear into Sydney-weighted totals. Aggregated down to the LGA, they are visible immediately. Both framings are technically true. Only one is useful for the question funders should be asking.

## The decision worth naming

If your fund has a "rural NSW" or "regional Australia" portfolio, the Atlas filter by state is a 10-second check. Look at the NSW LGAs ranked by desert score, look at the organisations in each one, ask your team how many of those organisations you have actually backed. The list is short, the names are public, and the answer will tell you whether your portfolio is doing the geographic work it claims to do.
