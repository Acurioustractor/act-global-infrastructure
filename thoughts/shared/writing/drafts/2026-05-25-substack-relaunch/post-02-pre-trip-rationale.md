On 29 June I am getting on a plane. For six weeks I will be away from Witta (Sunshine Coast hinterland), visiting five communities across Africa and Europe that have built something Australian civil society has not. I am calling the project **What Communities Already Know**. This post is what it is for, what it is not, and how to follow along.

## Why I am going

A Curious Tractor (ACT) has spent the last eighteen months building infrastructure for community-led civic work in Australia. The thesis, in shorthand, is that most of the AI being built for civil society is trying to become the CEO when what civic systems actually need is a working cerebellum. The reflex layer. The boring-but-essential work that should happen every time and currently does not.

We have learned a lot. We have also hit a wall I do not think we can think our way through from inside the Australian context. Some of what we are trying to build, communities elsewhere have figured out already. They have figured it out in places with different histories, different funding structures, different relationships to the state. Australian philanthropy has not absorbed those lessons. Australian government policy has not absorbed those lessons. I do not want to write essays from Witta speculating about what those lessons might be. I want to sit with the people doing the work and find out.

I am going as a witness. Not a consultant. Not a researcher with a hypothesis to confirm. Not a journalist with a story already half-written. The trip is six weeks because that is long enough for each stop to be real and short enough that I will come back. The point is to learn what is teachable from the communities running these models, write what is true, and bring it back into the work ACT does at home.

## What I am asking each stop

Three questions, the same three at every stop:

- What did your community figure out that we should know about?
- What did it cost you to figure it out, and what do you still get wrong?
- What part of it is yours, and what part is teachable to people who do not live here?

The third question matters most. A lot of what works in one place does not transfer. Some of it does. The honest answer to "what is teachable" is what I am there to find.

## The frame, said plainly

I want to name what this trip is **not**, because the shape of these projects in our sector tends to drift toward the wrong things if I do not say it up front.

It is not a tour. I am not performing. No public talks at the stops. No keynotes. No selling.

It is not a study. There is no research design, no hypothesis test, no comparative methodology I am pretending to run. The output is editorial, not academic.

It is not extractive. Each host gets the recordings, the writing, and the photographs first. They sign off before anything publishes. Nothing goes out under my name without their review. The consent contract is the same one Empathy Ledger uses, because the trip is also a real-world stress test of whether Empathy Ledger's consent flow holds up in the most demanding cultural contexts I could find. If it does not work in Senegal or Glasgow, we should not be asking Oonchiumpa to trust it in Mparntwe.

It is not philanthropy tourism. The stops are not branded "ACT case studies." They are not vehicles for ACT pitching. They are the work, and ACT is the visiting infrastructure.

## The stops

The longlist has been cut to five over the last three weeks of partner conversations. Locking by 8 June. I will name each stop publicly in the post the week of departure, with the host organisation's consent. For now, the broad shape:

- **Two stops in Africa.** Both centred on community-led financial self-determination and community-led justice models that have run for a generation. The premise is that mutual aid and cooperative savings infrastructures (still active in parts of East and West Africa) have figured out something the Australian community sector is currently rediscovering through the language of "social enterprise."
- **Three stops in Europe.** A drug policy ecosystem that produced 25 years of public evidence about what works. A youth justice model built on public-health logic with measurable outcomes. A civic-commons tradition that holds a city together through cooperative governance rather than commissioning.

Each stop is four to seven days. Not a drive-by. Long enough to sit with people, not so long that the trip becomes about me being there.

## What comes back to you

One post per stop in this section, published the week after the visit. Each post includes:

- A short essay, written in the field, edited fast. The argument and what I actually saw.
- A short film (three to five minutes), recorded with consent at the stop.
- One chart from CivicGraph showing what the same question looks like in Australia, for contrast. Honest about the limits of comparison.
- Where to read more from the host if they have published.

After the trip a longer synthesis essay lands here in mid-August. That essay becomes the spine of my keynote at Philanthropy Australia in September. You will read it before that room hears it.

## What I am asking from readers

Two things, quietly.

If you have spent time at any of the kinds of places I am visiting (East African mutual aid, Portuguese drug policy practice, Glasgow public-health justice, Bolognese cooperative governance, or adjacent terrain), and you know someone there who I should also sit with, reply to this email. The stops are nearly locked but the relationships are not.

If you are part of an Australian organisation that funds work in these areas, or builds in these areas, I will be back in early August with field evidence, not opinions. If you want a conversation then, the calendar will open.

## What I am not asking from readers

Money. I am not crowdfunding the trip. ACT is paying for it from operating revenue. The trip is part of the work, not separate from it.

Attention while I am gone, beyond what shows up in your inbox. The weekly cadence is for accountability to readers, not for performance. If a week is quiet because the work needs the silence, that will be honest too.

## Why this matters now

There is a particular failure mode in Australian civil society where we wait for a better policy environment, a better funding environment, a better political environment, before we look honestly at what other communities have already built. The wait is its own form of avoidance.

We do not have to wait. The work exists. The communities have been doing it for years. The lessons are mostly in the open if you are willing to sit with the people who carry them.

I am going to sit. I will write what I hear. I will bring it back to the work at home.

See you on the road.
