## Executive summary

Minderoo has spent a decade building the supply side of First Nations enterprise. Generation One funds the employers. Dream Venture funds the founders. The Indigenous Employment Index measures the parity. Backing Black Business called for an Indigenous-led patient capital fund. First Australians Capital is that fund, and QBE put $500,000 into it in April 2024.

The supply side is now funded. The demand side is not.

Goods on Country is that demand-side infrastructure. Australian buyers. First Nations suppliers. A contracted flow of trade between them. The work is measured on the employment parity frame Minderoo commissioned Murawin to build.

The pipeline is not a plan. It is live. $767,000 of trade sits in the register now, seeded from real Xero data. Anchor buyers are at stage. Suppliers are onboarded. An agent layer runs the reconciliation, the cadence, the invoice drift, the narrative review, nightly.

## The problem

The Front Project's 2024 report, commissioned with Minderoo's support, puts the bill at $22.3 billion a year. That is what Australian governments spend on late intervention for children and young people under twenty-four. Child protection. Youth justice. Youth unemployment. Homelessness. Mental health. Family violence. Up from $15 billion five years ago.

The report maps nine domains of late intervention against six wellbeing preconditions that should have prevented the cost. Eight of the nine fail the same precondition. The one the report calls "Participating."

Participating means work. Work and study. The thing the economy was meant to supply.

The Northern Territory costs $2,808 per person per year in late intervention, against $921 in Queensland. The report attributes the difference to remoteness, workforce shortage, and what it calls "levels of Indigeneity." Those are the same three descriptors that identify the communities First Nations enterprise would employ.

The report diagnoses the gap. It does not prescribe the fix.

The Indigenous Procurement Policy operates at Commonwealth level. Mid-market buyers, which is where the trade volume sits, have no equivalent infrastructure. Supply-side capital and capability programs are well funded. Demand-side infrastructure is not.

Goods on Country is the demand-side infrastructure.

## The work

Goods on Country is a CRM, a pipeline, and an agent layer built on live trade data.

The CRM holds the buyers. Twelve stages, from first contact to acquittal. A funder cadence agent nudges any contact dormant longer than fourteen days. A procurement analyst agent surfaces seasonal buying patterns from the Xero history. An invoice drift detector catches any invoice that sits in draft more than three days. A Xero-to-GHL reconciler runs nightly and flags the contact records that have drifted from the financial truth. A narrative gatekeeper reads every outgoing draft for voice and tells us when it sounds like an institution.

The pipeline holds the suppliers. Named, certified where Supply Nation certifies them, vetted where they are not. The register shows which supplier can deliver which order and on what lead time.

The agent layer is not experimental. It runs on PM2 on a daily and weekly schedule. Each agent writes its drafts and cost into a ledger that the team reviews.

The operational question is one line: what does it cost to route a dollar of trade. We track it. It is falling.

Goods on Country does not compete with First Australians Capital or Dream Venture. FAC supplies the capital. Dream Venture supplies the capability. Supply Nation supplies the certification. Murawin supplies the measurement frame. Goods on Country supplies the trade volume that makes all of them pay back.
