## Understanding — drafted
Sue-Anne Hunter is a dedicated advocate for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children and young people, serving as the National Commissioner for this community. Additionally, she holds the position of Deputy Chair of Victoria’s Yoorrook Justice Commission, where she continues to work towards justice and healing from the impacts of colonization. Sue-Anne brings extensive experience in social services, mental health care, and child and family support to her roles. Her background as a Wurundjeri and Ngurai Illum Wurrung woman deeply informs her commitment to ensuring that Indigenous voices are central to policy discussions and decisions at governmental levels.

Sue-Anne appears to care profoundly about the well-being and rights of Indigenous children and young people, emphasizing the importance of healing from historical injustices. Her work is grounded in a strong sense of justice and a dedication to creating systems that support rather than marginalize these communities. She advocates for policies and practices that honor cultural heritage while addressing contemporary issues faced by Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples. Sue-Anne’s passion for this cause is evident through her continuous efforts to amplify Indigenous voices and promote systemic change within governmental frameworks.
