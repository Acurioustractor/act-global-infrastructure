## 01 · THE THREE CIRCLES — LOCKED

**Subtitle:** *A community-led methodology for working with people the system has failed.*

**Diagram:** three concentric circles. Edge in the centre (red), then "the next", then "the rest" in the outer. A spiral arrow shows the work moving from the centre outward.

| # | Circle | Who | When |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **THE EDGE** | The people the system has failed hardest. | First. Always. |
| 2 | **THE NEXT** | The kin, workers, advocates immediately around them. | Second. They get strengthened by what comes from the centre. |
| 3 | **THE REST** | Funders, government, sector. | Last. They get changed by what travels outward. |

**Tagline under diagram:** *start at the edge. work outward.*

**Back-cover blurb:**
> *"Authority sits with the people closest to the consequence."* — Tyson Yunkaporta, after 65,000 years.
>
> Documented again by Elinor Ostrom across five continents. Proven at national scale by Fundación Diagrama in Spain over thirty years.
>
> The Three Circles is the methodology: start where the system has failed hardest, and let the work travel outward. Most programs do the opposite. Most programs fail.

**Lineage (shared appendix with Three Ripples):**
- Tyson Yunkaporta — *Sand Talk* (2019) — Aboriginal thinking as a structural refusal of hierarchy
- Elinor Ostrom — *Governing the Commons* (1990), Nobel lecture (2009) — distributed governance outperforms hierarchy
- Fundación Diagrama — Spain, 30+ centres, 30 years, 20–30% reoffending vs Australia's 85%
- Jack Dorsey / Block — *From Hierarchy to Intelligence* (2026) — the contemporary tech footnote

## 02 · THE LIVING MAP LOOP — LOCKED

**🚨 NAMING RULE:** Always spelled out as **"Australian Living Map of Alternatives"** — never "ALMA" on its own. Acronym only allowed in engineering/database contexts.

**Title on cover:** THE LIVING MAP LOOP
**Subtitle:** *How communities grow the Australian Living Map of Alternatives.*

### The four moves

| # | Step | What happens | Who does it |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **WITNESS** | Someone notices a working model the formal system has never recognised — a kinship-based diversion program, an Aunty's pre-court intervention, an outstation healing model. | A community member, a worker, anyone willing to look |
| 2 | **DOCUMENT** | The model gets captured properly — story, mechanism, conditions, outcomes — in the witness's own voice and language. | Storyteller + the person running the model |
| 3 | **VERIFY** | Community sovereignty over what counts as proof. Elder, kin, and place-led validation. **Not** academic peer review. **Not** government audit. | The community itself, on its own terms |
| 4 | **PUBLISH** | The entry joins the Australian Living Map of Alternatives. Now it can be found, cited, funded, and replicated. | The Map (the infrastructure does the rest) |

**Why it loops:** every new entry attracts the next witness. A community in the Pilbara reads about a Mparntwe model and recognises something they've been doing for twenty years that nobody ever wrote down. The library grows by recognition, not by extraction.

**Tagline under diagram:** *the things that work are already happening. we just have to write them down — properly.*

## 04 · I HAVE · I AM · I CAN — LOCKED

**Title on cover:** I HAVE · I AM · I CAN
**Subtitle:** *Nine resources every child draws on to face adversity.*
*(Edith Grotberg credited on the back, not the front.)*

**Note on slot:** This replaces the earlier "Intentionality Canvas" slot. The existing `tools/intentionality-canvas.html` (SEED · FURROW · SPIRAL) **lives on as a separate practitioner tool but is NOT one of the seven covers.** Two distinct things, two distinct lives.

### The card

```
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                  │
  │   I HAVE     external supports                   │
  │              what's around me                    │
  │     1.  trusting, loving relationships           │
  │     2.  role models who show what                │
  │         resilient looks like                     │
  │     3.  people who set limits with care          │
  │                                                  │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │                                                  │
  │   I AM       inner strengths                     │
  │              what I carry                        │
  │     4.  respectful of myself —                   │
  │         "I want something better"                │
  │     5.  confident and optimistic                 │
  │     6.  empathic and caring of others            │
  │                                                  │
  ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │                                                  │
  │   I CAN      skills                              │
  │              what I do                           │
  │     7.  solve problems                           │
  │     8.  stay with a task / persevere             │
  │     9.  reach out for help when I need it        │
  │                                                  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

         resilience is contagious.
         its absence is also contagious.
```

### How the card is used

You don't ask the child these nine. You **map the child's life** against them. Where is each one being *fed*? Where is each one *starving*?

Most programs design for the visible deficit — no school, no food, no shelter. The resilience research is the proof that the invisible resources (the nine) are what actually predict whether a child emerges from adversity. **So you fund the nine, not the deficit.**

| Use | What you do |
|---|---|
| **Before any program** | Walk through the nine for a specific child or community. Star the lit. Mark the dark. Design only for the dark — don't waste budget on the lit. |
| **As the design test** | If your program doesn't feed at least three of the nine, it's not a resilience program. It's a service. Useful, maybe, but not resilience. |

**Tagline under diagram:** *resilience is the human capacity to face, deal with, and emerge stronger from experiences of adversity.*

### Back-cover blurb (LOCKED)

> Edith Grotberg studied street children in Lima — kids who'd lived on the streets for months or years, who'd used drugs, who'd been beaten by police, who'd sold sex for food. And she found the same thing she'd found in twenty other countries: the kids who *made it* drew on three pools of resources.
>
> **I have.** External supports — trusting relationships, role models, people who held the line.
> **I am.** Inner strengths — self-respect, confidence, empathy.
> **I can.** Skills — problem-solving, perseverance, knowing when to ask for help.
>
> Most programs design for the visible deficit. The resilience research says: design for the nine.
>
> And the hardest finding from Lima — from the families displaced by political violence: *resilience is contagious. Its absence is also contagious.* When the parents fled into trauma, the children fell with them. When the parents engaged the new world, the children rose with them. Whatever you build for a child, build the same thing for the adults around the child — or the child has no model to copy.
>
> *— after Edith Henderson Grotberg, Resilience in Street Children and in Victims of Political Violence in Peru, Lima 2006.*

### Source

- Grotberg, E. H. (2006). *Resilience in Street Children and in Victims of Political Violence in Peru.* Department of Prevention and Community Health, School of Public Health, The Medical Center.
- Conference: Sociedad Peruana de Resiliencia, Lima.
- Source PDF: `/Users/benknight/Downloads/grotberg2006_resilience-street-children-peru.pdf`
- The seven Lima street children observations Grotberg names are the empirical anchor for items 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9. Item 3 ("people who set limits with care") is from Grotberg's broader I-HAVE list across her international research, not the Lima paper specifically.

## 05 · THE FIELD CANVAS — LOCKED

**Title on cover:** THE FIELD CANVAS
**Subtitle:** *Look honestly at the whole field — above the soil and below — before you change anything.*
**Filename note:** journal still references `field-diagnostic-journal-spread.png`. Keep filename, change title text.

**Core insight:** *what grows above is a function of the soil below.*

### The diagram (field cross-section)

```
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │   ☀  VISIBLE SYSTEM   what outsiders see         │
   │     revenue · platforms · community · proof      │
   ├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤  ← ground line
   │   ●  SOCIAL SOIL    what sustains or             │
   │      undermines everything above                 │
   │      culture · operations · market               │
   │      darkness ▸ compost ▸ rebirth                │
   │                                                  │
   │   ◆◆◆  ROOT SYSTEM    what connects underground  │
   │   infrastructure · relationships · philosophy ·  │
   │                                  financial spine │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The 5 P scan (the spine)

| Domain | What you read | Hidden pattern |
|---|---|---|
| **Projects** | What's active. What's earning. What's stalled. | Where money comes from vs where energy goes. They almost never match. |
| **People** | Who's here. Who's paid. Who decides. | The unspoken pay gap. The implicit operating agreement. |
| **Pulses** | Operating rhythm. Weekly / monthly / quarterly. | Whether urgency is driving priority instead of strategy. |
| **Process** | How work moves through. Closure rate. | Starting is easy, finishing is hard. WIP creep. |
| **Products** | What's packaged. What's priced. Go-to-market. | "If we build it well enough, they'll come" — they don't. |

### The PTO discipline

**The tractor powers one implement at a time.** After the scan, every project gets sorted:

| Lane | Meaning |
|---|---|
| ⚡ **Drive** | Connect the PTO now. Maximum 3 projects. |
| 🌱 **Field** | Planted. Not powered yet. Hold the line. |
| 🔧 **Shed** | Park, sunset, or hand over. |

### The 5 symbols (the shared vocabulary)

These five travel between every ACT canvas. Lock them as the brand vocabulary.

| Symbol | Colour | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ● **SOIL** | dark | hidden conditions |
| ◐ **SEED** | gold | emerging possibility |
| ▰ **FURROW** | amber | concrete action |
| ↻ **SPIRAL** | indigo | learning loop |
| ✦ **SPROUT** | green | visible proof |

### The presenting tension (named on the cover)

A field-canvas conversation always opens with one line: **"What outcome is the organisation producing that nobody wants?"** You answer that first. Everything else is downstream.

### The 90-day Furrow Plan (the output)

Every diagnostic ends in **6 tracks** — each with action verb, target, proof:

| # | Track |
|---:|---|
| 1 | Mission |
| 2 | Revenue |
| 3 | People |
| 4 | Product |
| 5 | Partnerships |
| 6 | Rhythm (weekly · monthly · quarterly) |

**The model is the pair.** Diagnostic without furrow plan = therapy. Furrow plan without diagnostic = busywork.

### Tagline under diagram

*the field is honest. the soil is honest. the only thing left is to look.*

### Back-cover blurb (LOCKED)

> Most strategic plans fail because they describe the field above the ground and ignore the soil below.
>
> The Field Canvas is the tool that makes you look at both.
>
> Above: revenue, platforms, community, proof — the things outsiders see. Below: culture, operations, market — the soil that's either growing the visible work or rotting it. And under that, the root system: the infrastructure, relationships, philosophy and financial spine that connect everything.
>
> Scan the field with five domains: **Projects, People, Pulses, Process, Products.** Then name the **presenting tension** — the outcome the organisation is producing that nobody wants. Then do the hardest thing: sort every project into Drive (powered now), Field (planted, not yet) or Shed (parked or handed over). **The tractor powers one implement at a time.**
>
> The output is a 90-day Furrow Plan across six tracks: Mission, Revenue, People, Product, Partnerships, Rhythm. Each one has an action, a target, and the proof it landed.
>
> *What grows above is a function of the soil below.*


### The four phases

| # | Phase | What you do | The discipline |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Listen** | Receive what's already there. Country, Traditional Owners, Community, Elders, Youth, the Silenced. Physical presence over time. Story over data points. | **Don't arrive with solutions.** |
| 2 | **Curiosity** | Follow the threads from listening with genuine questions. *What if? Who says it has to be this way?* | **Resist premature closure.** |
| 3 | **Action** | Build, prototype, test, iterate. Co-creation with community as designers. Ship early. Open source. Document for handover. | **If you can't hand it over, you're still in Curiosity.** |
| 4 | **Art** | The work made beautiful, meaningful, lasting. Installations, stories, performance, land art, writing. The journal you are holding is art. The CONTAINED tour is art. | **Art is how we know we've learned something. If we can't express it, we haven't integrated it.** |

### How the loop runs in practice

| | Listen | Curiosity | Action | Art |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Outputs** | conversations, research, relationships | prototypes, experiments, learnings | deliverables, tools, infrastructure | stories, exhibitions, cultural moments |
| **Where it lives in ACT** | Empathy Ledger, field research | CivicGraph, the Living Map, JusticeHub | Goods on Country, PICC, The Harvest | Empathy Ledger, Uncle Allan, **CONTAINED tour, the STAY. journal** |

The discipline is **staying present to where you actually are** — not rushing past Listen, not staying in Curiosity when it's time to build. Some projects need years in Listen. Some live primarily in one phase. Some span all four.

### Tagline under diagram

*if we're building without listening, we're building the wrong thing. if we're acting without curiosity, we're moving too fast.*

### Back-cover blurb (LOCKED)

> *If you can't hand it over, you're still in Curiosity.*
>
> Most methodologies are checklists. Listen, Curiosity, Action, Art is a loop — and the loop never closes.
>
> **Listen.** Country, Traditional Owners, Community, Elders, Youth, the Silenced. Physical presence over time. Silence before speaking. Story over data points. Don't arrive with solutions.
>
> **Curiosity.** Follow the threads. *What if? Who says it has to be this way?* Resist premature closure. This is where innovation lives — in the space between what is and what could be.
>
> **Action.** Build, prototype, test, iterate. Co-creation with community as designers. Ship early. Open source. **Document for handover.** If you can't hand it over, you're still in Curiosity. (See *Beautiful Obsolescence.*)
>
> **Art.** The work made beautiful, meaningful, lasting. Not decoration — the highest expression of the work. The journal you are holding is art. The CONTAINED tour is art. This story is art. They will all return us to Listen.
>
> Art is how we know we've learned something. If we can't express it, we haven't integrated it.
>
> Then the cycle begins again.

**Title on cover:** FIRE CRESCENDO
**Subtitle:** *On building things slowly. Year 1 to Year 3.*

### The six stages × three years

| # | Stage | Year | What's happening | What's *not* happening |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Ember** | Y1 | A single relationship. One Elder. One yarn. | No metrics. No press. No "deliverables." |
| 2 | **Tinder** | Y1 | The dry stuff that catches. Trust. Showing up when you said you would. | No scaling pressure. |
| 3 | **Kindling** | Y2 | Small structured work. The first camp. The first cohort. | Still no "results" funders can wave around. |
| 4 | **Campfire** | Y2 | A working program. Communities visit each other. Storytellers tell their own stories. | Still small. Still local. Still right. |
| 5 | **Roaring fire** | Y3 | The work spreads. Other communities ask how. Funders compete to be in. | The early Elders are still at the centre. |
| 6 | **Bonfire** | Y3 | Holding warmth for people who weren't there at the start. Visible at scale. | And **only now** is the bonfire stage real. You can't skip to it. |

### The core argument (the line that pays for the budget)

> ***"Fire does not arrive at bonfire stage. You build it, slowly, with attention."***

Most programs fail because someone tries to light the bonfire in Year 1. Funders ask for bonfire-shaped outcomes from ember-stage work. Boards ask for scale before kindling has even caught. Fire Crescendo is the diagram that gives funders **the language to fund Year 1 properly** — and gives the team **the language to say "no, not yet"**.

### Tagline under diagram

*fire does not arrive at bonfire stage. you build it, slowly, with attention.*
