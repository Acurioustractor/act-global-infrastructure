# The Harvest Website — R&D Assessment
Generated: 2026-05-31

## WHAT IT IS

The Harvest is a community space on a 5-acre property at 9 Gumland Drive, Witta, on Jinibara Country (Sunshine Coast Hinterland, Queensland). It is not a farm, not a restaurant, not a gallery — the stated intent is a place where art and food connect people with themselves and each other, built around three principles: impermanence (spaces always becoming, nothing fixed), community co-creation (kids design the kids area, makers shape the shop, artists shape the art space), and custodianship (built to hand over). The thread runs explicitly through regional history: timber-getting, the cooperative dairy, the Maleny credit union and food co-op movement, and the prior nursery that occupied this exact site (The Green Harvest). The website is the outward face of that project: a TypeScript full-stack SPA (React 19 + Express/tRPC + Drizzle + Supabase) with CRM integration via GoHighLevel, content from the Empathy Ledger, and a community-intelligence knowledge base built from 14 local datasets (census, heritage, biodiversity, local media, Facebook groups, etc.).

There is no novel architecture, no experimental modelling, no regenerative-systems simulation. The Bauhaus brand exploration (/bauhaus route, brand-guide, zone-workshop pages) is design iteration, not R&D. Conclusion: ordinary business development for a community venue — not R&D eligible under the 43.5% R&DTI pathway.

## FIELD-RESEARCH QUESTIONS

Comparable places — cooperative community venues, seasonal residency programs, permaculture learning centres, community-owned food systems — in Europe, Japan, or North America would answer:

1. Co-op governance inflection point: at what membership scale and revenue does transitioning from founder-controlled to genuine co-operative ownership become viable without fracturing the founding culture? Maleny has a local precedent (food co-op, credit union) but international models (La Via Campesina affiliate sites, Italian social co-ops) offer richer data on the governance mechanics.
2. Sublicensing the kitchen: how do high-integrity community venues sublicence food operations without losing the message (local, honest, known supply chain)? What contract structures protect the ethos when the sublicensee scales?
3. Seasonal residency economics: how do comparable art+agriculture residency programs (Headlands Center, La Becque, farming residencies in Tuscany) price and select residents to generate revenue without pricing out the artists who matter most?
4. Goods on Country physical integration: does placing a containerised manufacturing line on a community-event site create friction (noise, logistics, zoning) or does it reinforce the message? International examples of maker-spaces embedded in community venues would answer this directly.
