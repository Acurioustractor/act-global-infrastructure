# Empathy Ledger v2 — R&D Assessment
Generated: 2026-05-31

## 1. WHAT IT IS

Empathy Ledger v2 is a multi-tenant storytelling platform for community organisations — Indigenous, multicultural, advocacy groups — serving both the organisation's field workers and the storytellers whose lives are held inside the system. It runs on Next.js 15 + Supabase (dedicated instance `yvnuayzslukamizrlhwb`), Inngest for durable async workflows, and an Editorial Warmth design system (ochre / terracotta / sage palette, Georgia serif). Organisations collect stories through a five-stage AI pipeline: field capture → audio intake (Whisper transcription) → AI transcript analysis (v5-unified, PRISM v2.0 framework) → AI story drafting (The Writer, three variants: long-form, short-form, quote-card) → Guardian review → publish with consent-gated syndication. Stories roll up from storyteller → project → org via Inngest aggregation jobs, and are available to external platforms (JusticeHub, partner embeds) through an HMAC-signed syndication API. The platform is simultaneously its own proving-ground: a "World Tour" module documents a real physical journey (Witta QLD completed Feb 2026; Alice Springs active; Africa and Europe Jun–Aug 2026) as primary content, making the platform's field performance directly observable.

Stack: Next.js 15 · TypeScript · Supabase (`yvnuayzslukamizrlhwb`) · Inngest (20 durable background functions) · Tailwind · Whisper (audio transcription) · MiniMax / OpenAI / Ollama (multi-model fallback) · Leonardo.ai (illustration generation) · MiniMax music-2.6 (music generation) · fal.ai · Three.js / React Three Fiber · IndexedDB offline queue (PWA).

## 2. TECHNICALLY NOVEL / UNCERTAIN PARTS

- **OCAP-as-code consent pipeline** — Typed consent/cultural-safety layer (ConsentStatus, CulturalSensitivityLevel, CulturalReviewStatus enums), hard consent-block in transcript analysis, Elder review gate, and a daily Inngest job (`purge-withdrawn-media`) that enacts a 30-day grace window before permanently deleting storyteller-withdrawn media from Supabase Storage (nulling all CDN URLs, preserving path as audit breadcrumb). This is a novel technical operationalisation of the OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) Indigenous data sovereignty framework; no prior production implementation existed at the time of design. Whether the schema + RLS + job combination reliably enforces the sovereignty promise under all edge cases was not determinable in advance.

- **PRISM v2.0 / ALMA signal extraction from a single transcript** — The v5-unified transcript analyzer (Zod schema, ~150 fields) extracts, in a single LLM call: thematic taxonomy (aligned to OCAP + SDGs), spiral resonance scores (6 themes, 0–1, each grounded in named cultural traditions — Dadirri/Ngangiwumirr, Koru/Māori, Ubuntu/Bantu, etc.), PRISM v2.0 impact signals (authority level, OCAP compliance, community value-return, handover potential, harm risk inverted), structured cultural markers (languages, places of significance, ceremonies, kinship connections), and impact dimensions across individual / community / environmental axes. Whether one structured-output call could reliably extract this density of culturally-grounded signal from raw oral transcripts without systematic dimension collapse, hallucination, or theme conflation was unknown at the time of design.

- **Guardian voice-authenticity scoring as a publish gate** — Phase 4 of the publish pipeline is an AI agent (`guardian-review` Inngest function) that runs three checks: (1) voice authenticity via fuzzy substring matching — measuring what percentage of draft sentences use the storyteller's actual words (threshold 70%, draft hard-blocked at readiness < 80/100); (2) fact consistency across all stories from the same storyteller; (3) composite readiness scoring that caps culturally-flagged HIGH content at 40 and requires Elder/community review. Writer system prompt enforces "Use exact quotes. Never paraphrase." Whether fuzzy substring matching reliably distinguishes legitimate editorial structuring from AI-invented content — without false-positives on paraphrase or false-negatives on subtle fabrication — was not determinable without production-scale testing.

- **Multi-model LLM fallback with cultural classification parity** — Transcript analysis uses a three-tier fallback chain: MiniMax (primary) → OpenAI GPT-4o-mini → local Ollama. All three are tested against the same `cultural_sensitivity_level` enum (`low / medium / high / sacred`) and `requires_elder_review` boolean. Whether each model in the fallback chain produces equivalent cultural sensitivity classification — rather than a lower-tier fallback systematically under-classifying culturally sensitive content and allowing it past the Elder review gate — is a genuine ongoing uncertainty. This cannot be resolved without empirical testing across culturally-specific transcripts from the communities actually using the platform.

- **Consent-revocation syndication with 5-minute compliance window** — When a storyteller withdraws consent, an Inngest webhook job delivers HMAC-signed `content_revoked` events to all registered consumer sites. The system targets < 5 minutes delivery end-to-end, with retry/backoff. Whether this window is reliably achievable under Inngest retry failures, consumer site downtime, or low-connectivity field conditions (Central Australia, rural Africa) was a genuine technical uncertainty at time of design — and the consequence of missing the window is a sovereignty violation, not a data inconsistency.

## 3. FIELD-RESEARCH QUESTIONS

An overseas visit to communities and partner organisations who use the platform would directly inform these open technical design questions:

- **OCAP classification fidelity in practice** — Does the `cultural_sensitivity_level` enum (low / medium / high / sacred) map to how partner communities in Central Australia, Tanzania, and Uganda actually describe their own content protocols? Are there distinctions that "sacred" collapses — e.g., gendered restrictions, seasonal restrictions, age-gated knowledge, ceremonial vs. historical — that would require community-specific overrides rather than a shared universal taxonomy?

- **Voice-authenticity gate in oral-culture contexts** — The Guardian requires ≥70% of draft sentences to trace back to the storyteller's actual transcript words. In cultures where oral tradition involves retelling, paraphrase, and re-voicing as legitimate practice (common across East African and many First Nations storytelling traditions), does this threshold create friction with what communities consider good storytelling? Would affected communities prefer a human-override path rather than an automated block?

- **Offline-first scope vs. real-world connectivity** — The IndexedDB + service-worker offline queue handles form data and binary file queuing, but defers all AI analysis to when connectivity returns. In Alice Springs, remote Tanzania, and rural Uganda — three active or upcoming World Tour stops — what are actual connectivity patterns during a field story-collection session? Is the current offline scope sufficient, or does the intake pipeline need to run transcript analysis partially on-device (Ollama local inference is already in the stack)?

- **Spiral theme taxonomy cultural accuracy** — The six spiral themes (Deep Listening/Dadirri, Unfolding/Koru, Interconnection/Ubuntu, Cycles and Return, Story Sovereignty, Community-Led Change) are grounded in named cultural traditions applied by the platform's designers. Do the communities whose traditions are cited — in particular Ngangiwumirr/Daly River for Dadirri, Māori communities for Koru — recognise the taxonomy as an accurate representation, or has it been applied in a way that flattens or misrepresents the original practice?

- **5-minute revocation window vs. community governance timelines** — Is a 5-minute technical delivery window fast enough from a community trust standpoint, or is the expectation closer to synchronous removal? And conversely: in community governance structures where consent decisions are made collectively through an Elder or community council (rather than by an individual), can a single storyteller's withdrawal trigger immediate revocation, or does the consent model need to support multi-signatory consent chains where revocation also requires collective authority?
