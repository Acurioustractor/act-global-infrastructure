**To the Indigenous Languages and Arts Program**
Department of Infrastructure, Transport, Regional Development, Communications and the Arts

**RE: Letter of Support — "Voices on Country: Palm Island Elders Knowledge Journeys"**

I write this letter as a Manbarra Traditional Owner and Director of Palm Island Community Company to express my full support for the "Voices on Country" project.

I am Allan Palm Island. My father taught me the stories, the language, and the law of the Manbarra people — the Nanggarra Wanggarra — from when I was eight years old. This island is my Country. The Manbarra people have been here since the Dreamtime, when the Rainbow Serpent broke apart and formed the Palm Island group.

But Palm Island carries another story too. From 1918 onwards, Aboriginal people from right across Queensland and the Torres Strait Islands were forcibly removed here — over 57 different language groups sent to this one island. Those people and their descendants became the Bwgcolman — "many tribes, one people." That name was given by my Elder, Uncle Dick Palm Island, and it tells you everything about who we are. We are Manbarra Country, and we are every nation that was brought here.

Our Elders carry knowledge that exists nowhere else. Languages, stories, place names, kinship — fragments from 57 nations, held in the memories of old people who won't be here forever. When an Elder passes, that knowledge goes with them. This is urgent work.

The Elders group has been meeting every week since 2016. They fundraised for two years on their own to get to Mission Beach in 2025 — that was the first journey, where 8 Elders went back to Hull River country and recorded 12 filmed interviews about the cyclone that destroyed the mission in 1918 and sent our families to Palm Island. They presented that work at the SNAICC national conference. Nobody told them to do this. They did it because the knowledge matters and time is running out.

"Voices on Country" is the next step. Two more journeys — the Atherton Tablelands, where many of our families were taken from places like Mareeba and Ravenshoe, and Central Australia, where we will be hosted by the Bloomfield family at their cattle station at Atnarpa for a cultural exchange with their young people from the Oonchiumpa program.

On these journeys, we will record Elders speaking Manbarra and other languages on Country. We will photograph and film their stories. We will bring young people so they can learn directly from old people — on the land, not in a classroom.

As Manbarra Traditional Owner, I give my authority and cultural permission for this project to record and preserve Manbarra language on Country. I will oversee cultural protocols for all recordings and ensure that sacred and private material is protected through the tiered access system our Elders have designed.

As a PICC Director, I confirm that this project has the full backing of the Board and the organisation. PICC has been operating for 19 years and has the governance, the staff, and the financial systems to manage this project properly.

This project belongs to the Elders. They designed it, they lead it, and they will decide what is shared and what is kept. It is a celebration of the determination, strength, and resilience of the Bwgcolman people — and of the Manbarra Country that holds us all.

I give this letter freely and I am not in receipt of any direct financial benefit from this grant.

Yours faithfully,

**Allan Palm Island**
Manbarra Traditional Owner
Director, Palm Island Community Company
Artist (MFA, RMIT University Melbourne, 1999)
