## 1. Organisation Business & Impact Mission (200 words max)

Goods on Country, operated by A Curious Tractor Pty Ltd, manufactures durable household goods from 100% recycled plastic for remote and marginalised Australian communities where conventional products fail within weeks.

Our flagship Stretch Bed — co-designed with First Nations communities across Central Australia, the Top End, Palm Island and Kalgoorlie — weighs 26kg, supports 200kg, assembles in five minutes, is fully washable, completely repairable, and built to last 10+ years. Each bed diverts 25kg of plastic from landfill.

To date we have deployed 389 beds and 20 washing machines across 8 communities, directly impacting over 1,000 lives and diverting 9,225kg of plastic waste. Demand significantly exceeds capacity, with 200–350 additional beds requested and 500+ mattress requests from Groote Eylandt alone.

Our mission-locked structure ensures 40% of profits flow back to the First Nations communities where we operate. We don't design for communities — we design with them, employing the Listen-Curiosity-Action-Art methodology to ensure every product reflects lived experience.

Co-founded by Nicholas Marchesi OAM (Orange Sky Laundry co-founder, 2016 Young Australian of the Year) and Benjamin Knight, Goods on Country exists to prove that circular economy manufacturing can be community-owned, culturally grounded, and commercially sustainable.

## 2. Business Model / Theory of Change (500 words max)

**The Problem:** In remote Australian communities, standard beds and appliances last weeks, not years. Extreme heat, overcrowding, dust, and limited repair infrastructure destroy conventional products. Families sleep on floors. Children arrive at school exhausted. Health outcomes deteriorate. Replacement cycles are constant and wasteful.

**Our Theory of Change:** By manufacturing indestructible household goods from recycled plastic — designed with communities, produced on country, and maintained through local employment — we simultaneously address housing inadequacy, waste, and economic exclusion.

**Product-Market Fit:** The Stretch Bed is engineered for conditions where everything else fails. 100% recycled HDPE plastic, UV-stabilised, fully washable with a garden hose, tool-free assembly, and every component individually replaceable. Production cost: $550–650. Retail: $850–1,200. Institutional/bulk: $700–850 per unit. At scale (5,000+ units/year), margins reach 35–40%.

**Revenue Model — Three Streams:**
1. **Institutional sales** (60% of revenue): Bulk orders from housing providers, health services, and community organisations. Centre Corp Foundation has approved 107 beds for Utopia Homelands. Miwatj Health (East Arnhem) exploring fleet deployment.
2. **Government procurement** (25%): State/territory housing departments replacing disposable mattress programs with durable alternatives at lower lifecycle cost.
3. **Retail and corporate** (15%): Direct sales through our e-commerce platform, plus corporate partnerships (camping, emergency services, defence).

**The Containerised Manufacturing Model:** This proposal centres on deploying containerised production facilities — self-contained manufacturing units housed in shipping containers that can be transported to regional centres. Each container produces 1,000+ beds annually, employs 4–6 local workers, and processes locally-sourced recycled plastic. This is not a factory that ships to communities — it is manufacturing infrastructure that communities own.

**Scale Plan:**
- **Year 1 (2026–27):** First containerised facility operational in QLD (Jinibara Country), producing 1,500 beds. Workforce: 6 FTE.
- **Year 2 (2027–28):** Second facility deployed to Central Australia (Oonchiumpa partnership, Alice Springs). Combined output: 3,500 units. Workforce: 12 FTE.
- **Year 3 (2028–29):** Third facility to Top End or Torres Strait. National output: 5,000+ units. Revenue: $3.5–4.2M. Workforce: 18 FTE.

**Circular Economy Loop:** Each facility accepts local plastic waste as feedstock, creating a closed loop — community waste becomes community furniture. This diverts plastic from landfill while reducing freight costs for raw materials.

**Path to Repayment:** At 5,000 units/year with blended average price of $800, annual revenue reaches $4M. With 35% gross margin ($1.4M) and operating costs of ~$900K, net operating surplus of ~$500K supports PFI repayment within the funding term while maintaining 40% profit-sharing with communities.

**Existing Validation:** $423,785 in philanthropic funding received (Snow Foundation, FRRR, Vincent Fairfax, The Funding Network). 389 products deployed. 8 communities served. 40+ verified community testimonials. Asset tracking system operational with QR-coded lifecycle monitoring for every product.

## 7. Funding Gap (400 words max)

**The Manufacturing Infrastructure Gap**

Goods on Country sits in a funding gap that is common for social enterprises transitioning from proof-of-concept to commercial scale: we have validated product-market fit and secured philanthropic support for program delivery, but cannot access the capital needed for manufacturing infrastructure.

**Why traditional lenders won't fund this:**
Commercial banks require 2–3 years of trading history and conventional asset security. As a mission-locked social enterprise in its third year of operations, with assets primarily deployed in remote communities (not recoverable as collateral), we don't meet standard lending criteria. Our track record is strong — 389 products, 8 communities, $423K philanthropic investment — but our financial statements reflect an enterprise still scaling, not one with the balance sheet banks require.

**Why philanthropy alone is insufficient:**
Our philanthropic funders (Snow Foundation, Vincent Fairfax, FRRR, The Funding Network) support program design, community engagement, R&D, and initial deployment. They do not fund capital manufacturing infrastructure — that falls outside their grant-making guidelines. We have exhausted the philanthropic funding available for our current stage.

**Why SEFA alone won't cover it:**
We are in advanced discussions with Social Enterprise Finance Australia (SEFA) regarding a social impact loan. However, SEFA's risk appetite requires co-investment from government or other institutional sources to de-risk their position. PFI funding provides exactly this: a government-backed repayable instrument that gives SEFA confidence to commit their portion.

**What PFI uniquely unlocks:**
PFI's $640K specifically funds containerised production equipment, mould tooling, and plastic processing machinery — capital expenditure that generates trade revenue from day one. This isn't subsidising operations; it's investing in productive infrastructure with a clear return path. Every dollar of PFI capital directly increases our manufacturing capacity and therefore our revenue-generating ability.

**The multiplier effect:**
PFI funding unlocks approximately $2.5M in additional investment: SEFA's social impact loan (contingent on government co-investment), accumulated trade revenue (contingent on production capacity), and continued philanthropic support for community engagement and workforce development (contingent on demonstrated scale-up capability). Without the manufacturing infrastructure that PFI funds, none of these other sources can be realised at the required scale.

**Repayment confidence:**
With validated demand exceeding production capacity by 3–5x, the primary risk is not revenue — it's manufacturing throughput. PFI removes this constraint, enabling revenue generation that comfortably services repayment.

## 8. Social Impact (500 words max)

**Target Beneficiaries and Impact**

**Primary beneficiaries:** First Nations families and individuals in remote and very remote Australian communities who currently lack access to durable, culturally appropriate household goods. Secondary beneficiaries include people experiencing homelessness in regional Queensland centres (Cairns, Townsville, Mount Isa).

**Scale of Impact:**
- Year 1: 1,500 households provided with durable beds/goods (estimated 4,500–6,000 individuals)
- Year 3: 5,000 households annually (15,000–20,000 individuals)
- Cumulative by 2029: 10,000+ households, 30,000+ lives directly impacted

**Impact Dimension 1: Health and Wellbeing**
Sleep quality directly determines health outcomes. Community health workers report that families sleeping on floors experience higher rates of skin infections, respiratory illness, chronic pain, and fatigue. Children attending school after sleeping on floors cannot learn effectively. Our beds provide a fundamental health intervention — not a luxury, but basic infrastructure that remote communities are currently denied.

Community voice (Jessica Allardyce, Miwatj Health, East Arnhem): "When we visit homelands, the beds are often the only furniture that has survived. Everything else is broken. These beds are still there, still being used, still in good condition."

**Impact Dimension 2: Circular Economy and Environmental**
Each bed diverts 25kg of recycled plastic from landfill. At 5,000 units/year, that's 125 tonnes of plastic waste transformed into 10+ year products annually. The containerised facility model accepts local waste plastic as feedstock, creating place-based circular economies where community waste literally becomes community furniture.

**Impact Dimension 3: Employment and Economic Participation**
Each containerised facility employs 4–6 workers, with priority given to First Nations people and disadvantaged Queenslanders. The QLD flagship facility creates at least 6 direct jobs in regional manufacturing. Our 40% profit-sharing model means communities where products are sold also receive direct economic benefit — not as charity recipients, but as economic participants.

**Impact Dimension 4: Community Ownership and Self-Determination**
Our Listen-Curiosity-Action-Art methodology ensures communities drive product design. The Stretch Bed wasn't designed in a Brisbane office — it was co-designed with Elders in Tennant Creek, tested in Alice Springs, refined on Palm Island. Elder Dianne Stokes (Tennant Creek) named the washing machine. Fred Campbell (Oonchiumpa, Western Arrernte man) consults on product design and cultural appropriateness. This is manufacturing infrastructure that respects and centres Indigenous knowledge.

**Queensland-Specific Impact:**
- Palm Island: 141 beds already deployed, community requesting expansion
- South East QLD: Flagship manufacturing facility creating regional jobs
- Torres Strait / Cape York: Identified as Year 2–3 deployment priorities
- Brisbane/Gold Coast homelessness services: exploring institutional partnerships

**Key Assumptions:**
- Average household size of 3–4 people (conservative for remote communities where overcrowding is common)
- 70% capacity utilisation in Year 1, scaling to 85% by Year 3
- Community demand sustained based on 1,000+ documented requests exceeding current 389 deployed
- Workforce recruited through existing community partnerships and Indigenous employment networks

## 9. Impact Measurement (500 words max)

**Impact Measurement Approach**

Goods on Country has built a technology-first impact measurement system from inception, not as an afterthought. Every product we manufacture is tracked through its entire lifecycle using our custom-built digital asset register.

**1. Product-Level Tracking (Operational — Live)**
Every bed and washing machine receives a unique QR code at manufacture. The QR code links to our Supabase-backed asset register, which tracks:
- **Deployment location** (community, household)
- **Condition check-ins** (community health workers scan QR codes during visits)
- **Support tickets** (communities can report issues by scanning the QR code)
- **Usage data** (IoT-enabled washing machines report wash cycles)
- **Repair history** (every component replacement logged)
- **Product lifespan** (days since deployment, condition trajectory)

Current data: 389 assets tracked across 8 communities in real-time. This system is production-ready and scales automatically with manufacturing output.

**2. Community Impact Indicators (Quarterly)**
Working with community health partners (Miwatj Health, NPY Women's Council, Palm Island Housing), we measure:
- **Sleep quality indicators**: Self-reported sleep quality improvements (baseline vs 3-month follow-up)
- **Product survival rate**: Percentage of products still functional at 6, 12, 24, 36 months (current survival rate: >95% at 12 months)
- **Replacement frequency**: Comparison with conventional products (beds previously replaced every 2–3 months vs our 10+ year design life)
- **Health worker observations**: Qualitative reports from community health staff on household conditions

**3. Environmental Impact (Annual)**
- **Plastic diverted**: Measured at input (kg of recycled plastic processed per facility)
- **Carbon offset**: Lifecycle analysis comparing recycled plastic manufacturing vs virgin material + disposal
- **Waste-to-product ratio**: Efficiency of plastic conversion process
- **Local waste absorption**: Percentage of each facility's feedstock sourced within 200km

**4. Economic Impact (Annual)**
- **Direct employment**: FTE jobs created, Indigenous employment percentage, wages paid
- **Community profit share**: Dollar value of 40% profit distributions to community partners
- **Government savings**: Estimated avoided cost from reduced mattress/furniture replacement cycles
- **Local procurement**: Dollar value of goods and services purchased from local suppliers

**5. Storytelling and Community Voice (Ongoing)**
We systematically record community stories through our Empathy Ledger methodology — ethical, consent-based documentation of lived experience. We currently hold 40+ verified testimonials from community members across 6 communities. Every story is recorded with explicit consent and community ownership of the narrative. This qualitative data provides the human context that quantitative metrics cannot capture.

**Reporting Framework:**
- Quarterly dashboard reports to funders (automated from asset register data)
- Annual impact report aligned with Social Traders Impact Measurement Framework
- Real-time public dashboard showing deployed products, community reach, and plastic diverted
- Independent evaluation at Year 2 midpoint (budget allocated in proposal)

**Data Infrastructure:**
Our measurement system runs on Supabase (PostgreSQL), with automated triggers, real-time dashboards, and API integrations. It is designed to scale — adding 5,000 tracked assets requires zero additional measurement infrastructure.

### 2. Pakkimjalji Kari — Washing Machine (PROTOTYPE — register interest)

| Spec | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Name** | "Pakkimjalki Kari" (Warumungu = "washing machine") |
| **Named by** | Elder Dianne Stokes |
| **Base unit** | Speed Queen AWNA62 (commercial grade, 70% of Aus commercial market) |
| **Warranty** | 5-year parts and labour |
| **Design life** | 10+ years (vs 1–2 years for standard machines in remote) |
| **Price** | ~$4,000 (including modifications) |
| **Deployed** | 5 units in Tennant Creek (Wilya Janta + Anyinginyi Health) |

**Custom modifications** ("What a bull bar is to a four-wheel drive"):
- Wrapped in recycled plastic case (protects from damage)
- Reduced to ONE button (community: "we don't know what all these buttons mean")
- Glass top protected/removed
- Customisable language panel (swappable community to community)
- Built-in telemetry (GPS, power monitoring — knows when it breaks)
- Simplified water cycle

**Why washing machines fail in remote** (East Arnhem Spin Project):
- Blocked pumps, broken suspension rods (40%+ of Simpson machines)
- 2,300 cycles/year vs 200 typical
- Vermin damage, incorrect installation, no local repair
- Half of all faults could be fixed on the spot with basic servicing

**Market context:** $3M/year washing machines sold → dumps (Alice Springs data). 59% of remote homes lack washing machines (FRRR, 2022).

## F. Production Facility — $100K Already Invested

### Container #1: Shredding & Collection
- Plastic shredder
- Community collection point
- Fills 200 tubs of processed plastic
- **Stays in community** between production runs (ongoing collection)

### Container #2: Production
- Hydraulic press (180°C)
- Computer-controlled router
- Moulds for beds, washing machines, AND fridges (same facility)
- Builds 200 beds from 200 tubs of plastic

### Circuit Deployment Model
1. Deploy to community for ~2 months
2. Produce ~30 beds/week
3. Move to next community
4. Shredding container stays; production container travels

- Communities may pay ~$5,000/week to host facility

### The Health Cascade (RHD Pathway)

1. No washing machine → dirty bedding → **scabies**
2. Scabies → skin infections → **Strep A**
3. Strep A → rheumatic fever → **Rheumatic Heart Disease**
4. RHD → death certificates for children

**Prevention point:** A washing machine interrupts the cascade. Clean bedding breaks the scabies cycle. "A good bed can prevent heart disease."
