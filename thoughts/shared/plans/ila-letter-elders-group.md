**To the Indigenous Languages and Arts Program**
Department of Infrastructure, Transport, Regional Development, Communications and the Arts

**RE: Joint Letter of Support — "Voices on Country: Palm Island Elders Knowledge Journeys"**

We are the Elders of the Bwgcolman people, writing together to support this application for our project, "Voices on Country."

We are the descendants of over 57 Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander nations that were forcibly removed to Palm Island. Our families were taken from places across Queensland and beyond — from the Atherton Tablelands, from the Gulf, from the coast, from the Torres Strait. Some of us carry language from those places. Some of us carry stories our grandmothers told us. Some of us carry knowledge that nobody else alive still holds. When we go, it goes with us.

That is why we started this work ourselves. Our Elders group has been meeting every week since 2016. We are not a committee that was formed for a grant. We are old people who sit together because the work needs doing. In 2024, we fundraised on our own — holding community events and putting our own resources together — so we could make our first journey back to Country.

In 2025, eight of us travelled to Mission Beach, to the place where Hull River Mission stood before the cyclone of March 1918 destroyed it and our families were sent to Palm Island. On that trip we recorded 12 filmed interviews. We stood on Country our grandparents were taken from and spoke their stories. We shared what we learned at the SNAICC national conference. That trip showed us this work is possible and it matters.

"Voices on Country" is the next chapter. We designed this project together in our meetings. We chose the destinations:

**The Atherton Tablelands** — because many of our families come from Mareeba, Ravenshoe, Herberton, and the surrounding communities. We want to go back to that Country, record our languages and stories there, and photograph the places our people were taken from.

**Central Australia** — because we have been invited by the Bloomfield family, Traditional Owners of Mparntwe, to visit their cattle station at Atnarpa for a cultural exchange. Their family runs Oonchiumpa, a youth diversion program for young people on Country. We want to bring our knowledge together with theirs and share it with the young ones from both communities.

We want young Bwgcolman people to come with us on these journeys. Many of our young ones do not know which of the 57 nations their family came from. These trips give them a chance to walk on their ancestral Country with old people who can tell them the stories.

We designed the way our knowledge will be protected. We created four levels:
- **Public** — exhibition prints and edited films that can be shared broadly
- **Community** — full interviews and photographs for Palm Island people
- **Family** — stories and portraits that belong to specific families
- **Sacred** — material that stays offline, accessed only with Elder permission

Nobody sees anything without our approval. The knowledge belongs to us and our families.

We also want to record Manbarra language — the language of this island's Traditional Owners — on Country. Allan Palm Island, our Manbarra Traditional Owner, leads this cultural authority. The words, the place names, the kinship terms that our old people still carry need to be written down and given to the school so the children can learn.

This is not a project that was designed in an office. It came from us, sitting together, talking about what we need to do before it is too late. PICC supports us and has the capacity to manage the funding properly, but the project belongs to the Elders.

We give this letter of support freely and collectively. We are not in receipt of any direct financial benefit from this grant.
