## 3. Investment priorities — funds applied per Snow's framework (§ 11)

Snow's signed proposal sets investment priorities in order: **(1) more beds → (2) production facility → (3) Nic + Ben's time**. The $395K commitment maps as follows:

- **Status as of report date**: facility ~85% complete, final assembly + community siting decision pending. Production model targets community-based manufacturing in Alice Springs, Tennant Creek, Katherine, Darwin circuit through 2026.

## 4. Alignment to Snow First Nations Principles (§ 8)

Reporting against each of the 8 principles in the signed proposal:

### 1. First Nations leadership and empowerment
**Evidence:**
- Bloomfield family (Tennant Creek) leads design decisions on washing machine V1 build. Asked Nic + Ben to help plan next homestead iteration ($100K project) — demonstrating reciprocal partnership rather than one-way technology transfer.
- Cultural advisors paid at university research rates ($3,800/day) throughout 2024-25. Acknowledged in meeting: "we asked for no favours."
- Aboriginal ownership and control of products — exploring models where communities own production assets and generate revenue (Palm Island offered to buy a plant; QIC building 50 beds with staff).

### 2. Early partnership and co-design
**Evidence:**
- Product evolved through iterative yarning + relational feedback (not formal surveys) — Norm Frank's maroon request became a design feature; Diane Stokes' "20 more" became validation of cleanability + durability.
- 15-20 beds with 8 families = current iteration sample. Targeting a few hundred (this $120K round) before scaling to 5,000+ units.
- Co-creating an agreement template with Snow Foundation that can be replicated for other organisations — best-practice co-design framework as a Snow + ACT joint product.

### 3. Place-based, on-Country delivery
**Evidence:**
- Northern Territory focus: Alice Springs · Tennant Creek · Katherine · Darwin circuit. Utopian Homelands + Maningrida sites confirmed.
- Production planned in community sheds (Tennant Creek Community Shed has expressed hosting interest) — under community control, not imposed infrastructure.
- 25 kg of local plastic waste per bed → adapts to community waste streams (200 tubs fillable locally, then production container arrives to assemble).
- NT is one of Snow Foundation's four priority places — full alignment.

### 4. Genuine collaboration and partnerships
**Evidence:**
- Orange Sky-inspired model: ACT builds container facilities, local organisations staff and operate them. Partner pipeline includes Red Dust Robotics, Deadly Science, Centre of Appropriate Technology, NPY Women's Council (Angela Lynch — "always looking for beds"), Tennant Creek Community Shed, Mayor Sid Tennant Creek (night patrol involvement in plastic collection).
- Reciprocity: products address community-chosen priorities (beds, washing machines, fridges). Communities identify what they need; ACT responds — not the inverse.
- Knowledge return: product IP + production knowledge transfer to communities. Palm Island and others can purchase/own production plants.
- Anti-duplication: partnering with existing strong organisations rather than creating a new "Goods charity" or separate Aboriginal-controlled entity.

### 5. Advocacy and policy influence
**Evidence:**
- Addressing the documented $3M/year washing machine dumping cycle in Alice Springs (one provider sells $3M annually, most ending in dumps). Each bed diverts 25 kg of plastic from landfill.
- Building community-led evidence base for policy change on plastic waste, circular economy in remote communities, and procurement standards.
- "Virus of better products" framing — introducing desirable products that displace disposable imports, naturally reducing demand.

### 6. Evidence-based and culturally safe programs
**Evidence:**
- Product evolution through community yarning + relational feedback, not formal surveys or extractive research.
- Cultural safety in design: beds work inside/outside, are cleanable + movable, match house colours (Norm Frank's maroon).
- Trauma-aware approach: no imposed solutions; no "fixing" communities with Western furniture models — creating products people want and choose.
- Workforce training: production model creates skills in manufacturing, materials processing, computer-controlled routing, plastic collection and sorting.

### 7. Long-term, flexible funding
**Evidence:**
- Multi-year vision: 1,000-bed inventory + social enterprise model in progress. Snow Foundation has offered access to social impact loans for working capital — exploring this pathway.
- Full cost recovery model: moving from zero-margin product delivery toward sustainable pricing that enables reinvestment in programs.
- Flexible funding lines: the $120K request explicitly includes production plant infrastructure that serves multiple purposes (beds, washing machines, fridges) and can generate revenue.
- Local workforce pipelines: production facility creates ongoing jobs in communities. 30 beds/week over 2 months supports sustained local employment per production run.
- Stability for planning: the FY26 Scale-Up round enables completion of production facility + 100-bed deployment, creating foundation for Q2-Q4 2026 circuit production runs.

### 8. Data sovereignty and Indigenous IP
**Evidence:**
- Products co-designed with Bloomfield family — cultural knowledge + design input owned by community.
- Testing feedback returns to Bloomfields first; production learnings shared with partner communities before external reporting.
- Not an academic study or evaluation — practical production partnership where communities retain control.
- ACT commits to Indigenous Data Sovereignty principles for any future impact measurement: agreeing purpose, access, and storage upfront with community partners.

## 5. Safeguarding — Do No Harm + Dynamic Consent (§ 9)

### Do No Harm
- Staged scale-up: 15-20 beds → 100 beds (this round) → 1,000+ inventory (with social impact loan pathway). Pause points between each stage for community-led feedback.
- Testing + clear iteration gates tied to evidence, not timelines. V4 design changes informed by field deployment.
- Risk register maintained: waste management + environmental risk, demand risk, payment + cashflow risk, quality/safety/durability risk, partnership + cultural safety risk (full list with mitigations in signed proposal § 6).

### Dynamic Consent
- Consent treated as ongoing, not one-off. Partners can change scope, pause, or reshape activities as relationships and on-ground realities evolve.
- Agreements revisited before each production run and before any public storytelling or data use.
- Community leaders guide cultural protocols, payment structures, partnership models — ACT follows their lead.
- Reciprocity commitment: leave tangible assets, skills, and revenue-generating capability in community — not just consumable products.

## 9. Context: shift toward social enterprise self-determination

2024-2025 was a challenging philanthropy conversion year. ACT's R&D + growth supporters (beyond product purchase) were Snow Foundation, The Funding Network ($80K for production facility — actual costs exceeded $100K), and Centrecorp Foundation.

This context catalysed ACT's shift toward **self-determination through social enterprise** — not abandoning philanthropy, but building financial independence to control the program's future while maintaining deep community partnerships. The $120K FY26 Production Scale-Up round positions ACT to:

1. Complete production infrastructure (one-time capital investment)
2. Build inventory for commercial sales (1,000+ bed target)
3. Explore Snow's social impact loan pathway for working capital
4. Move from zero-margin product delivery → sustainable pricing → reinvestment in programs

**The model**: philanthropy builds the plant, then loan or rent it to communities, creating revenue stream for program reinvestment. Queensland's large social impact fund has limited recipients — ACT is positioned for that pathway as production scales.
