# Confessions to philanthropy: a history of the gift

A deep-dive for A Curious Tractor, grounding the "Confessions to Philanthropy" campaign during QLD Philanthropy Week.

The tax-advantaged foundation feels like the natural shape of giving. It is not. It is one branch, grown in two countries, in the last hundred-odd years. For most of human history, and for most of the world right now, the gift was something else: a duty, a debt repaid, a thing kept moving, a hand reaching across a relationship. This is the long story the campaign asks philanthropy to remember.

## The word

The word comes from a god in chains.

"Philanthropy" is built from two Greek roots: *phileô*, the love between friends, and *anthrôpos*, humankind. Love of humanity. Scholars trace its first appearance to Aeschylus and the play *Prometheus Bound*, where the gift Prometheus gives the human race, fire, is called an act of *philanthrôpía* ([Johnson Center, Defining Philanthropy](https://johnsoncenter.org/blog/defining-philanthropy-part-i-love-of-humankind/)).

Look at what that gift was. Prometheus defied the Olympian gods. He took fire from them and carried it back to humanity in a fennel stalk, and with it the technical knowledge that fire alone would be useless without ([Britannica, Prometheus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Prometheus-Greek-god); [EBSCO, Prometheus and the Theft of Fire](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/religion-and-philosophy/prometheus-and-theft-fire)). Zeus chained him to a rock for it. An eagle ate his liver. It grew back each night so the eagle could return.

So the first philanthropist in the Western record is not a rich man writing a cheque. He is a figure who stole from power and handed the means of survival down to people who had nothing, and was tortured forever for the theft. The original meaning was not surplus wealth disposed of well. It was love of what a human being could become, and a willingness to defy the powerful to give it. Philanthropy began as defiance, not as an instrument of the wealthy.

## Classical antiquity

Greek and Roman giving carried a tension that has never gone away: the gift that helps, and the gift that buys standing.

In the Roman world the historian Paul Veyne named the pattern euergetism, from the Greek for "doing good works." The wealthy, the senatorial families, and the emperor gave to the city: games, grain, baths, temples, the bread and the circuses ([Bryn Mawr Classical Review on Veyne](https://bmcr.brynmawr.edu/2003/2003.04.28); [Aeon on Paul Veyne](https://aeon.co/essays/the-scorched-earth-iconoclasm-of-the-historian-paul-veyne)). It was understood as a duty and a privilege of the rich to benefit their fellow citizens.

Veyne was careful about what it was not. It was not redistribution. It was not relief for the poor as poor. The wealthy gave to members of the community whether they needed it or not, and they gave for the sake of giving. In return they received honour ([Aeon on Paul Veyne](https://aeon.co/essays/the-scorched-earth-iconoclasm-of-the-historian-paul-veyne)). Your name went on the wall. Your family's name went on the aqueduct. This is the original naming rights. The gift was real and the city was fed, and at the same time the gift was a transaction in status, the donor's standing bought with the donor's purse.

The tension sits at the root of the Western tradition: a gift that does good and props up the giver in the same motion. Carnegie inherited it. So did the modern foundation. So does the gala with the donor's name on the lectern.

## Religious obligation

Step outside Greece and Rome and the gift stops being optional. In the great religious traditions, giving is law, debt, and relationship before it is generosity.

In Judaism the word is *tzedakah*. Its root is *tzedek*: justice, righteousness, fairness. This is the load-bearing point. English "charity" comes from the Latin *caritas*, love, a feeling the giver chooses to have. *Tzedakah* is not a feeling. It is the legal requirement to do rightly by another person ([Wikipedia, Tzedakah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tzedakah)). You give because justice demands it, not because you feel moved. Maimonides ranked it in the twelfth century: eight levels, with the highest being a gift, loan, or partnership that ends the recipient's need for gifts altogether, and the second a gift given anonymously to someone unknown, so neither party can lord it over the other ([Jews for Judaism, Maimonides' Eight Levels](https://jewsforjudaism.org/knowledge/articles/maimonides-eight-levels-charity/)). The dignity of the person receiving is built into the rule.

In Islam there are three forms. *Zakat*, a fixed annual share of wealth given to those in need, is one of the five pillars, an obligation, not a choice. *Sadaqah* is voluntary giving on top of it. And *waqf* is the endowment: a permanent, irrevocable dedication of an asset, land or a building or cash, so that its benefit flows to people in need while the asset itself stays intact ([Islamic Relief, What is waqf](https://islamic-relief.org/news/what-is-waqf-and-how-does-it-work/)). Read that definition again. A protected asset held in perpetuity, its returns directed to public benefit, governed by deed. That is the modern endowed foundation. By the tenth century the Islamic world had hospitals, medical schools, and universities running on waqf revenue, paying doctors, teachers, and students from the endowment ([Islamic Relief, What is waqf](https://islamic-relief.org/news/what-is-waqf-and-how-does-it-work/); [Academia.edu, A history of philanthropic foundations: the Islamic world](https://www.academia.edu/2153270/A_history_of_philanthropic_foundations_The_Islamic_world_from_the_seventh_century_to_the_present)). The perpetual charitable foundation that Carnegie and Rockefeller are credited with inventing already existed, by name and by deed, roughly a thousand years before they were born.

Christianity carried *caritas* and the tithe, a tenth set aside, with the early church holding goods in common, "no one said that any of the things that belonged to him was his own" ([Pushpay, tithing in the early church](https://pushpay.com/blog/6-quotes-on-tithing-in-the-early-church/)). In the dharmic and Sikh traditions the word is *dana*, the cultivation of generosity, treated in Hinduism as part of one's *dharma*, one's duty, and in Buddhism as a practice that works on the giver's own mind ([Wikipedia, Dāna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%C4%81na)). *Seva*, selfless service, runs through Hindu and Sikh practice, service to God and to people expecting nothing back ([Wikipedia, Sevā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sev%C4%81)). The Sikh *langar*, the free kitchen in every gurdwara, seats the rich and the poorest on the same floor to eat the same food. The point is stated plainly in the tradition: in langar the donor-and-recipient power relationship that "Aid" or "Charity" depends on does not exist, so no one is made a charity case ([Sikh Missionary Society, Seva](https://www.sikhmissionarysociety.org/sms/smssikhism/institutions/seva/)).

Across all of them the gift is duty and reciprocity inside a relationship. You give because you are bound to. The receiver's dignity is part of the rule. Nobody is doing anyone a favour.

## Indigenous and gift economies

There is an older shape still, where "giving" and "economy" are not separate words.

In 1925 Marcel Mauss read the ethnographies and wrote *The Gift*. His argument: in these societies the gift only looks free. It is held in place by three obligations, the obligation to give, the obligation to receive, and the obligation to repay ([Wikipedia, The Gift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Gift_(essay))). A gift creates a debt, the debt creates a relationship, and the relationship is the point. He drew on Franz Boas on the potlatch of the Pacific Northwest, where a host raised and held status by giving away, sometimes destroying, great quantities of goods, and on Bronislaw Malinowski on the kula ring of the Trobriand Islands, where shell valuables travel between islands in a circle that never stops. The valuables of the kula, in theory, never cease to circulate ([Wikipedia, The Gift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Gift_(essay))). Wealth that stops moving is wealth that has failed.

Aboriginal Australia runs on the same logic, sharpened by kinship and Country. Sharing is not a kindness offered. It is an obligation set by rules of kin, taken seriously, with food and tools moving as a matter of duty within and beyond immediate family ([Centre for Indigenous Policy Research, The calculus of reciprocity](https://cipr.cass.anu.edu.au/research/publications/calculus-reciprocity-principles-and-implications-aboriginal-sharing)). Kinship binds two groups in mutual obligation. The Dreaming sets the responsibilities that keep the relationships balanced: reciprocity, care for Country as a living relative, the passing of knowledge only with permission ([Beasts of Legend, Kinship, Totems, and Obligation](https://beastsoflegend.com/bestiary/oceania-2/australian-aboriginal/cosmology-dreaming/kinship-totems-and-obligation/)). Custodianship is not ownership you can be generous with. It is a duty to hold and pass on.

This is the frame the modern foundation lost. Giving here is circulation and relationship, not a one-way transfer from a rich pile to a poor one. The wealth moves sideways and keeps moving. The keys do not stay in one hand.

## The modern invention

Now the recent branch. In June 1889 Andrew Carnegie published an essay in the *North American Review*, first titled simply "Wealth," that the world came to call "The Gospel of Wealth" ([Wikipedia, The Gospel of Wealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Gospel_of_Wealth); [Carnegie Corporation, Gospel of Wealth](https://www.carnegie.org/about/our-history/gospelofwealth/)). It set out the responsibility of the new self-made rich. The wealthy man is a trustee of his fortune. He should live without show, leave little to his heirs, and spend the surplus in his lifetime for the general good. Carnegie disapproved of charity that left people where they were and pushed instead for gifts that gave people the means to lift themselves, libraries above all ([American Yawp Reader, Carnegie's Gospel of Wealth](https://www.americanyawp.com/reader/16-capital-and-labor/andrew-carnegies-gospel-of-wealth-june-1889/)).

It reads as humility. Look at what it assumes. The rich man, by virtue of being rich, is the right person to decide what the public good is and how to fund it. The fortune is his to administer. The poor are recipients of his better judgment.

Then came the machine to make it permanent. John D. Rockefeller's adviser Frederick T. Gates called it "scientific philanthropy": apply business rigour to giving, target the root causes rather than the symptoms ([Grokipedia, Rockefeller Foundation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Rockefeller_Foundation); [Wikipedia, Rockefeller Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rockefeller_Foundation)). Rockefeller tried to get the Rockefeller Foundation chartered by the United States Congress. After three years of argument, with the Standard Oil antitrust suit running and deep public suspicion of one family steering that much money, he withdrew the federal bill and took a state charter from New York instead, on 14 May 1913, with an initial endowment of 35 million dollars, another 65 million the next year ([Wikipedia, Rockefeller Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rockefeller_Foundation)). The structure he built, the tax-favoured, perpetual, professionally staffed foundation governing a large endowment, became the template.

So the thing people picture when they hear "philanthropy" is roughly a century old, born in the United States, and contested from its first day. Giving became institutional, perpetual, and a tool to shape society from the top. The waqf had done the perpetual-endowment part a thousand years earlier. What was new in 1913 was the marriage of the perpetual endowment to private industrial fortune, the tax advantage, and the claim that the donor should set the public agenda.

## The critique era

A hundred years on, the critics arrived, and three books from 2018 carry the argument.

Rob Reich's *Just Giving* puts the foundation on trial against democracy. His question is blunt: what is the public justification for a private, perpetual foundation, an institution that takes a tax subsidy, faces little accountability, and can push one wealthy donor's preferences into public life forever ([Just Giving, Princeton University Press / Amazon listing](https://www.amazon.com/Just-Giving-Philanthropy-Failing-Democracy/dp/069118349X); [Inside Philanthropy interview with Reich](https://www.insidephilanthropy.com/home/2019-1-7-impossible-to-justify-a-political-scientist-takes-on-american-philanthropy))? The perpetual endowment is the sharp edge. A foundation set up today can still be spending a dead donor's money, on a dead donor's priorities, generations from now, with the public covering part of the cost through forgone tax.

Anand Giridharadas in *Winners Take All* goes wider. His target is the whole class of "winners" who give back generously while defending the arrangements that made them winners. Philanthropy becomes the way the rich change the world in any direction except the one that would cost them their position ([HistPhil, on Just Giving and Winners Take All](https://histphil.org/2018/12/17/keeping-philanthropy-fully-accountable-in-a-democracy/)).

Edgar Villanueva, of the Lumbee Tribe, wrote *Decolonizing Wealth* from inside the grantmaking world. His frame is older than the foundation: money as medicine, a thing that heals when it circulates and harms when it is hoarded. His prescription is to give control back to the communities the money was extracted from, to put decision-making in their hands rather than the funder's ([Decolonizing Wealth, on the three-book critique](https://www.reviews4radicals.org/book-reviews/the-pitchforks-are-coming-for-philanthropy))). Villanueva is reaching back past Carnegie to the gift economy and the obligation traditions, money that moves, decisions held by the people closest to the harm.

## Contemporary shifts

The practice is moving with the critique, unevenly.

Participatory grantmaking and trust-based philanthropy both try to break the donor's grip on the decision. Participatory grantmaking hands the funding decisions to the communities affected. Trust-based philanthropy gives multi-year, unrestricted money and strips out the reporting that treats grantees as suspects. Community philanthropy builds funds owned and controlled from the bottom up, where a community names its own needs and pools its own resources, in cash or in kind, to meet them ([Mott Foundation, expanding community philanthropy in Africa](https://www.mott.org/news/articles/qa-expanding-community-philanthropy-in-africa/)).

The 2020 pandemic pushed mutual aid back into view, neighbours organising direct support for neighbours, outside any foundation, closer to the kula and the langar than to the grant cycle.

MacKenzie Scott took the trust-based idea and ran it at a scale nobody had tried. Since 2020 she has given roughly 19 billion dollars to more than 2,000 organisations with no strings: no application in many cases, no reporting requirements, no limit on when the money must be spent ([Grantmakers In Health, on MacKenzie Scott's grants](https://www.gih.org/publication/advancing-health-and-creating-lasting-impact-mackenzie-scotts-grants-to-health-foundations/); [CEP, research on Scott's unrestricted gifts](https://cep.org/news/press-releases/research-shows-mackenzie-scotts-large-unrestricted-gifts-create-sustained-impact-for-nonprofits-and-communities/)). The model is a deliberate rebuke to the controlling foundation: trust the people doing the work, then get out of the way.

At the other pole, effective altruism tried to make giving a calculation, fund the causes that research shows do the most measurable good per dollar. Then its biggest funder collapsed. Sam Bankman-Fried, a public face of the movement, ran FTX, which filed for bankruptcy on 11 November 2022, vaporising billions earmarked for EA causes and calling the whole donor philosophy into question ([Fortune, FTX collapse and effective altruism](https://fortune.com/2022/11/14/bankman-fried-ftx-collapse-threatens-effective-altruism-billions-charity-philanthropy/); [WBUR On Point, FTX and effective altruism](https://www.wbur.org/onpoint/2022/12/21/what-is-the-effective-altruism-movement)). The optimising, head-over-heart model hit its crisis in public.

## Global picture

The American foundation is the exception, not the rule. Look around.

The United States runs the foundation-and-tax model: private perpetual endowments, a charitable deduction, hundreds of billions held in endowments ([HistPhil, on the critique books](https://histphil.org/2018/12/17/keeping-philanthropy-fully-accountable-in-a-democracy/)). That is the version most people mean by "philanthropy," and it is geographically narrow.

The Gulf and the wider Muslim world give through *zakat* and *waqf* at large scale, obligation and endowment rather than discretionary largesse, the system that built hospitals and universities centuries before the Carnegie libraries ([Islamic Relief, What is waqf](https://islamic-relief.org/news/what-is-waqf-and-how-does-it-work/)).

India made corporate giving law. Section 135 of the Companies Act 2013 requires large companies to spend at least 2 per cent of their average net profit on social responsibility. In FY 2023-24, 27,188 companies ran 59,633 projects under the rule, spending about 34,908 crore rupees ([UNESCO, Mandatory CSR in India](https://www.unesco.org/en/dtc-financing-toolkit/mandatory-corporate-social-responsibility-csr-india); [ClearTax, CSR under Section 135](https://cleartax.in/s/corporate-social-responsibility)). India turned giving back into obligation by statute.

China runs a state-adjacent model. The 2016 Charity Law set the legal ground for the sector, but the state actively promotes its own version, a state-led, mobilisation-style philanthropy aimed at social cohesion and at competing with independent NGOs for influence ([Library of Congress, China Charity Law adopted](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2016-05-17/china-charity-law-adopted/); [USALI, Rebooting China's Charity Law](https://usali.org/usali-perspectives-blog/rebooting-chinas-charity-law)). Giving routes through the state.

Africa carries the community philanthropy movement and horizontal giving: people give to each other in kind, time, goods, networks, rallying in hard times, and build their own foundations controlled from the bottom up, the Kenya Community Development Foundation among the early ones, from 1997 ([Mott Foundation, community philanthropy in Africa](https://www.mott.org/news/articles/qa-expanding-community-philanthropy-in-africa/); [Kenya Community Development Foundation](https://kcdf.or.ke/)). The flow is sideways, neighbour to neighbour, not down from a pile.

Latin America leans on family and faith. The Catholic Church remains a leading recipient and channel, and underwrites many of the region's human rights organisations and grassroots movements, alongside a growing set of family foundations, with Mexico and Colombia each home to close to 100 grantmaking foundations ([Harvard ReVista, Latin American Philanthropy in Changing Times](https://revista.drclas.harvard.edu/latin-american-philanthropy-in-changing-times/)).

Five regions, five shapes. Only one of them looks like the Gospel of Wealth.

## The ACT frame

Put the history in one line and the campaign writes itself. The tax-advantaged perpetual foundation is recent, narrow, and Anglo-American. It was born around 1913, in one country, contested from the start, and it is the way a small slice of the planet gives. For most of human history, and across most of the world today, the gift was something else. It was *tzedakah*, justice owed. It was *zakat* and *waqf*, obligation and endowment held in trust. It was *dana* and *seva* and the *langar* floor where rich and poor eat the same food. It was the kula valuable that must never stop circling, the potlatch that raises you by giving away, the Aboriginal kin rule that says sharing is duty and Country is a relative you are bound to. The gift was obligation, reciprocity, justice, and relationship. It moved sideways. It kept moving. It left the keys in many hands, not one.

That older shape is closer to how A Curious Tractor works than the Gospel of Wealth ever was. Design for our own obsolescence. Hand over the keys. Put the decision with the people closest to the work and get out of the way. None of that is new. It is what giving was before it became a foundation. The Confessions to Philanthropy campaign asks the sector a question with a long answer behind it: before the endowment, before the deduction, before the donor's name went on the wall, what was the gift for? It was for keeping each other alive in relationship. Philanthropy can remember that. It used to know.
