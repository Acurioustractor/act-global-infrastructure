## 1. What is this report

**COLI = The Cost of Late Intervention.** The "24" is the year. It is the 2024 update to a 2019 baseline report, costing what Australian governments spend each year on statutory, acute, and essential services for children and young people 0-24 when they are already in crisis.

Published by **The Front Project** (Melbourne, philanthropically-funded early childhood policy org), authored by M. O'Connell, released 2025 with 2023-24 data. **Supported by Minderoo Foundation** (logo on inside cover). The original 2019 study was a joint project between CoLab (Telethon Kids Institute + Minderoo), the Front Project, the UK's Early Intervention Foundation (now Foundations), and Woodside Energy. Minderoo co-parented this methodology.

Audience: federal and state treasurers, ministers, policy teams, philanthropic sector.

## 2. Headline findings

- Australia spends **$22.3 billion a year** on late intervention for 0-24s, up from $15.2bn in 2019 (p. i, p. 8).
- $838 per Australian per year, $2,704 per child or young person 0-24 (p. i).
- **Child protection alone is $10.2bn (43% of total)**, up from $5.9bn in 2019, real increase of 74% despite stable out-of-home care numbers (p. iii, p. 9, p. 24).
- Youth justice spend grew 81%, family violence spend grew 110% between 2019 and 2024 in real terms, while offender numbers fell (p. 24).
- States and territories bear 86% of late intervention spend ($19.2bn state vs $3.1bn Commonwealth); Commonwealth captures long-term benefits through tax and reduced welfare (p. 11, p. 21).
- More than half of young people at government secondary schools miss a month or more of school per year; attendance rate has fallen over the last decade, steepest in low-SES, regional, remote, and Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children (p. 16).

## 3. Core areas of need (nine domains)

In order of current annual cost:

1. **Child Protection ($10.2bn, 43%)**: out-of-home care, protective intervention, intensive family support. 45,000+ children in OoHC, 60,000+ on care orders.
2. **Youth Crime ($3.4bn, 14%)**: police, court, custody, health for offenders 10-17. 48,000+ offenders.
3. **Youth Unemployment / NEET ($2.5bn, 11%)**: Youth Allowance, JobSeeker, Rent Assistance, Special Benefit 16-24. **13.4% not fully engaged in work or study, up from 12.6% a decade ago.**
4. **Youth and Adult Justice combined ($2.2bn)**: youth $1.5bn, adult 18-24 $0.7bn.
5. **Youth Homelessness ($1.8bn)**: 17,600+ under 12, 28,200+ 12-24 homeless (2021 Census).
6. **Mental Health and Substance Misuse ($1.6bn)**.
7. **Physical Health ($1.2bn)**: preventable hospitalisations.
8. **Family Violence ($0.6bn direct to children)**: real spend has doubled since 2019.
9. **Adult Justice 18-24 ($0.7bn)**.

Cross-cutting framework: **"The Nest" (ARACY 2025)** with six preconditions: Valued Loved and Safe, Material Basics, Healthy, Learning, **Participating**, Identity and Culture. Each issue is mapped to which precondition fails and which sub-system should respond.

## 4. Indigenous community and employment angles

The report is **not structured around First Nations content**. Explicit Indigenous references are sparse but load-bearing:

- **NT is highest-spending jurisdiction per head ($2,808 vs $921 QLD)**, driven by remoteness, workforce shortages, **"levels of Indigeneity"** (report's phrasing), and complex community needs (p. 20, p. 23).
- NT has the highest proportion of population in remote or very remote locations (~40%) and most disadvantaged households (~30%).
- **Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children named as more likely to miss school regularly**, alongside low-SES and regional/remote students (p. 16).
- **"Participating" is the Nest precondition repeatedly flagged as failing** across youth unemployment, homelessness, crime, justice. Eight of nine issue pillars mark "Participating" as a failure.
- **NEET trend is the worrying one**: unemployment fell from 13.5% to 9.2%, but full disengagement from work and study rose. "Young people not fully engaged in work or study by age 24 have a high propensity to remain disengaged throughout their lives" (p. 15).

**No material in this report on First Nations procurement, Indigenous business development, CDP/CDEP replacement, or community enterprise.** Closest adjacent framing: the "Participating" precondition and NEET employment-trajectory data.

## 6. What the report says is NOT working

Position Goods against these:

- **System is reactive, not preventive**. Cost rising even where volumes stable.
- **Workforce shortages and service blockages driving costs up**: child protection cost up 40% with stable OoHC numbers; cost per youth detention place up 50%.
- **Vertical fiscal imbalance breaks the incentive to invest early**: "benefits run across many others, reducing the incentive for ministers and departments to invest" (p. 10).
- **Data infrastructure is inadequate**: no comprehensive data on school refusal, disability spend, prevalence.
- **Single-issue programs miss compounding risk**: siloed services vs "stacked interventions".
- **School disengagement worsening but invisible in the cost model** — especially Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander, regional, remote.
- **NDIS absorbing demand that should have been prevented**: children 0-8 now 23% of NDIS participants.
- **No coordinated national early intervention roadmap**: "a comprehensive roadmap is needed that identifies early intervention opportunities (across the life course and portfolios)" (p. 24).

## How this anchors the Minderoo pitch in one line

Minderoo is a named supporter of CoLI24, so this report is effectively Minderoo's statement of what they consider "the problem" in dollar terms. Goods on Country does not sit inside any of the nine late-intervention cost domains directly, so anchor the pitch on:

- **(a)** The "Participating" precondition the report flags as failing for almost every youth cohort;
- **(b)** The NEET trajectory (13.4% and rising);
- **(c)** The NT cost-per-head story that links remoteness, Indigeneity, and disadvantage to spending pressure;
- **(d)** The report's own statement that wrap-around, stacked, evidence-based early intervention is what shifts trajectories.

Position Goods as **the demand-side early-intervention lever for Indigenous employment and enterprise that the report implicitly calls for but does not itself supply.**
