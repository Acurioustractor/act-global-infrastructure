# Snow Airport — Stretch Bed display copy

Five panels. Each one a single moment. The reader walks past, glances, reads, walks on. The bed in front of them carries the rest.

---

## Panel 1 — The bed

```
This bed weighs 26 kilos.
Holds 200.
Assembles in five minutes with no tools.
Washes down with a garden hose.
Lasts ten years in country that destroys an Aldi bed in six weeks.

Made of recycled HDPE plastic.
Made on Country.
Made to last.
```

---

## Panel 2 — Where it came from

```
Designed in Tennant Creek.
Tested in Alice Springs.
Refined on Palm Island.

The Stretch Bed was not designed in a Sydney studio.
It was designed by sitting down with Elders and asking
what a bed has to do to survive the conditions inside
a remote-community house.

Fred Campbell, Western Arrernte man, signed off on
the cultural appropriateness of the design.

The bed in front of you is the result.
```

---

## Panel 3 — Why it matters

```
In remote Aboriginal communities, families sleep on the floor.
The bed lasted six weeks. The mattress lasted four.
The kids are tired at school. The replacement budget is gone.
The next bed will not arrive this year.

A bed that lasts ten years rewrites that maths.
That is what this one is built for.

389 beds deployed so far.
9,225 kilograms of plastic diverted from landfill.
8 communities on the map.
```

---

## Panel 4 — Who makes it

```
Goods on Country is not Indigenous-owned.
It partners with Indigenous-owned organisations.

Oonchiumpa Consultancy in Mparntwe — the manufacturing partner.
Palm Island Community Company — 141 beds deployed, expansion in negotiation.
Centrecorp Foundation in Tennant Creek — 107 beds in production for Utopia Homelands.

The communities are co-designers. Not recipients.

The next plant goes into Mparntwe on Arrernte Country, run by Aboriginal staff,
with the unit cost cut by a third at scale.
```

---

## Panel 5 — The room you are standing in

```
This week is National Reconciliation Week.

The work of reconciliation is concrete.
A bed that does not break is concrete.
A plant on Country, staffed by Aboriginal workers, is concrete.
A 26-kilo recycled plastic frame in a kid's bedroom on Palm Island is concrete.

Snow Foundation makes this work possible.

Talk to Ben Knight — benjamin@act.place — about what comes next.
```

## Notes for the design team

- Each panel works on its own. Read in order, the panels move from the bed itself → the design story → the social context → the partners → reconciliation as concrete action.
- The bed should be visible from each panel position if possible. The copy assumes that.
- Snow Foundation logo and Goods on Country mark at the foot of each panel.
- If only three panels fit the install, use Panels 1, 3, 5. If four, drop Panel 2.
- Font: serif for the line breaks to read as breath, not as design. Avoid stacked sans condensed — the copy is doing the work.
- Photography (if any): the bed itself, the Oonchiumpa team, an Aunty's hand. Never stock.
