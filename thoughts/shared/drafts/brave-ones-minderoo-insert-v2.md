# The Brave Ones

*A journal, a journey, and an exchange for young people who have already been counted out.*

---

[PHOTO: Judges on Country, Atnarpa, 21 April 2026. Real photograph. No stock imagery in this envelope.]

---

Last month Kristy Bloomfield took eight young people out to Atnarpa for three days on Country. On the last night one of the boys — 15 years old, on the police radar since he was 12 — said to her: *"Aunty, can we come back next week?"*

That is the sentence this program is built around. Not a policy ask. A young person who spent three years being watched asking to keep showing up.

Oonchiumpa knows the method works because it already runs. 95% diversion from detention. 72% school re-engagement. 97.6% less cost than a cell. Three of those eight boys are back at school. Two are in jobs. All eight are off the police list.

The Brave Ones makes the method portable, visible, and hard to look away from.

---

## What it is

**A journal.** A5, hardcover. Theirs to keep. iPad beside it locked to Procreate and Voice Memos. Most of these kids prefer painting or talking over writing. The goal state is simple — a young person walks in and says, *"Can I grab my journal?"*

**An exchange.** The journal practice unlocks travel. Alice Springs to Mount Isa. Mount Isa to Sydney. Four kids, two workers, three days. By Year 2 the exchanges go international — Diagrama in Spain (13.6% recidivism over 30 years), YOPE in Amsterdam. These young people have never been on an aeroplane.

**A book.** Drawings, transcripts, paintings, audio — compiled into physical books. One per community. One national collection. Not written about them. Made by them. Goes to magistrates, ministers, funders. A physical object that refuses to be a statistic.

**Alumni.** Graduates become peer mentors for the next cohort. Over three years this turns into a national network of young advocates speaking from experience, not from interview transcripts.

---

## The young people these journals are for

Jackquann is 14, from Upper Camp in Mparntwe. On detention he says, *"At six o'clock you get locked down. You wait till tomorrow."* On what keeps him out now, *"Looking after my family."*

Nigel is 14, from Double Camp. He wants to play AFL. He can see the path. *"When I'm talking to the judge, I feel like I'm panicking."*

Laquisha is 16. The NT sent her 1,500 kilometres to Darwin because Central Australia has no youth detention facility. *"I don't like going to Darwin because I have no family there."* Asked what sits underneath all of this, she said one word: *"Oppression."*

Xavier was told in formal reports that he had "limited ability to understand." He built a Stretch Bed from recycled plastic that the community now uses for ceremonies and bush trips. Fred Campbell, Xavier's case worker, puts the method in one line: *"He trusts us. We earned that trust."*

As Tanya Turner says: *"Our young people are just collateral in a bigger issue. The issue doesn't sit with them. It sits on a much broader level and with adults, not with children."*

The Brave Ones is an artefact of what those young people have to say when the adults who hold the pen hand it back.

---

## The pilot

Oonchiumpa, Mparntwe. June 2026. Proceeding on existing budget — this is a proof point, not a promise.

- Journals start June
- Brisbane inspiration trip (June 8–13) is the first domestic exchange
- First data flows to Empathy Ledger by August
- Pilot review September
- First inter-community exchange by December

---

## The cost

One iPad costs $1,500. One night in youth detention costs $3,644. For the price of one week in detention, you equip a community with journals for a year.

The Brave Ones sits inside the $2.9M Three Circles envelope, not on top of it. It is the daily practice that makes Circle 2 tangible, the source material that makes Circle 3 inevitable, and the evidence that the program is real.
