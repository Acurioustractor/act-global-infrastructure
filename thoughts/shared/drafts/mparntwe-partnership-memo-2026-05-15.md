# The Mparntwe production plant

## Who Oonchiumpa is

Oonchiumpa Consultancy and Services sits on Arrernte Country at Atnarpa, the Bloomfield family's ancestral station east of Mparntwe.

Kristy Bloomfield runs the operation. Tanya Turner leads the program work. Fred Campbell, Western Arrernte man, sits across product design and cultural protocol.

The organisation is 100% Aboriginal-staffed. It is not a contracting body. It is the community partner that ACT works with on Country, and the partner that decides what gets made and how.

## What we are building together

A containerised production plant in Mparntwe. Output 1,500 to 1,800 Stretch Beds per year at first run. Six to eight Aboriginal staff on the floor. Recycled HDPE feedstock sourced locally where possible, supplemented by Envirobank.

Two years of co-design lie behind this. The Stretch Bed itself was tested on Country before it was a commercial product. Fred Campbell signed off on the cultural appropriateness of every product line. Aunty Dianne Stokes named the washing machine in Warumungu.

This is not a contract-manufacturing relationship. The plant is co-owned operationally: Oonchiumpa runs the line, ACT runs the supply chain and the books, both parties hold a stake in the unit economics.

## Why a plant changes the maths

At small-batch production the Stretch Bed delivers at $550 to $650 per unit. At plant scale, with 5,000+ units across the network annually, delivered cost drops to about $350. Institutional sale price holds at $700 to $850.

That margin is the difference between Goods as a pilot and Goods as an enterprise.

The same maths runs in the other direction for impact. At plant scale, the marginal cost of placing a bed in a remote community drops by a third. The communities most in need are the ones where the unit cost was previously prohibitive.

## What is already in place

- The first facility on Jinibara Country (Queensland) is operational. 1,500-bed annual capacity, six FTE roles in design.
- The Mparntwe site has been confirmed with Oonchiumpa. Site preparation underway.
- 200 beds were delivered to Tennant Creek communities in 2025. Invoices closed: Centrecorp $37,620, Our Community Shed $13,500, Julalikari $19,800. Total $70,920 paid, ledger clean.
- 141 beds deployed on Palm Island through PICC partnership.
- 107 beds in production with Defy Design for Utopia Homelands (Centrecorp tranche).
- Aunty Dianne Stokes (Warumungu) named the washing machine. 20 washers across communities to date.

Goods publishes delivered unit cost monthly against the decision band: ≥25% scale, 15–24% workable, 5–14% red flag, <5% stop. We will not pitch capital without that number on the page. The first run of the report against the last 50 beds lands [DATE]. We can show QBE the actuals at that point.

## The honest line

A bed in a remote community lasts six weeks today. Goods is the bed that lasts ten years. Mparntwe is where we make enough of them to matter.
