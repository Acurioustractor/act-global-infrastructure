## What this is

On Tuesday 21 April 2026, 55 judges and magistrates, including at least one High Court judge and Indigenous magistrates, come onto Eastern Arrernte Country at Atnarpa homestead, Loves Creek Station, 80km east of Mparntwe (Alice Springs). They are hosted by Oonchiumpa, under the authority of Aunty Bev and Uncle Terry, and led by co-directors Kristy Bloomfield and Tanya Turner.

This is what the day is for, what the bench is being asked to see, and what we ask them to do next.

## What the bench saw

Three young people, and the Country that holds them:

1. **Xavier.** Formal reports said Xavier had "limited ability to understand." Oonchiumpa bet on a different version of his capability. He built a Stretch Bed from recycled plastic that the community now uses for ceremony, for bush trips, for sorry business. What this shows the bench: the system's assessment of capability is often wrong. A community-led program that stays long enough sees what the system misses. Fred Campbell, case worker: *"He trusts us. We earned that trust."*

2. **The 15-year-old from Atnarpa.** In March 2026 Kristy took eight young people out to Country for three days. On the last night, a 15-year-old boy who had been on the police radar since he was 12 asked her: *"Aunty, can we come back next week?"* Three of those eight are now back at school. Two are in jobs. All eight are off the police list. What this shows the bench: Country is the method, not the backdrop. Culture is the intervention, not the decoration on top of it.

3. **Jackquann and Nigel.** Both 14. Both from Mparntwe camps. Jackquann on detention: *"At six o'clock you get locked down. You wait till tomorrow."* Jackquann on what keeps him out now: *"Looking after my family."* Nigel on court: *"When I'm talking to the judge, I feel like I'm panicking."* Nigel wants to play AFL, knows school attendance is the path, has someone walking it with him. What this shows the bench: when a young person is given the chance to explain their world in their own words, the explanation is already there. The system's job is to listen before it sentences.

Alongside the three: Atnarpa itself. Country as evidence. The Bloomfield family's reclaimed ancestral land, held under Elder authority, running the program that Oonchiumpa is.

## The numbers, where the numbers help

- **95% diversion** from the justice system. 72% school re-engagement.
- **97.6% less cost** than detaining one child for one year. Australia spends over $1.3M per child per year in detention with reoffending above 80%.
- **Operation Luna:** 20 of 21 high-risk young people in Alice Springs came off the list within a year of Oonchiumpa working alongside NT Police.
- **7 language groups** served. **150km** homeland radius. **21** active young people right now.

## What Oonchiumpa is, in one paragraph

Oonchiumpa is a 100% Aboriginal-led youth civic agency in Mparntwe, grounding young people in culture, Country, and identity across seven Central Australian language groups. Cultural authority is held by Aunty Bev and Uncle Terry. The central site is Atnarpa homestead on Loves Creek Station, the Bloomfield family's reclaimed ancestral Country. The program stays when other services leave. That is the method.

## What we are asking the judiciary to do next

1. **Before sentencing an Indigenous young person,** ask what community-led programs exist within their kinship and Country. Oonchiumpa can help identify them.
2. **Open a public conversation** about community-led alternatives. One judge, one magistrate, within 60 days.
3. **Shape the tool.** JusticeHub is building a magistrate-facing service-directory prototype. The bench's questions from Tuesday define what it has to answer.
