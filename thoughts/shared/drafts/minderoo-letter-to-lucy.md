# Body

Lucy,

This is already running. In April 2026, 54 Australian judges and magistrates travelled to Atnarpa, Mparntwe, to sit with Oonchiumpa. They watched the young people of Oonchiumpa lead the welcome. They carried postcards back to chambers. The postcards, the video Kristy and Tanya made with the young people, and the live field guide that lets a magistrate in Townsville search for alternatives near their own court are all at justicehub.com.au/judges-on-country.

The Three Circles is what it costs to do this in seven communities instead of one.

The seven First Nations and First-Nations-led communities in this proposal are already doing the work Minderoo wants to support. Four are confirmed. Oonchiumpa in Mparntwe. BG Fit in Cherbourg. PICC in South Australia. MMEIC on Minjerribah. Three more we want to co-select with you.

They are running the alternatives the Heckman curve rewards. They are the people the $22.3 billion late-intervention bar is being spent against, building the answer to it without that money. They are not asking for funding to start. They are asking for the kind of trust that lets them keep going, document what works, and lend it to the next seven communities behind them.

The Three Circles is what it costs to back them properly. $2.9M over three years. $780K Year One, $960K Year Two, $1.16M Year Three.

**Why this is aligned with Minderoo, not adjacent to it.** The young people in these seven communities are the same young people Thrive by Five is trying to reach in the first five years. Generation One is what waits for them when they walk into work instead of remand. The Three Circles backs the years between, in places where the community has already designed the alternative. Same prevention thesis. Same return-on-investment logic. Same kids. We are not asking Minderoo to pick up a new portfolio. We are asking Minderoo to back the seven communities the rest of the portfolio is already trying to reach.

**The lenses Minderoo brings to all its work, applied here.** Every confirmed anchor is First Nations or First-Nations-led. Story sovereignty is enforced at the data layer through OCAP and CARE protocols, not just stated in the pitch. The voices that anchor the artefact are run by women and elders as often as by men: Tanya, Kristy, Aunty Ethel, Rachael, Laquisha, Aunty Bev. The same First Nations and gender lenses Minderoo applies across the 2030 strategy are built into who holds this work, who is heard, and who decides what counts as evidence.

**What gets built underneath the seven.** A live database, CivicGraph, that tracks who funds what across government, philanthropy, NDIS, child protection, disability and corrections, so the next funder and the next minister and the next magistrate can see what these communities have already proven. A live map, JusticeHub, of every verified youth justice intervention in the country, currently 1,155 of them. Six AI agent tools so funders, policymakers and communities can ask plain-language questions of the data and get back evidence. The data spine is already running. Year One hardens it. Year Three opens parts of it to the sector.

**What comes out at the end.** The artefact is how the seven communities' work travels. A documentary. A published Stay Series of seven method volumes. A touring exhibition. A set of hand-bound journals, one per young person. The artefact is the receipt for what these communities have already done. It is also the form in which the next seven communities will pick it up.

**The ask.** $2.9M over three years, with Option 1 as the easiest yes because it is what the seven communities already voted for by showing up. Two stepped alternatives are on the table if Year One needs to prove the model first.
