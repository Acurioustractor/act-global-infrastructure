# Goods on Country

A family in Maningrida sleeps on the floor. The bed lasted six weeks. The mattress lasted four. The kids are tired at school. The replacement budget is gone. The next bed will not arrive this year.

This is the room Goods works in.

## What we make

We make beds that do not break. We make washing machines that survive twelve months of red dust. We make fridges for hot houses where the wall socket is fifteen years old.

The Stretch Bed weighs 26 kilos. Holds 200. Assembles in five minutes with no tools. Washes down with a garden hose. Every piece is replaceable. It is made of recycled HDPE plastic, UV-stabilised, built to last ten years in country that destroys an Aldi bed in six weeks.

The bed was not designed in an office. It was co-designed with Elders in Tennant Creek, tested in Alice Springs, refined on Palm Island. Fred Campbell, Western Arrernte man, consults on every product line.

## Who makes it with us

Goods is not an Indigenous-owned organisation. We partner with Indigenous-owned ones.

- **Oonchiumpa Consultancy** (Mparntwe) — 100% Aboriginal-staffed, our manufacturing partner for the Central Australian containerised plant. Two years of co-design. Kristy Bloomfield and Tanya Turner lead.
- **Palm Island Community Company** — 141 beds deployed. Housing expansion in active negotiation. PICC sits with us in the REAL Innovation Fund consortium.
- **Centrecorp Foundation** — approved 107 beds for Utopia Homelands. Tranche 2 active.
- **Wilya Janta** (Simon Quilty) — fridges requested for Norm's community.

The communities are co-designers. Not recipients.

## What we have built

- **389 products deployed across 8 communities**
- **9,225 kilograms of plastic diverted from landfill**
- **33 community storytellers documenting the work through Empathy Ledger**
- **$445,685 in philanthropic funding received** (Snow Foundation, Vincent Fairfax, FRRR, AMP Spark, The Funding Network)
- **$537,595 total revenue invoiced through A Curious Tractor Pty Ltd**
- **141 beds on Palm Island**, 200 in Tennant Creek (delivered 2025), 107 building now for Utopia
- **20 industrial washing machines deployed**, named in language by Aunty Dianne Stokes (Warumungu Elder)

## What we are asking for

Production scale. A second containerised plant in Mparntwe takes the unit cost from $550 to $350. That is the difference between a one-off pilot and a national social enterprise.

The ask sits across three tiers:

- **$50K** — keeps a community deployment moving (one pallet of beds plus install)
- **$250K** — funds a year of the Oonchiumpa partnership, including Fred's consultation work and the Mparntwe production line
- **$900K over three years** — closes the gap to plant-scale economics and a workforce of eight Aboriginal staff in Mparntwe

The Stretch Bed sells institutionally at $700 to $850 per unit. Gross margin at scale lands at 50 to 55 percent. The model is real.

## Why now

The Mparntwe plant is half-built. The Palm Island bed contract is open for expansion. The 13-person Goods Advisory Group is reconvening next month with Sally Grimsley-Ballard (Snow), Judith Meiklejohn (Orange Sky First Nations Communities), Kristy Bloomfield (Oonchiumpa), Daniel Pittman (Zinus), Adam Demmler (CYP), Corey Tutt (Deadly Science).

Snow Foundation is installing a Stretch Bed at the airport for Reconciliation Week. Brian M Davis Charitable Foundation, Bryan Foundation, and the TABOO / Butterfly / Inclusive Employment cluster in Adelaide are all in proposal stage.

The pieces are in place. The next $250K to $900K decides whether this is a pilot or an enterprise.
