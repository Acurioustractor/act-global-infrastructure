# Brave Ones — insights from Oonchiumpa young-people stories

> Six young voices, one elder, two founders. Read in sequence, they do something a pitch deck never does on its own — they relocate the definition of bravery from "surviving the system" to "naming what the system is doing," from "good behaviour" to "showing up for family." Brave Ones as a campaign lands only if it trusts these voices to lead.

## The young people in their own words

| Storyteller | Age, Place | The line that does the work |
|---|---|---|
| **Jackquann** | 14, Upper Camp, Mparntwe | *"At six o'clock you get locked down. You wait till tomorrow."* And: *"Looking after my family."* |
| **Nigel** | 14, Double Camp, Mparntwe | *"When I'm talking to the judge, I feel like I'm panicking."* |
| **Laquisha** | 16, Mparntwe (sent to Darwin detention) | *"I don't like going to Darwin because I have no family there."* And, on the root cause: *"Oppression."* |
| **Xavier** | (age not stated), Mparntwe | Written off as "limited ability to understand." Built a Stretch Bed the whole community uses. |
| **Fred Campbell** (worker voice) | Oonchiumpa case worker | *"He trusts us. We earned that trust."* |

## Four patterns the corpus surfaces

### Pattern 1 — Young people name the system before the system names itself

Laquisha is 16. Asked what sits underneath her contact with the justice system, she says one word: **"Oppression."** She uses a concept policy papers hedge around and names it in a sentence a child can say. That is not vulnerability to be protected from. It is analysis to be listened to.

Brave Ones' job is to make that naming land. The portrait series should not soften the word. It should put it on the wall.

### Pattern 2 — The bravery the system looks for is the wrong bravery

Courts and corrections look for "good behaviour." The brave act these young people describe is much smaller and much harder:

- Naming the panic instead of hiding it (Nigel)
- Choosing the family responsibility over the street (Jackquann)
- Telling the truth about a 1,500 km plane trip to a building of strangers (Laquisha)
- Building a thing after being told you can't (Xavier)

None of those are the bravery that makes a news headline. All of them are what Brave Ones needs to photograph.

**Implication for the Brave Ones portrait set:** pair face with the young person's own sentence, not a caption written by a campaign. The caption is their line. The portrait is the counter-mugshot because the subject is the one telling you who they are.

### Pattern 3 — Family and Country are the operating system, not a value statement

- Jackquann on what keeps him out: *"Looking after my family."*
- Laquisha on what hurts: *"I have no family there."*
- Henry Bloomfield (67, grandfather), on returning to Atnarpa: *"Seventeen grandkids and six kids. The whole family working together."*
- Nigel, on the path he can see: school, footy training, someone to walk it with — all located in Mparntwe, all Double Camp.

The system's response — detention in Darwin, 1,500 km from home — is the precise opposite of what the young people say works. The Brave Ones campaign's political bite is that the young people's own theory of change contradicts the system's stated method. That gap is the force-a-trap paragraph for the Minderoo pitch opener.

### Pattern 4 — Trust is the unit of work, not services

Fred's *"He trusts us. We earned that trust"* is the method. The founders' *"We responded with cultural connection, practical support, and the authority that comes from being their Elders"* (Kristy & Tanya's canonical framing) is the same method at community scale.

The Brave Ones portraits that carry this should include Fred, Kristy, and Tanya in the set — not as staff, but as the trust holders. A portrait of a young person without the trust that earned the photo is just another image.

## How these feed the Brave Ones campaign concretely

### A. Portrait-caption structure

Each portrait in the series carries three layers of copy, not one:

1. **Name, age, place** (quiet, Arrernte and English, place named Indigenous-first)
2. **The young person's own line** (no edit, no paraphrase — their sentence, as recorded)
3. **One frame-line** drawn from the corpus, naming what the image refuses

Example, Laquisha:

> **Laquisha, 16 — Mparntwe**
>
> *"I don't like going to Darwin because I have no family there."*
>
> Australia sent her 1,500 kilometres from home for behaviour. She named the root cause herself: oppression. This is not a mugshot. This is the girl who told the truth before the policy papers did.

### B. Opening move for the Minderoo envelope

Use **Jackquann's two lines together** as the opener that does the work in one paragraph:

> Jackquann is 14, from Upper Camp, Mparntwe. On detention he says, *"At six o'clock you get locked down. You wait till tomorrow."* On what keeps him out now, he says, *"Looking after my family." Australia spends $1.3 million a year to lock that first sentence in. Oonchiumpa spends $31,000 a year to hold the second sentence together.*

That paragraph is force-a-trap: every sentence the Minderoo reader can ask about is sourced to a 14-year-old's own words or ROGS 2024–25. There is no clean policy counter.

### C. Judges on Country one-pager

The 21 April trip put 55 judges on Country hearing from Oonchiumpa. This corpus is the post-trip evidence of what they heard. The one-pager should quote three young people (Jackquann, Nigel, Laquisha) and one worker (Fred), titled in a way that makes the judges the audience accountable: *"What we said, when you listened."*

### D. autoreason pool for campaign writing

These seven stories now sit in `wiki/stories/` and are automatically in autoreason's story-anchor pre-pass (Gap 2 of the review). Any future Minderoo-adjacent draft autoreason produces will be judged against the question: *does this draft carry at least one named storyteller by name, place, and quote traceable to the corpus?* Unanchored pitch prose now loses the Borda vote by design.

## Ideas this unlocks for Brave Ones beyond portraits

1. **The postcards**. Each young person's story is titled "judges-on-country-postcards" in its metadata — the series is already framed as postcards. Produce them physically as A6 cards: image one side, quote and name the other. Mail to the 55 judges post-trip with a *"remember what they said"* cover card. Postcard series = ongoing touch through the FY2026 cycle.

2. **Paired portraits — young person + trust-holder**. Jackquann + Fred. Laquisha + Kristy. Xavier + whoever was there when he built the bed. The photograph refuses the single-figure mugshot frame entirely; the young person is never alone in the image because they are never alone in the method.

3. **The audio cut**. The quotes above are from interviews. An audio artefact — three minutes of the young people in their own voices (not re-recorded) played under a single static portrait — lands differently than a photograph. Budget line in the $15–30K Phase 1 release.

4. **Xavier's Stretch Bed as the object artefact**. A young person written off by the system built a thing the community uses. That object, photographed, with Xavier's permission, with a short caption in his own words — is the single most powerful visual in the series. An object that exists is harder to deny than an outcome statistic.

## New anchors from the extended sync (2026-04-18 pm)

The extended consent apply (privacy_level upgrade + elder_approved_at set per Kristy's Traditional Owner authority) brought five more Oonchiumpa stories through. Three of them add Brave-Ones-relevant material the hand-authored postcards did not carry:

### Kristy in her own voice — "Bringing Kids Back to Country"

Kristy describes taking eight young people out to Atnarpa for three days. The line that does the most work comes from a 15-year-old at the end of the trip:

> *"Aunty, can we come back next week?"*

Compare to the usual frame: a young person on the police radar, with a history of trouble since age 12, at the end of a "program" asking not to go back to town but to stay on Country. The Brave Ones campaign has a new anchor quote that is simpler and quieter than any of the earlier ones — no statement about the system, just a request to come back. The bravery here is the adult method (taking the trip) AND the young person's trust (asking for more of it).

Supporting data from the same story: of those eight, three returned to school, two got jobs, all eight are off the police radar. Monthly trips are the cadence. The cost comparison is in the text but the dollar signs are currently mangled in the EL v2 row (content reads `,000` and `,852` — looks like a $2,000 per-trip vs $1,852 per-night-detention comparison with dollar signs stripped by a prior import). **Data-quality flag for Ben: worth fixing the source text in EL v2 before the envelope ships so anyone quoting the line quotes it correctly.**

### Kylie Bloomfield — "Creating Our Own History"

Kylie adds a Brave-Ones-adjacent theme the youth postcards don't capture: the *reciprocal* impact of on-country visits. She talks about welcoming interstate school groups and a young visitor asking:

> *"What can I change in my privileged life to benefit here?"*

This matters for Brave Ones because it flips the camera. The series is not only about Indigenous young people being seen — it is about what being seen produces in the seer. That is a much sharper campaign claim than "meet these young people" — it is "meet these young people, because your life has a question in it you didn't know to ask."

Other Kylie lines worth noting:
- *"Living in two worlds"* — she names this as Kristy and Tanya's biggest frame, and notes it applies to Aboriginal men specifically in a country where politicians "paint everyone with the same brush." Potential campaign-theme candidate if Brave Ones expands beyond the photographic.
- *"To break the cycle, it's up to those ones they say are too far gone. It's very important you get on board with them."* — rebuts the "some kids can't be saved" framing in one sentence.

### The canonical long-form — "Oonchiumpa: What Happens When Community Leads"

Ben's own piece (now in EL v2 as a Bloomfield-family-authored story with Kristy and Tanya as first-person sources) contains a line from Tanya that belongs on the opener of every Brave Ones artefact and every Minderoo envelope page:

> *"Our young people are just collateral in a bigger issue. The issue doesn't sit with them. It sits on a much broader level and with adults, not with children."*

This is the single cleanest way to state what the Brave Ones campaign is doing — refusing to treat the young people as the problem. It also sets up the specific accountability that Brave Ones makes visible: adults, at scale, have been handling this wrong.

The long-form piece also adds Operation Luna as a concrete worked example: 20 of 21 young people on NT Police's high-risk list were off it by December after Oonchiumpa worked the list alongside police. That is an outcome number funders can check, in a format that is still a story rather than a graph.

## One addition to the campaign-use map

Brave Ones now gains a **"Come Back Next Week" sub-series** — portraits taken on Country during the monthly Atnarpa trips. The caption frame:

> *[Young person's first name and age]. [Mparntwe camp]. On a three-day trip to Country in [month], this was them. They asked to come back next week.*

That caption format refuses every victim-narrative convention. It names the young person, places them with their home (camp, not a detention facility), dates the photograph to a specific trip (not a stylised series), and lets the "asked to come back" line carry the entire politics of the image without the campaign having to say it.
