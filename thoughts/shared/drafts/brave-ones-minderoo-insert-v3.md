# The Brave Ones

*A journal, a journey, and an exchange for young people who have already been counted out.*

*A method that works in more than one place.*

---

[PHOTO: Judges on Country, Atnarpa, 17 April 2026. Real photograph. No stock imagery in this envelope.]

---

## Three communities, one method

In Mparntwe (Alice Springs), Kristy Bloomfield and Tanya Turner run Oonchiumpa. Kristy is a Traditional Owner of Mparntwe. Tanya came home from a Supreme Court of Victoria associateship to build it. They run a 95% diversion rate at 97.6% less cost than detention.

In Mount Isa, Brodie Germaine — a proud Pita Pita Wayaka man — runs BG Fit. He started it after watching too many of his mob go through the justice system without support. *"I played rugby league at a high level and I know what sport can do for young people. It's not just about fitness — it's about discipline, respect, turning up when you said you would. These are the things that keep our mob strong."*

In Mount Druitt, Daniel Daylight leads Mounty Yarns — a youth-led movement built by the young people who needed it most, for the ones coming up behind them. No police-run PCYC. No adults making choices for kids. Young people at the table, in the room, running it themselves. Taleigha, a Biripi woman from Glebe, names it:

> *"Because young people are going through all of this stuff, why are the older fellas making choices for the younger fellas when they're the ones going through it?"*

Three Countries. Three entry points — culture-first at Oonchiumpa, sport-first at BG Fit, youth-led-advocacy-first at Mounty Yarns. The same method underneath: show up, keep showing up, be the adults who stay — or get out of the way when the young people can lead themselves.

>

In Mount Isa, Benji, a young person from Mount Isa, answers the question *"would you trust Brodie to help you out?"* in his own words:

> *"He right. That big fell. He know half of the black people. That's why."*

Seventeen words. A young person naming why the trust is there — not because of the program, not because Brodie is a professional, but because Brodie holds a web of kin connection that reaches into Benji's family. Benji goes on: *"Been fishing with them, been hunting, been going, doing all the fun stuff with all our family, come back that way."*

Rashad Gavin Isaacson, a young Kalkadoon dancer with the Sundowners crew, says NAIDOC and culture are *"everything"* to him — one word, from a young person who has found the thing that keeps him rooted.

On BG Fit's first on-Country camp, 12 young fellas from Mount Isa went bush for three days. Uncle gave a Welcome, taught the boys about the old tracks and the songlines that run through the area. *"You could see it clicking for some of them — this connection to something bigger than themselves."* One boy said afterward:

> *"That was the best three days of my life."*

He's now a regular at Tuesday gym. The method doesn't stop at the camp. The camp opens the method.

BG Fit's camp work travels further too. The Doomadgee trip — 500km from Mount Isa into one of Queensland's most remote Indigenous communities — set up at the oval and within an hour had 30 kids there. *"Word spread fast. They were hungry for it — hungry for someone to show up and do something with them."* A local woman said: *"Please come back. These kids need this."* Quarterly trips planned. Funded on a Sport and Rec grant that makes the distance affordable.

In Mount Druitt, the young people say what they found in Mounty Yarns before they found anywhere else. Isabella is 14, from Seven Hills:

> *"I like the fact that the mentors and the youth workers, they're easy to talk to. There's no pressure. It's more fun and you don't feel judged."*

Polly is 13, from Blacktown:

> *"I like this area a lot because I can express myself, I can be myself around these people. I don't have to hide my personality."*

And the young men describe what the system did to them before Mounty Yarns existed. One, shot by police at 14: *"Within seconds I've been shot in the arm, shot in the chest. And then the last word I said was like, boy, they were shot with blood in my mouth. I died twice. One at the scene and on the operating table."* Another: *"I've been through 15 caseworkers in two years. How does that help a kid?"*

Shayle McKellar, a proud Wangkamarra man who completed his HSC in custody and now works as a lived-experience consultant, names what the system does: *"They enter the system as victims, and then they come out as even more victimised people."*

One unnamed young man from Mount Druitt says what Mounty Yarns gave them for the first time:

> *"It feels good. I don't know how to explain it 'cause it's the first time that this has ever happened, like, with all of us. Get shit off your chest though."*

In Mount Druitt the cost of holding a child behind a door is $1,700 a day. The young people of Mounty Yarns know the number by heart — they've been asked about it a hundred times. What they have never been asked is what that money could buy instead.

At Spinifex Residential, where young people live after being removed from home, a BG Fit worker describes what happens:

> *"When we first started going in there, the staff were sceptical. After about the fourth session, the kids started waiting for us at the gate. They'd have their shoes on, ready to go. One boy who wouldn't even look at us in week one was leading the warm-up by week six. We treat them like young athletes, not like problems."*

And on the Tuesday gym sessions at Mount Isa PCYC:

> *"It's not magic. It's just showing up. Being consistent. Giving them something to look forward to. A lot of these kids don't have that."*

Xavier, in Mparntwe, was told he had "limited ability to understand." He built a Stretch Bed from recycled plastic that the community now uses for ceremonies and bush trips. Fred Campbell, his case worker: *"He trusts us. We earned that trust."*

Last month Kristy took eight young people to Atnarpa. On the last night, a 15-year-old on the police radar since he was 12 asked her: *"Aunty, can we come back next week?"* Three of those eight are back at school. Two are in jobs. All eight are off the police list.

Tanya names what this is:

> *"Our young people are just collateral in a bigger issue. The issue doesn't sit with them. It sits on a much broader level and with adults, not with children."*

## What The Brave Ones is

**A journal.** A5, hardcover. Theirs to keep. iPad locked to Procreate and Voice Memos beside it. The goal state is a young person walking in and saying, *"Can I grab my journal?"*

**An exchange.** Journal practice unlocks travel. Alice Springs to Mount Isa. Mount Isa to Sydney. Sydney to Palm Island. By Year 2, Diagrama in Spain (13.6% recidivism over 30 years), YOPE in Amsterdam. Four kids, two workers, three days. Many of these young people have never been on an aeroplane.

**A book.** Journal entries, transcripts, paintings, audio, compiled into physical books. One per community. One national collection. Made by them. Goes to magistrates, ministers, funders. A physical object that refuses to be a statistic.

**Alumni.** Graduates become peer mentors. Over three years, a distributed national network of young advocates speaking from experience.

## The pilot — already distributed across three sites

The Brave Ones is not a proposal to pilot a method. It is a proposal to connect three methods that are already running — in Mparntwe, Mount Isa, and Mount Druitt — and give the young people inside them the journal, the exchange, and the book that turn their voices into a national artefact.

- **Oonchiumpa, Mparntwe.** June 2026 Brave Ones launch. Brisbane domestic exchange 8–13 June. First data to Empathy Ledger by August.
- **BG Fit, Mount Isa.** Parallel Brave Ones launch. Tuesday gym at PCYC runs weekly with 15–20 young people. Monthly bush camps at Atnarpa-equivalent sites. Quarterly Doomadgee trips 500km remote. Gracie Ryder at Mount Isa Police Liaison is the cross-service bridge.
- **Mounty Yarns, Mount Druitt.** The youth-led site — Daniel Daylight as CEO, but Adam and Leah co-running Youth Peak, Shayle McKellar the lived-experience consultant. Brave Ones' book and exchange components plug directly into a movement the young people built themselves. No programming-from-above required; they already have their own.

Three Countries. Three different cultural authorities. Three different entry points. Three sites where the method is visible to anyone who shows up on the day. The Three Circles ten-community ambit is not a Year-3 promise — it's a Year-1 demonstration that the method already travels, with seven more anchor communities to come.

## The cost

One iPad costs $1,500. One night in youth detention costs $3,644. For the price of one week in detention, you equip a community with journals for a year.

The Brave Ones sits inside the $2.9M Three Circles envelope, not on top of it. It is the daily practice that makes Circle 2 tangible, the source material that makes Circle 3 inevitable, and the proof that the program is real and portable.

## What we are asking Minderoo for

To underwrite the Year 1 distributed pilot at Oonchiumpa + BG Fit, the Year 2 expansion to three additional anchor communities, and the Year 3 national collection that returns the young people's own words to the bench and the budget papers that sent them away.
