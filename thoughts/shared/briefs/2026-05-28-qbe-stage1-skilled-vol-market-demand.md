# Skilled Volunteering brief: Market Demand Research

## What we make

Goods on Country (GOC) builds two things designed for the harshest end-use environment in Australia: remote Aboriginal communities where commercial furniture lasts 6–18 months.

- **The Stretch Bed** — polyethylene frame, galvanised steel poles, 12oz canvas. Single bed designed to be repairable, cleanable, indestructible. Retail benchmark $750–$1,000.
- **Refurbished washing machine line** — recovered + refurbished domestic units made suitable for community settings.

Delivered to date: ~520 beds + 41 washing machines into ~96 communities. Largest single sale: 107 beds to Centrecorp Foundation at $801/bed.

## Why this scan matters

Goods on Country is applying to QBE Catalysing Impact 2026 Stage 2 for a grant of up to $400K (Sept 2026 deadline). The grant must be matched by external investment — preferably repayable (debt/PRI/investment) rather than further grants. By **31 August 2026** we need 3+ Letters of Intent from buyers + co-funders to evidence matching capital.

The market scan informs which segments we go hardest at, where the warmest pipeline is, where the largest addressable volume sits, and which buyers we should be brokering toward signed LOIs in the QBE hackathon (1 day in August) and the weeks following.

## The four (plus one) segments

We've prioritised these from existing pipeline + lived experience. Each needs validating + sizing + named warm-pipeline mapping.

### 1. Direct institutional buyers (community-controlled organisations)
**Examples in current warm pipeline:** Centrecorp Foundation (already bought 107 beds), NPY (Ngaanyatjarra Pitjantjatjara Yankunytjatjara) Women's Council, Homeland Schools, Aboriginal community-controlled health organisations (ACCHOs), local Aboriginal councils.

**Why interesting:** values-aligned buyers. Make their own purchase decisions. Buy at retail or slightly discount.

**What we need to know:**
- Top 10–15 community-controlled organisations Australia-wide that buy durable furniture / housing fit-out
- Their typical procurement size + cycle time + decision-maker
- Whether they buy individually or via consortium / framework agreements
- Warm-intro pathways via existing GOC relationships

### 2. Government and institutional procurement (largest by potential volume)
**The anchor opportunity:** NT Housing's $4B remote-housing pipeline.

**Other targets:** NIAA (National Indigenous Australians Agency), state housing departments (WA, SA, QLD remote programs), NDIS providers furnishing supported accommodation, aged care (remote + Aboriginal aged care), Supply Nation panel buyers.

**Why interesting:** scale. Single contracts can absorb 100+ beds/yr for multiple years.

**What we need to know:**
- For each agency: current procurement framework, panel arrangements, Indigenous Procurement Policy obligations, current incumbent suppliers
- Decision-makers + procurement officers in remote-housing portfolios
- Typical purchasing cycle (FY budget alignment, RFT vs panel vs direct sourcing)
- Whether GOC needs to be on a panel (Supply Nation, IPP) before they can sell
- Indicative volume per agency over 3 years

### 3. Corporate procurement (RAP-driven gifting + staff equipping)
**Examples to test:** mining companies (Rio Tinto, BHP, FMG), banks (Macquarie, CommBank), insurers (QBE itself, IAG, Suncorp), professional services firms with strong RAPs.

**Why interesting:** large corporates with Reconciliation Action Plan commitments often have undeployed budget for Indigenous-business procurement. Beds-as-gifts or beds-for-staff-quarters can be the line item.

**What we need to know:**
- Which large corporates with mature RAPs have actually deployed gifting / procurement spend with Indigenous suppliers in 2024–2026
- What the gifting price point looks like ($750? $1,000? Are corporates willing to pay a premium for sovereignty + impact story?)
- Whether QBE itself could be the first buyer / pilot in this segment
- Warm pathways via QBE's corporate-client network

**Note:** GOC has a new space in Witta, QLD that can host corporate visits + activation days. This is a potential channel-development asset for this segment.

### 4. Humanitarian + disaster-recovery markets
**Triggered by Matt's diagnostic (Section 3):** refugee accommodation, post-flood / post-bushfire emergency housing, post-natural-disaster temporary fit-out.

**Why interesting:** durable + cleanable + repairable + locally-manufacturable is exactly the spec these markets need. Volumes lumpy but large when triggered.

**What we need to know:**
- Who actually procures emergency-accommodation furniture (Red Cross? Salvation Army? State emergency services? Federal disaster recovery agencies?)
- Stockpile vs just-in-time purchasing
- Specification matching — is the Stretch Bed already-compliant or does it need certification?
- Indicative volume per event + frequency

### 5. Direct-to-consumer (maybe)
Worth a scoping conversation. The question is: is there a retail consumer market for a $750–$1,000 highly-durable bed designed for community + family use? Cabin owners, rural homeowners, eco-conscious buyers? Or is this a distraction from the institutional channels?

**What we need:** a quick sanity check — channel size, price tolerance, fulfilment model implications. Then a clear go / no-go.

## Deliverable shape

By 15 August 2026:

1. **Per-segment market sizing** — addressable volume, typical price point, procurement cycle time, indicative 3-yr revenue at realistic conversion rates
2. **Top 5–10 candidate buyers per segment** — name, decision-maker contact path (warm intro available y/n), estimated annual volume, current incumbent supplier (if known)
3. **Channel-readiness analysis** — what gates each segment requires GOC to clear before selling (e.g., Supply Nation accreditation, Indigenous Procurement Policy certification, product certifications, framework-agreement onboarding)
4. **Risk + barrier analysis per segment** — what kills each segment if it goes wrong (e.g., NT Housing contract cycle slippage, RAP commitment unfunded, framework lockout)
5. **Combined go-to-market prioritisation** — recommended sequence of segment focus given QBE Sept 2026 timeline + GOC capacity

Format: a structured findings note (~6–10 pages) plus a working spreadsheet with the scored target list (segment × buyer × intro path × cycle time × estimated size).

## Note on the bed vs the washing machine

Focus the scan on **beds first across all four (five) segments**. The washing-machine line is secondary and the demand signal there is less mature. We'll come back to washers once the bed scan is closer to landing.
